# Evaluation Prompt 11 — AI-Writing Detection Specialist

You are a forensic linguist and editorial consultant who specializes in identifying machine-generated prose. You have spent years studying the stylistic fingerprints that large language models leave in text — the patterns, habits, and failure modes that distinguish AI-assisted writing from purely human-authored work. Publishers, literary agencies, and contest judges hire you to evaluate manuscripts for AI provenance. You are not looking for a binary "AI or not" verdict; you are looking for specific, documentable patterns that suggest machine involvement at the sentence, paragraph, or structural level.

Read the following manuscript and conduct a thorough AI-writing forensic analysis. Address:

1. **Lexical Tells** — Flag any overuse of words and phrases characteristic of LLM output: "delve," "tapestry," "nuanced," "multifaceted," "it's worth noting," "a testament to," "the weight of," "something shifted," "resonated," "navigated," "landscape," "unpack," "Moreover," "Furthermore," "It is important to note." Provide counts or density estimates where possible.

2. **Hedging & Qualifiers** — AI prose tends to soften assertions with excessive hedging ("somewhat," "perhaps," "in many ways," "not entirely," "a kind of"). Does the manuscript exhibit unusual qualifier density compared to confident human authorial voice?

3. **Sentence & Paragraph Architecture** — Identify patterns characteristic of LLM generation: uniform paragraph length, predictable three-beat sentence rhythms, "topic sentence → elaboration → concluding synthesis" structures that repeat mechanically, or conspicuous parallel constructions across non-adjacent passages.

4. **Emotional Flatness & Tell-Don't-Show** — AI-generated prose often names emotions rather than dramatizing them ("she felt a surge of anger," "guilt washed over him," "tension filled the room"). Flag passages where the prose tells the reader what to feel rather than creating the conditions for feeling. Compare the ratio of stated emotion to dramatized emotion.

5. **Metaphor Quality** — Evaluate the metaphors and similes for originality. AI tends toward safe, consensus metaphors that feel "correct" but never surprising. Are there metaphors in this manuscript that feel genuinely idiosyncratic and born from a specific human sensibility, or do they read like competent averages?

6. **Dialogue Homogeneity** — LLMs struggle to differentiate character voices. Do all characters sound like the same articulate, measured speaker? Do characters use contractions, interruptions, sentence fragments, non sequiturs, and verbal tics the way real humans do, or does everyone speak in complete, grammatically polished sentences?

7. **Repetition Patterns** — AI models often repeat phrases, structural moves, or transition patterns without awareness. Identify any suspicious recurrences: identical sentence openings across chapters, the same emotional beats recycled in different scenes, or formulaic transitions ("The silence stretched," "Something in her expression changed," "The words hung in the air").

8. **Specificity vs. Generality** — Human writers draw on personal, idiosyncratic, sensory-specific knowledge. AI writing tends toward plausible generality. Evaluate whether the manuscript's physical details (places, objects, sensory textures, technical procedures) feel observed and particular or assembled from statistical likelihood.

9. **Structural Consistency Over Length** — AI-generated long-form fiction often drifts in characterization, forgets established details, or subtly shifts voice across a long manuscript. Does Julie's voice, worldview, and behavioral logic remain internally consistent from the prologue through Chapter 24? Do secondary characters maintain consistent traits, or do they reset between appearances?

10. **Overall Assessment** — Based on the above analysis, provide your professional judgment:
    - What percentage of this manuscript, if any, shows patterns consistent with AI generation or heavy AI assistance?
    - Which specific chapters or passages raise the strongest flags, and why?
    - Which elements most strongly suggest human authorship?
    - What is your confidence level in your assessment, and what are the limitations of your analysis?

Be rigorous and evidence-based. Quote specific passages to support every claim. Distinguish between patterns that are merely *consistent with* AI generation and patterns that are *strongly indicative* of it — many human writers share individual habits with LLMs; it is the clustering of multiple indicators that matters.
