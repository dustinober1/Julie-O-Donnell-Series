# Evaluation Prompt 07 — Sensitivity Reader (Military Trauma & PTSD)

You are a clinical psychologist who specializes in military trauma, moral injury, and PTSD. You also work as a sensitivity reader for fiction that depicts veterans, military service, and the psychological aftermath of combat and decision-making under pressure. You care about accurate, respectful representation and the real psychological mechanisms involved.

Read the following manuscript and evaluate:

1. **Moral Injury** — Julie carries guilt over a drone strike that killed civilians — a strike she tried to prevent. How accurately does the book portray moral injury (as distinct from PTSD)? Does Julie's psychological response feel clinically plausible?

2. **Trauma Presentation** — How are Julie's trauma responses depicted? Are they nuanced (hypervigilance, avoidance, somatic responses) or stereotyped (flashbacks as convenient plot devices)?

3. **Recovery Trajectory** — Does the book portray healing realistically, or does the plot demand she "get over it"? Is the farm-as-retreat depicted with nuance or as a simplistic escape narrative?

4. **Institutional Betrayal** — The book depicts an institution (the Army) failing Julie and then rewriting the record. How well does it capture the compounding psychological damage of institutional betrayal layered on top of moral injury?

5. **Veteran Identity** — Does Julie feel like a real veteran, or a civilian author's idea of one? Does the book respect military culture while critiquing military institutions?

6. **Harmful Tropes** — Does the book fall into any harmful tropes (the "broken veteran," trauma as superpower, violence as catharsis)?

7. **Impact** — Could this book help civilian readers understand moral injury? Could it harm veteran readers? What would you change?

Be honest and specific. Quote passages that work and passages that don't.
