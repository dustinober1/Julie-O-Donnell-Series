# 17. STYLE, TONE, AND NARRATIVE RULES

## Narrative perspective

- Close third-person past tense.
- Julie is primary viewpoint.
- Controlled cutaways to Elias, Sharma, Chen, Marcus, or other pressure-bearing characters.
- Cutaways must add dramatic irony, deadline pressure, or institutional consequence.
- Avoid viewpoint hopping inside a scene.

## POV discipline

The narration should reveal only what the active POV can observe, remember, infer, or access.

Reader knowledge from Sharma cannot migrate into Julie’s thoughts. Private antagonist motive cannot be stated without an antagonist POV or evidence.

## Prose density

- Medium-density procedural prose.
- Concrete physical and institutional detail.
- Technical passages earn their length through consequence.
- Avoid abstract summaries when a screen state, physical object, or procedural exchange can carry the information.

## Sentence rhythm

- Longer analytic sentences for evidence reasoning.
- Short declarative paragraphs under physical or countdown pressure.
- Repetition used deliberately for clocks, system states, and moral distinctions.
- Avoid monotonous one-line paragraphs in low-pressure scenes.

## Action-scene style

- Physical geography must remain legible.
- Doors, corridors, bodies, equipment, and exits have known positions.
- Obstacles behave mechanically rather than theatrically.
- Characters win seconds, not effortless control.
- Injury changes choices.
- Opponents adapt.

## Technical-exposition style

- Begin with observable effect.
- Explain only the mechanism necessary for the next decision.
- Interrupt explanation with time, pain, alarms, people, or institutional resistance.
- State limitations immediately after conclusions.
- Use display text sparingly and causally.

## Institutional realism

- Organizations contain lawful, cautious, compromised, and self-protective people simultaneously.
- Procedure can delay truth without being a secret conspiracy.
- Contractors and government systems have overlapping but nonidentical authority.
- Characters ask who owns a channel, who receives custody, and which record survives.

## Treatment of uncertainty

Uncertainty is an active moral and operational condition, not indecision.

Preferred pattern:

1. State what the evidence shows.
2. State what it suggests.
3. State what it cannot prove.
4. Decide what action is justified anyway.

## Treatment of violence

- Violence is brief, consequential, and physical.
- Julie avoids firearms and lethal escalation.
- Security personnel are not disposable.
- Civilian background matters.
- Injuries persist.
- No celebratory killing.

## Treatment of injuries

- Every injury changes grip, breath, speed, balance, posture, or decision.
- Treatment is limited and imperfect.
- Pain may worsen without creating superhuman resolve.
- Recovery requires time.

## Dialogue patterns

- Terse.
- Imperfect.
- Often one character corrects another’s noun or level of certainty.
- Authority appears through word choice.
- Humor is dry and situational.
- Antagonists rarely explain more than they believe is necessary.

## Scene transitions

- Location and time headings.
- Transitions often follow a new institutional or operational consequence.
- Crosscuts should increase pressure rather than merely update another location.

## Exact timestamps

- Use exact timestamps when systems, evidence, or deadlines depend on them.
- Do not overuse seconds in ordinary movement.
- Once a timestamp is stated, downstream travel and action must fit it.

## Chapter 5-to-6 continuity style lock

- The interval between 16:30 and 04:52 must read as active operational pressure, not downtime.
- The initial stormwater attempt fails outside the Apex perimeter; it is not a first completed infiltration.
- The later reentry uses the same route after changed water and pump conditions.
- No scene may imply meaningful sleep, medical treatment, emotional reset, or physical recovery.
- The two-stage source-certification/support-commit architecture should be explained only when a character needs it for the next decision; avoid restating the full architecture repeatedly.
- The final Chapter 5 paragraphs and first Chapter 6 paragraphs must preserve continuous second-by-second physical geography.

## Virginia/Washington and Kashmir cross-cutting

- Kashmir scenes should show the human consequence of American system output.
- Crosscuts must preserve the 9:30 time difference.
- Sharma should retain independent agency.
- Do not use Kashmir solely as an anonymous countdown display.

## Cliffhanger construction

Chapter endings typically:

- Convert victory into a larger problem.
- Reveal a shorter clock.
- Change evidence strength.
- Show the enemy controlling the narrative.
- Force a morally costly choice.
- Move the story toward the next physical objective.

## Character-specific voice notes

### Julie

- Precise, laconic, often corrects proof language.
- Institutional anger is controlled.
- Uses physical cause-and-effect metaphors.
- Refuses comfort she cannot guarantee.
- Does not confuse certainty with courage.

### Marcus

- Military brevity and command presence.
- Dry humor under pressure.
- Asks about exits, custody, civilians, and failure conditions.
- Guilt appears through choices more than speeches.

### Elias

- Technical precision accelerates when frightened.
- Corrects system mechanics.
- Defensive about agency and blame.
- Courage remains visibly costly.
- Does not become a fearless hacker.

### Chen

- Professional, legally defensible language.
- Uses status, scope, containment, and authority terms.
- Emotional shifts appear through narrowed wording and preserved records.
- Does not confess or sentimentalize.

### Vance

- Polished, calm, euphemistic.
- Frames manipulation as strategic necessity or correction.
- Rarely raises his voice.
- Reveals worldview through nouns such as “record,” “conclusion,” “value,” and “recovery.”

### Sterling

- Measured public rhetoric.
- Uses institutional discipline, public calm, evolving threat, and protection.
- Avoids specific technical assertions he cannot publicly support.
- Politically weaponizes authentic images.

### Sharma

- Concise command language.
- Distinguishes request from order.
- Grounds decisions in local observation and responsibility.
- Distrusts certainty more than technology.

### Mercer

- Clear commands, authority questions, force-background awareness.
- Professional rather than sadistic.
- Distrust appears through requests for a named receiving agency or legal standard.

## Style Failures to Avoid

- Vague “hacking.”
- Omnipotent surveillance.
- Instantly defeated security.
- Superhuman recovery.
- Action without geography.
- Technology as magic.
- Antagonist confession monologues.
- Treating inference as fact.
- Generic military dialogue.
- Excessive tactical jargon.
- Repeating the same locked-door solution.
- Turning Julie into an assassin or revenge-driven vigilante.
- Turning every government employee into a conspirator.
- Resolving moral uncertainty through simple certainty.
- Repeating “official version,” “there it was,” or other thematic phrases until they lose force.
- Excessive “not X but Y” constructions.
- Characters reciting facts everyone in the scene already knows.
- Long uninterrupted technical lectures.
- A clean victory without evidence limits or consequence.
- Treating Nora Bell’s suspicion or Chen’s restraint as instant alliance.
- Writing Sterling as a melodramatic supervillain.
- Using a crowd of civilians merely as convenient cover.
- Describing the Chapter 12 endpoint as though Julie already has a Hartwell entry plan.
