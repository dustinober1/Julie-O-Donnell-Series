# Chapter 3 - The Exit Protocol

The deadbolt began to retract.

Julie watched the transfer bar.

Eighty-five percent.

The motor inside the steel door turned slowly, drawing the locking cylinder back through the frame. Beyond it, someone issued a quiet command. Shoes repositioned in the corridor.

Marcus stood to the side of the door, his body angled away from the opening.

“Do we have enough?” he asked.

“No.”

“Can we leave with what we have?”

“That’s a different question.”

The remote sanitization process climbed to ninety-three percent. Files vanished from the temporary review environment as quickly as Julie’s hidden process could copy them. She opened the evidence-capture manifest: the system had transferred the critical packet samples first, followed by the validation index, the unexplained ninth checksum, and the software-dependency records. The full relay-health history remained incomplete, as did the original access log showing who had modified Payload 88 after it was archived.

Useful evidence, but not decisive—the kind institutions buried beneath requests for more context.

The deadbolt completed its withdrawal, and a second lock released above it. Julie canceled the remaining bulk transfer and entered the command to seal the partial capture.

A warning filled the screen:
INCOMPLETE ARCHIVE
FINALIZATION MAY EXCLUDE ACTIVE FILE BLOCKS
PROCEED?

Julie confirmed. The terminal began writing a cryptographic manifest to the evidence drive. Until that process finished, removing the device could corrupt the directory and make every copied file vulnerable to challenge.

The door moved inward half an inch, and a security officer’s voice came through the gap. “Colonel Reed, step away from the door.”

Marcus did not move.

The manifest advanced: one of four blocks.

“Colonel, acknowledge.”

Marcus looked at Julie. She held up one finger.

The door pressed against Marcus’s shoulder. He allowed it to open another inch, then planted his foot against the base. “I acknowledge that you are attempting to enter a classified review room while government evidence is being preserved,” he said. “Identify yourself and state your authority.”

“Daniel Mercer, Apex Protective Services. We have an administrative order to secure the room.”
“Signed by whom?”
“Director Vance.”
“Director Vance is a contractor.”
“Colonel, move away from the door.”

The manifest advanced: two of four blocks. Julie could hear at least three people behind Mercer in the corridor. No weapons were being drawn, which was almost worse. A gun would have clarified the situation; procedure allowed everyone to pretend nothing unlawful was happening.

Sarah Chen spoke from outside the door. “Marcus, stop making this harder than it needs to be.”

Marcus’s expression hardened at the use of his first name. “You suspended my authority after I submitted a defect notice.”
“Your authority was suspended because you exceeded the approved review scope,” Sarah said.
“You locked two people inside a sealed room and shut off the ventilation.”
“The environmental interruption lasted less than two minutes,” Sarah replied.
“Long enough for you to destroy the review partition.”
Sarah did not answer.

Three of four blocks. The door pushed harder against Marcus, who looked back at Julie. She shook her head—not yet.

“Colonel,” Mercer said, “if you continue obstructing the door, we will document your conduct as resistance to a lawful security directive.”
“Then document this too,” Marcus said. “The evidence device in this room is government property under my custody. Any seizure requires a federal chain-of-custody receipt and identification of the receiving authority.”

Sarah’s voice remained calm. “No one is seizing anything. We are escorting you to a compliance interview while the classification status of the copied material is reviewed.”
“By Apex.”
“By the facility security authority.”
“Name the government official overseeing the review.”
Silence answered him.

The final manifest block began writing. Onscreen, the remote sanitization reached COMPLETE, and every directory in the visible partition disappeared. Only the finalization window remained.

The door opened another inch. Julie reached for the cable.
“Wait,” Marcus said.
“Working on it.”

The terminal displayed: VERIFYING CAPTURE SIGNATURE.
A gloved hand appeared through the gap, gripping the edge of the door. Marcus drove his shoulder against it. The hand vanished, and Mercer swore.

“Last warning, Colonel.”

Julie watched the gray verification wheel rotate. Once. Twice.
The terminal chimed:
PARTIAL CAPTURE SEALED
INTEGRITY MANIFEST COMPLETE

She pulled the cable, and the screen went black. “Done.”

Marcus stepped away, and the door opened immediately. Two Apex security officers entered in dark uniforms and lightweight body armor, sidearms holstered. One carried a compact electroshock weapon low beside his thigh. Sarah Chen stood behind them, tablet held against her chest, her hair still perfectly arranged. A third officer waited in the corridor.

Mercer was in his forties, broad-shouldered, with the contained irritation of a man accustomed to being obeyed. “Hands where I can see them,” he said.

Marcus slowly lifted his hands. Julie left hers on the table.

Mercer’s eyes moved to the aluminum evidence case. “Step away from the device.”
“That device is in my custody,” Marcus said.
“Your access authority has been suspended.”
“My commission has not.”
“This facility is operating under an active data-loss response,” Mercer said.
“Then call the Defense Criminal Investigative Service and have them take custody.”

Sarah Chen stepped forward. “That won’t be necessary, because no crime has been established. We quarantined a compromised analytic environment after an unauthorized export attempt.”
“The export used an approved government device,” Marcus said.
“The approval was issued in error.”
“Before or after Julie found Payload Eighty-Eight?”

For the first time, Sarah’s control slipped. Her eyes shifted toward Julie for a fraction of a second. It was enough.

“You knew the package number,” Julie said.
Sarah recovered quickly. “I have access to the query record.”
“You denied access to the archive before I searched the index.”
“The validation archive is proprietary.”
“That wasn’t my question,” Julie said.

Mercer stepped between them. “This discussion is over. You will both accompany us to the compliance suite.”
“Are we under arrest?” Julie asked.
“No,” Mercer said. “But you are required to remain on-site during the security assessment.”
“That is detention,” Julie said.
“It is an administrative hold.”
“Can we refuse?”
Mercer’s jaw tightened. “You can cooperate, or we can refer the matter to federal law enforcement as a suspected classified-data theft.”

Marcus lowered his hands, and Mercer’s grip moved toward his electroshock weapon.
Sarah pointed to the aluminum evidence case. “Leave that here. It contains proprietary material.”
“It contains potential evidence of intelligence manipulation,” Marcus said.
“Your authority to make that determination has been suspended.”
“By the subject of the determination,” Marcus countered.

Sarah’s gaze hardened. “Marcus, you are standing inside a secure contractor facility after assisting an unauthorized consultant in penetrating protected development records. You can continue escalating this, or you can come upstairs, make a statement, and allow the appropriate offices to resolve it.”

Julie looked at the camera in the corner. The indicator light was dark, though it had been green when they entered. “Why did you turn off the room recording?”
Sarah didn't look back. “The sector quarantine interrupted local systems. The recording server went offline.”
“Convenient,” Julie said.

Mercer reached for the aluminum case, but Marcus took hold of its handle first. The two men locked eyes across the metal box.
“Colonel,” Mercer said quietly, “do not make me remove that from you.”
“Put your name on a custody receipt,” Marcus said, his voice dropping.
“That is not the procedure.”
“Then you are not touching it.”

Mercer looked at Sarah. If her officers seized the drive without documentation, Marcus could later describe the act as destruction of government evidence. If she allowed him to retain it, Julie might carry proof out of the facility.
She chose a third option. “Escort them to Conference Room Four,” she said. “Colonel Reed may retain the case until counsel arrives.”
Marcus demanded Army counsel rather than the Apex lawyers Sarah offered, but she only repeated that he could request whoever he liked once they were in the room.

Marcus gave Julie a brief look: a room controlled by Apex, telephones controlled by Apex, and counsel selected after the company had time to define the record. The official version was already being written.
Julie rose from the chair. “Lead the way.”

The corridor outside Room 402B was lit only by emergency fixtures, the ventilation pushing cold air through the acoustic panels with a low hiss. Mercer walked ahead, while Sarah followed behind Marcus and Julie, flanked by the other two officers.

Julie counted doors—four offices, a secure records room, a service corridor, and the elevator lobby at the far end. No employees were visible.
“You evacuated the floor,” Julie said. “You didn't want witnesses.”
“We reduced unnecessary personnel movement during the incident,” Sarah replied. “We didn’t want additional exposure to classified material.”

Mercer stopped at the elevator lobby and swiped his badge, but the call panel remained dark. One of the officers checked his radio and was told that elevators were still isolated in Sector Four, directing them to hold the "subjects" at the second-floor internal muster area instead.
Marcus caught the word. “We’re not going to a conference room. Conference Room Four is on six.”
Sarah didn't look back as she descended the concrete stairs of Stairwell C. “The location changed. The internal muster area has secure communications.”

Bare concrete and red emergency lights replaced the carpeted corridor. Julie checked the evacuation map beside the door as they descended: they were on the fourth floor; the visitor skybridge connected to the parking structure from the third; the internal muster area was on the second, behind another controlled security boundary.

At the third-floor landing, Julie paused beside a manual fire-alarm station. Mercer ordered her to keep moving, but she stayed long enough to read the life-safety panel. The independent system was active, and a second light displayed:
SECTOR 4 AIR-HANDLING FAULT
ACKNOWLEDGMENT PENDING

“You locked an occupied room while the ventilation system was offline,” Julie said.
“It has been restored,” Sarah said, continuing down the stairs. “Facilities will clear the fault.”
“The system still shows it active.”
“Julie,” Marcus said, understanding a split second before Sarah.

Julie pulled the handle.

A siren erupted, filling the concrete shaft with a rising electronic howl and white strobe flashes. A recorded warning ordered immediate evacuation via the stairs.

Mercer seized Julie’s wrist, his grip holding as she twisted. “What the hell are you doing?”
“Reporting a life-safety failure.”
“This is a false alarm.”
“The panel says otherwise,” Julie said.

Sarah Chen reached for her lanyard’s reset key, but Marcus stepped between her and the alarm. “Don’t. You kill the alarm before county dispatch clears it, and I add interference with an emergency system to the report.”
“You have no report,” Sarah said. “Your credentials are suspended.”
“The fire department won’t care about my credentials,” Marcus said. “They’ll care why your secure floor lost ventilation while occupied.”

Doors opened on the landings above and below. Employees flooded the stairwell, dissolving the orderly containment into a crowded evacuation. Mercer released Julie’s wrist and ordered his officers to keep them moving toward the ground-floor assembly point.
Sarah leaned close to Marcus. “You think an alarm makes you safe?”
“No,” he said. “I think witnesses make you careful.”

At the third-floor landing, Julie reached for the crash bar beneath a sign reading PARKING SKYBRIDGE / EMERGENCY EGRESS.
Mercer caught her shoulder, redirecting her. “Ground floor. Ground floor assembly.” The electroshock weapon was in his hand, held low.
“My vehicle is on three,” Julie said.
“The parking bridge is not an authorized assembly route,” Sarah added, coming down behind them.

Julie pressed the crash bar. The door moved half an inch before Mercer pulled her back. Marcus set the aluminum case on the concrete. Mercer’s eyes flicked to it—and Marcus drove his forearm between Mercer’s wrist and the weapon, redirecting the muzzle. The electroshock cartridge discharged with a flat crack, sending two barbed probes into the emergency map, where they snapped against the plastic cover.

The stairwell erupted in shouting. Marcus twisted Mercer’s arm to drop the weapon, shoved him back against the handrail, and grabbed the evidence case. “Go.”

Julie hit the crash bar, and they ran into the third-floor corridor. Behind them, Sarah’s voice echoed, ordering security to "contain the bridge"—language selected for the recording. Julie sprinted toward the glass doors at the end of the corridor, her reflection multiplying across the panels under the flashing emergency strobes. The biometric turnstiles had retracted into emergency-egress position, allowing employees to stream through. Julie and Marcus joined the flow, crossing the skybridge into the warm Virginia air. Below them, a county fire engine was already turning into the campus entrance as the outer security bollards retracted. The alarm had reached someone Apex did not employ.

Julie cut through the evacuees toward the visitor parking section, where her Ford sat. Behind them, Sarah stood at the end of the bridge, talking rapidly into her tablet, while two security officers pushed through the crowd.
“Keys?” Marcus asked.
Julie pulled the mechanical key from her pocket. “They kept the electronic fob.”
“Can you start it?”
“It’s a truck, not a space program.”

She unlocked the door manually, and Marcus climbed into the passenger side with the evidence case on his lap. Julie inserted the key. The engine turned once and failed. The officers were twenty yards away. She tried again, and the V8 caught with a rough growl.
“Any chance this thing has a quiet setting?” Marcus asked.
“No.”

Julie backed out of the space. One officer slapped the rear panel and jumped aside; the second ran in front of the hood, hand resting on his holstered sidearm. “Turn off the engine!” he shouted.
Marcus lowered his window. “Move away from the vehicle.”
“Colonel, you are not authorized to leave.”
“Then call federal law enforcement,” Marcus said. “They can meet us somewhere your company doesn’t own.”
The officer drew his electroshock weapon. Julie shifted the truck into drive, and the officer stepped aside.

She accelerated down the ramp, tires squealing against the concrete. In the rearview, Sarah was climbing into a black security SUV. Julie bypassed the traffic using the outer ramp. At the exit, the outbound lane was blocked by raised steel bollards, but the inbound lane stood open for an arriving fire engine. Julie swerved across the painted divider, ignoring the guard waving his arms, and slotted the truck directly behind the fire engine. Trapped in the wake of red lights and diesel exhaust, the checkpoint guards couldn't raise the bollards without stopping the emergency response.

Julie followed the engine through the checkpoint. At the first intersection, the engine turned right, and Julie went left. Behind them, the black Apex vehicle emerged from the gates. Julie merged into the lunchtime traffic on the Fairfax County Parkway, slotted between a delivery van and a utility truck, but the tail remained two car lengths back.

“Open the case,” Julie said.
Marcus popped the latches. The evidence drive rested inside in its foam, its indicator dark. Beside the cradle, the monochrome display read:
CAPTURE SEALED
PARTIAL ARCHIVE
INTEGRITY VERIFIED

“What does partial mean?” Marcus asked.
“It means we have enough to make them nervous and not enough to make us safe,” Julie said. The drive held the archive index, checksums, and the matching relay interval, but only part of the deployment record. It did not contain the carrier profile or proof of who had modified the package.

“Then we go to Hackett,” Marcus said, looking at the tail three cars back.
“Call him first.”

Marcus pulled out his secure Army handset and connected the battery module. The screen loaded slowly, but instead of the normal prompt, it displayed:
TERM AUTHORITY REVOKED
CONTACT SECURITY OPERATIONS

“They killed my credentials,” Marcus said.
“Someone pushed the suspension beyond Apex,” Julie said. “Vance doesn’t need to revoke your commission. He just needs a government official to flag your token while they investigate.”
Marcus tried the emergency-call function and then an unclassified number, but the handset rejected his biometrics and the call dropped. The phone was dead.

“I can explain this face-to-face,” Marcus said.
“To get to Hackett, you have to pass the Pentagon gate,” Julie said. “You’re an active-duty colonel who just fled a secure contractor facility with classified data after setting off an alarm and discharging an officer's weapon. The official version will be cleaner than the truth. The moment your token scans at the gate, you’ll be detained and the case seized.”
“We can’t sit on it.”
“We’re not,” Julie said. “We inspect what survived and make a copy somewhere forgettable, where our computers won't leave a trail.”

The Apex SUV remained two car lengths back. As the light ahead turned yellow, Julie waited until the last second, then braked hard behind a delivery van. The Apex vehicle swerved into the adjacent lane to avoid hitting them, and Julie cut right into a shopping center entrance.

She navigated the parking lot, drove behind a grocery store, and slipped into a service lane between loading docks to reach a local road. No black vehicle followed.

After two miles of silence, Marcus laid the dead phone on the console. He asked about her phone and keys; both remained at Apex, except for the mechanical ignition key. They knew the truck, and they knew her Culpeper address.

Julie thought of the listing porch, the damaged fence, and the horses. They didn't need a national surveillance network to find her; they only needed a map.
“We don't go to Culpeper,” she said.
“I wasn't suggesting it.”
“We need evidence first,” Julie said. “We have files copied from a monitored partition. If they let us copy them, they could be poisoned. Trusting the first thing a system hands us is how this started.”

Marcus looked out at the trees and nodded. “Where do we inspect it?”
Julie saw a sign for Manassas. “Somewhere forgettable.”

Forward Post Arjun

Kashmir

22:18 Indian Standard Time

Major Ananya Sharma read the American assessment twice: first as an officer, then as someone who had spent eleven winters watching machines misunderstand mountains. Outside the reinforced observation post, wind drove ice against the armored windows. The valley below had disappeared behind cloud and darkness.

Lieutenant Sameer Qureshi stood by the console. “Argus confidence is ninety-nine point eight percent.”
“I can see the number,” Sharma said. “Where is the corroboration?”

Qureshi opened the telemetry: satellite collection was degraded by weather, forward acoustic arrays reported only intermittent heavy-engine signatures in Sector Zebra-Nine, and two communication relays had caught encrypted command traffic. There was no ground observation, drones were grounded for icing, and local signals intelligence was inconclusive.

Sharma studied the tactical map. Red symbols marked a predicted Pakistani artillery formation moving into firing positions beyond the Line of Control. The movement was too orderly to be real, but if it was accurate, it threatened three forward posts. If it was false, firing first could ignite the sector.

“When does the United States certify the assessment?” she asked.
“Zero-two-hundred local,” Qureshi said.
“And the support window?”
“Immediate execution is recommended when the counter-battery support product releases at fourteen-thirty. Northern Command has requested we prepare firing solutions now.”
“Requested or ordered?”
“Requested, ma’am.”

Sharma looked back at the assessment, which described the threat as certain. She had learned to distrust that word long before algorithms began using it.
“Bring the batteries to readiness,” she said. “But no ammunition at the guns yet, and no targeting data loaded. We prepare, and we wait for corroboration. If the armor is real, four hours will give us more than one source.”
“And if the American certification arrives unchanged? Northern Command may expect execution.”
“Northern Command is not standing in this bunker,” Sharma said.

Qureshi allowed himself a faint smile, then the console chimed. A new line appeared beneath the Argus assessment:
SOURCE REVIEW IN PROGRESS
FINAL CONFIDENCE CERTIFICATION PENDING

The warning remained for less than a second, then vanished. The display returned to:
CONFIDENCE: 99.8%
SOURCE INTEGRITY: VERIFIED

Sharma leaned toward the screen. “Did you see that? What changed?”
Qureshi opened the history. “No revision is listed.”
"Then someone revised it without leaving a revision."

She stared at the red symbols on the screen. Thousands of miles away, a machine had declared the enemy’s intentions with mathematical certainty, while in the actual mountains, the storm revealed nothing.
“Save a local image of that screen,” Sharma ordered.
“The packet is access-controlled.”
“Use a camera.”

Qureshi reached for his phone. Sharma watched the countdown beside the assessment:
03:41:22 UNTIL ALLIED SOURCE CERTIFICATION.

“Major?” Qureshi asked.
She kept her eyes on the clock. “At fourteen-thirty, if the support release arrives and the threat remains confirmed, we decide whether to fire.”
“And if it isn’t?”
“Then we find out why a machine was so certain about something no human being could see.”
