# Chapter 13 — The Carrier Stream

Downtown Washington
07:18 Eastern Daylight Time

The last vehicle in Sterling’s motorcade did not follow the armored sedan. It cleared the intersection behind two black sport-utility vehicles, held the broad federal avenue for half a block, then moved right without the police motorcycles. No flags. No visible seal. One rear running light burned weaker than the other. Julie watched it through the windshield while cross traffic remained stopped.

“Support vehicle,” Marcus said.

“Or staff,” Elias replied from the cargo floor.

“Or nothing connected to the signer,” Julie said.

The weakened rear light disappeared behind a city bus. Ahead, the rest of the motorcade continued beneath controlled signals toward Hartwell. The police motorcycles opened the intersection one lane at a time. A delivery truck moved first. Then a taxi. PCF-27 waited beneath the red light with its known Virginia plate, white Potomac Critical Facilities markings, and three people the city had been told were armed. The service radio transmitted the Fenwick update again.

“District units, suspect contractor van confirmed leaving the L Street response area eastbound. Electronic media believed inside. Coordinate with Hartwell traffic restrictions and do not approach without support.”

The light changed. Julie put the van in motion. Her left hand held the wheel high. The turn pulled against her braced right wrist where her forearm pressed the lower spoke. Pain moved from the base of her thumb into the elbow and stayed there.

Marcus unfolded the road map across his left thigh. Blood had dried to his jaw, but a fresh line had reached the edge of his collar from the reopened scalp wound. The aluminum case stood between his boots. He kept one ankle against it whenever the van changed lanes.

Elias held the incident module in both hands. The administrator-token board remained inside the padded sleeve beneath his torn shirt. Without the borrowed rain shell, he looked less like a technician than a man who had slept in his clothes and been dragged through machinery.

“What exactly are we following?” Marcus asked.

“Not Sterling,” Julie said. “The physical path.”

“To Hartwell.”

“To the local challenge.”

Elias looked at the module. “The same signer serial has to answer from inside the briefing security domain between seven-forty-six and seven-forty-eight-thirty.”

“We know.”

“We know the device has to be inside. We do not know when it enters.”

Marcus looked through the windshield toward the avenue where the motorcade had vanished. “It may already be there.”

“Yes.”

“Then this is over before it starts.”

“No.” Julie changed lanes behind a municipal utility truck. “If the device went in with the principal motorcade, we identify that route and preserve the limit. If it enters with staff, support, counsel, or a courier, we identify that route. We do not pick the answer before the evidence.”

The map shook once in Marcus’s hand. He flattened it against his thigh.

“And the van?”

“We get it out of the immediate plate stream.”

“How?”

“We find a place with more than one exit and a view.”

“That is not a place.”

“It is the requirement.”

The service radio carried a Hartwell traffic advisory. Two approaches were closing to ordinary vehicles. One commercial street remained open west of the federal block, with access restricted at the next avenue. Marcus traced the street with one finger.

“There is a public garage west of the annex district. Seven levels if they have not rebuilt it. Office parking, early contractors, two vehicle exits.”

“View?”

“Upper levels face east.”

“Distance?”

“Two blocks from the outer Hartwell approaches. Maybe three from the actual perimeter. I have never entered the annex.”

“Good.”

Marcus looked at her.

“You keep using that word when the information is bad.”

“I use it when the information admits what it is.”

A marked Metropolitan Police cruiser appeared ahead. It sat beside a line of temporary cones where officers were separating government vehicles from delivery traffic. A white electrical-service van had been pulled to the curb. One officer stood behind it reading the plate while another spoke into a shoulder radio. PCF-27 was four vehicles away. Marcus folded the map.

“Garage entrance is right before the cones.”

Julie saw the blue parking sign beneath an office tower. The ramp descended half a level before climbing inside the concrete structure. A camera housing faced every entering plate.

“No clean entry,” Elias said.

“There was never a clean entry.”

The electrical van’s driver stepped onto the sidewalk with both hands visible. The officer at the rear looked toward the moving traffic line. Julie turned into the garage. A ticket machine stood on the driver’s side beneath white fluorescent light. She lowered the window. Her right hand could not reach across her body without turning the wrist inside the brace. Marcus reached for the button.

“No.”

“You want to protect the integrity of the parking ticket?”

“I want your hand on the case.”

Julie leaned out and pressed the button with her left thumb. The machine clicked. A camera flashed above the plate. The paper ticket extended too far from her injured side. She caught it between two fingers and nearly dropped it before pulling it inside. The barrier rose.

“The camera has us,” Elias said.

“It has the van entering at a known time,” Julie replied. “That record may matter later.”

Marcus looked at the concrete ramp ahead. “You are choosing cameras now?”

“I am choosing which facts they record.”

The digital clock changed to 07:22. Julie drove upward. Each level opened around rows of commuter cars and concrete columns striped with old paint. The lower floors were nearly full. On the fourth, two maintenance crews unloaded ladders from unmarked pickups. On the fifth, the eastern wall opened into broad rectangular bays. Beyond them, the federal district spread beneath pale morning light.

Hartwell did not announce itself with a sign visible from the garage. Traffic control did. Police motorcycles held the broad northern approach. Black vehicles passed through a temporary outer vehicle line and disappeared between federal buildings. To the east, a narrower lane ran behind a row of offices toward a controlled staff-and-service approach.

The principal motorcade entered from the north. The support vehicle with the weak rear light was not with it. Julie continued to the sixth level. The upper deck was less crowded. A delivery van occupied one corner. Three sedans stood beneath monthly-parking signs. The west exit ramp descended behind a concrete wall. The east spiral was visible through the center opening.

Two routes out. For now. Julie parked PCF-27 nose-first toward the west ramp, close enough to the open east wall for Marcus to see the approaches without leaving the passenger seat. A concrete column hid most of the van from the garage’s internal aisle camera. It did not hide the plate image already stored at the entrance.

She shut off the engine. The sudden quiet made their breathing audible. Marcus’s came shallow and controlled. Elias’s carried a faint catch when he shifted his injured hip. Julie’s pulse moved inside the wrist brace. She looked at the dashboard clock. 07:24:16.

“Hard abort is seven-forty-two-thirty,” she said.

Marcus turned toward her.

“Conditions?”

“We have continuous observation or a defensibly recoverable record of the suspected signer route. The module and board remain intact and with Elias. You retain the originals and a viable way out.”

“And if one fails?”

“We leave.”

“If the challenge is one second from beginning?”

“We leave.”

“If the device appears while police are taking the ramp?”

“We leave.”

Marcus held her gaze.

“You stayed eight seconds at Fenwick.”

“I know.”

“You told me the rule mattered most when success was close.”

“I know.”

“And then you let Elias change it.”

Elias looked up from the module. “I changed it.”

“You both did.”

Julie did not defend the seal. It had strengthened the evidence. It had also cost the primary exit and placed Bell, building security, Mercer, and a police officer inside a more dangerous extraction.

“Seven-forty-two-thirty,” she said. “If the conditions do not pass, the operation ends.”

Marcus waited.

“Say the part you do not want to say.”

Julie looked through the open wall toward Hartwell.

“If the route is one step from proving Sterling held the device, and the conditions fail, we still leave.”

“The device,” Elias said. “Not Sterling.”

Julie turned to him. “Correct.” Marcus nodded once. The rule existed outside her now. She opened the driver’s door with her left hand. The wind across the upper deck carried traffic noise and the intermittent chirp of reversing trucks. Julie crossed twenty feet to the open wall. Elias followed with the incident module held against his abdomen. His left leg shortened every third step. He used the concrete columns instead of Julie’s arm.

Marcus remained in the van with the aluminum case, the recovery cartridge, the paper custody log, and the dead transponder. The role cost him more than movement would have. Julie could see it in the way he watched them go. They stopped behind a waist-high barrier at the east edge. From there, the outer Hartwell traffic divided into three visible streams. The north approach carried the principal motorcade, marked security vehicles, and police control.

A western curb held media vans and staff cars waiting beneath temporary restrictions. The east lane carried deliveries, technical support, and office vehicles toward a covered checkpoint that sat outside the buildings hiding Hartwell’s secure interior. None of the routes identified SSO-NS-004. Elias leaned one shoulder against the concrete.

“The local challenge can happen only after the device crosses into the briefing domain. Nothing here tells us where that boundary starts.”

“Then we do not name it.”

Julie studied the cameras instead. A traffic camera stood above the intersection feeding the east lane. A private loading-dock camera watched the same lane from the opposite building. The garage camera behind them covered the van, Marcus, and the opening where Julie and Elias stood. Three independent systems. Different owners. Different clocks. Not proof of what they saw. A record that could later test it.

“Can the module make event markers without touching either partition?” Julie asked.

Elias looked at her.

“The handling journal can record a local button event and device time.”

“Does it change Partition A?”

“No.”

“Partition B?”

“No. Both remain sealed. The journal belongs to the enclosure, not the evidence volumes.”

“That someone pressed the control at a recorded time while the module was in this hardware state.”

“Does it prove what we observed?”

“No.”

“Good.”

He gave her a tired look. “You are becoming predictable.”

“Only in narrow areas.”

Elias used his left thumb to open the module’s handling screen. He kept his bandaged right index curled against the case.

PARTITION A: SEALED / READ-ONLY / INTEGRITY VERIFIED
PARTITION B: SEALED / WSS-4 CONTROLLER SEAL PRESENT

“Record the observation position. Sixth level, east opening. Garage entry camera, internal aisle camera, municipal traffic camera above east intersection, private loading camera opposite the support lane.”

His pen moved. The hand shook. The words remained legible.

“What is the mission description?” he asked.

“Observe the route. Do not identify a person.”

He wrote that too. For the next ninety seconds, they watched ordinary vehicles. A food-service truck entered the east lane and stopped at a delivery barrier. The driver presented paperwork. A uniformed guard directed him toward a loading position. A gray government sedan arrived from the north and continued to the western staff curb.

Two black SUVs entered the principal approach and disappeared behind the outer motorcade line. No weak rear light. No portable signer. No fact worth turning into one. At 07:27:13, a police motorcycle left the north route and crossed toward the east lane. Julie raised one finger. Elias pressed the event control.

“Same immediate decision.”

They kept watching.

Apex Building Three
Reston, Virginia
07:28 Eastern Daylight Time

“Direct Mercer to recover the media before Hartwell admits the signer.”

Sarah looked at the words. He had not asked whether Julie was in the garage. He had not asked whether the signer was there. He had placed the material and the Hartwell event inside one sentence. She marked the time in her incident file. Daniel Mercer answered over vehicle noise.

“We are six minutes out. District units are closer.”

The request was denied pending federal review. The denial entered her incident file anyway. On the garage image, PCF-27 climbed out of camera view. Julie had not escaped observation. She had moved to where every institution would have to explain what it saw.
