# Chapter 20 — The Custody Exception

October 15
09:06 Eastern Daylight Time

Secure MPD Evidence Intake
Washington, D.C.

The first useful answer arrived as a refusal to surrender the signer.

SSO-NS-004
STATUS: LOCATED WITHIN LEGISLATIVE SECURITY CONTROL
FEDERAL TRANSFER: NOT AUTHORIZED
COMPROMISE-CONTROL INTAKE: PENDING

Located and pending belonged to different clocks.

Located meant someone had found the compact office device after Hartwell returned it to the credentialed support path. Pending meant no named legislative-security custodian had yet accepted physical responsibility, no exterior condition had been recorded, and no one had promised the device would remain unused while the institutions argued over who could touch it.

Julie watched from the observation alcove. Her right hand remained open inside the brace because it could not close. Park had raised the padded support after the fingertips began to darken. Through the glass, the common chest stayed locked beneath fixed cameras. Grant occupied the Hartwell channel. Alvarez appeared from DCIS. Hackett waited in a second window. On a muted television, Sterling continued describing the October 13 events as an attack on American warning systems.

A second legislative-security message entered.

DUTY AUTHORITY: DEPUTY DIRECTOR MARISOL VEGA
PROPOSED CUSTODIAN: MARTIN VANN / COMPROMISE CONTROL
CONDITION: EXTERIOR INVENTORY / NO POWER / NO NETWORK / NO USE

The second response supplied what the first had lacked: people whose authority and physical responsibility could later be reviewed.

Hackett said, “Bring the device to MPD.”

Alvarez shook her head. “Legislative Secure Services will accept a no-use hold. It will not transfer Senate property into executive custody without separate authority.”

“The signer is physical evidence in a defense-intelligence incident.”

“It is also an office asset under legislative security. Immediate seizure gives us one object and a new jurisdictional fight. The assignment record may remain behind the same wall that put it in circulation.”

A duty attorney beside Alvarez said, “Exterior verification leaves us without internal state.”

Grant answered from Hartwell. “Internal state does not identify the person who authorized its movement. WSS-four and Hartwell already established that the same serial answered both challenges.”

“It may contain local logs.”

“It may contain nothing we are authorized to read,” Alvarez said. “Opening it now creates a new state-change argument before we have the custody exception that explains why it moved.”

Julie had been about to say the same thing.

She stopped herself.

This was exactly what she was supposed to learn: correct method did not need her voice attached to every decision.

Hackett looked toward her window anyway. “You agree?”

“The device had rules,” Julie said. “Find the exception.”

Grant asked, “What should exist?”

“The Hartwell record says a credentialed office-support path delivered and received the case. WSS had a sponsor. The same serial crossed both secure domains. Someone had a carrier assignment, a start time, an end time, and an authority that permitted the device to leave ordinary office custody.”

“That is the assignment framework,” Grant said. “Legislative security may own the framework even if the office owns the reason.”

“Ask for them separately.”

Office counsel entered through the LSS channel and objected to any production that exposed legislative deliberation or protected source material.

Alvarez did not ask what anyone discussed.

She requested the device serial, carrier, custody interval, stated purpose category, authorizing office authority, original-record custodian, and any withheld fields. LSS would separately document present physical custody and the no-use hold. No route content. No message content. No office deliberation.

The office could preserve privilege without making the device ownerless.

Apex counsel supported federal seizure. “The signer may contain evidence tied to an ongoing threat to classified systems. Delay risks loss.”

Vega answered from the legislative-security channel. “The no-use hold prevents operation, connection, challenge, rekey, zeroization, transfer, and office retrieval. State loss is not improved by moving the device through an unnecessary boundary.”

Her voice held the exhaustion of someone who had spent a day explaining that emergency did not dissolve jurisdiction.

Alvarez muted herself. Julie could see her speaking to the duty attorney and another person outside the camera frame. No one in the MPD room moved toward the chest. Ortiz stood beside it with the key still clipped inside his vest.

Alvarez returned.

“DCIS accepts exterior-only verification and Legislative Secure Services physical custody under a named no-use hold. Original office and LSS records remain with their lawful custodians. Any production is derivative and source-limited. Any later attempt to image, challenge, or operate the device requires a separate authority and new recorded seal event.”

Hackett began to object.

“My decision,” Alvarez said. “Your office remains supporting authority.”

The correction cost her something visible. She had taken the senior official’s urgency and placed her own name against the narrower course.

Grant disconnected from Hartwell and moved to Legislative Secure Services with no MPD evidence and no access to the signer.

Before her image vanished, she said, “The carrier is not the authorizer.”

Julie almost told her to put it in the first line.

Grant looked through the camera as if she had heard the unspoken instruction. “I know.”

Julie let her carry it.

Legislative Secure Services Compromise-Control Intake
Washington, D.C.

The closed black signer case arrived inside a gray tamper-evident pouch.

Grant stood behind a yellow floor line in a room designed for objects no institution wanted carried farther than necessary. A fixed camera covered the stainless-steel transfer surface. A second camera showed the wall clock and every hand within the marked area. No cable, reader, power source, or wireless device entered.

Deputy Director Marisol Vega watched from behind glass. Martin Vann, the named compromise-control officer, stood at the receiving position with both hands visible.

The transferor remained on the opposite side of the table.

Samuel Drennan’s credential identified him as a secure-communications courier for Senator Sterling’s office. He was in his thirties, clean-shaven, and dressed in the same dark suit he had worn through the previous day’s emergency briefing. A strip of adhesive residue marked the cuff where Hartwell had removed a temporary access band.

Nothing about him suggested the person behind a covert operation. He looked tired, angry, and aware that every movement could be assigned meaning by someone who knew less than he did.

Grant read the transfer authority before anyone touched the pouch.

“State what you are surrendering.”

Drennan glanced toward office counsel beyond the camera line.

Counsel said, “He may identify the container and his custody without waiving privilege.”

Grant waited for Drennan.

“One closed legislative secure-communications pouch containing one compact signer case assigned to my custody under an emergency continuity authorization.”

“Pouch serial?”

He read it.

“Exterior device serial?”

“SSO-NS-004.”

“Did you open the pouch after sealing?”

“No.”

“Did you activate, connect, challenge, image, decrypt, rekey, zeroize, or operate the signer after Hartwell returned it?”

“No.”

Grant looked at Vann. “You are receiving that as his statement, not adopting it as verified internal state.”

“Correct.”

A clear window in the pouch exposed the compact case’s asset plate and one corner of the numbered closure band. The serial matched Hartwell’s departure record. The closure band matched the field recorded when the device left the challenge room.

Grant did not need the signer opened to establish that the same closed case had entered LSS control.

She asked Drennan, “Did you continuously observe the case before the office assigned it to you?”

“No.”

“Then your condition statement begins when?”

“When I accepted the pouch at 06:54 on October thirteenth.”

Office counsel objected to any implication that the earlier state was unknown.

“It is unknown to this witness,” Grant said. “Another source may establish it.”

Drennan confirmed no observed change during his custody interval. Grant put the limitation into the transfer record before he signed. His hand shook once at the end of his name. She did not decide whether the movement meant fear, anger, exhaustion, or guilt.

Vann inspected the pouch without opening it. He recorded exterior seams, visible closure band, pouch and asset serials, transfer time, transferor, receiving custodian, cameras, and clock source.

At 09:42, he accepted physical custody.

“Device and pouch remain closed,” he said for the record. “Storage under two-person access in compromise-control locker C-seven. No release to the office, DCIS, MPD, Apex, Hartwell, Northbridge, or any other party without Deputy Director Vega’s written order, named receiving authority, and recorded transfer.”

Vega activated the partition microphone. “Add that no existing order authorizes use.”

Vann added it.

The physical target stopped moving.

Grant felt no relief. Stillness changed the question from where the device would go to why it had gone anywhere.

“Assignment record,” she said.

Office counsel placed a single-page certification in a separate document tray. The original remained inside the office secure-communications ledger. Vann moved the derivative beneath the overhead camera.

It named Drennan as assigned carrier for SSO-NS-004. The interval began at 06:54 on October 13 and ended at the present LSS intake. The stated purpose was emergency national-security continuity support. The authorizing office authority was Diane Kessler, deputy director for national security in Sterling’s office.

No destination list appeared.

No operational purpose appeared.

No initiating instruction appeared.

The withheld-fields statement identified route details, internal requestor, substantive purpose, and office deliberations.

Grant asked, “Who created the exception?”

“Kessler authorized it,” counsel said.

“That identifies the authorizer. Did she initiate it or act on an instruction?”

“Outside today’s production.”

The interval began fourteen minutes before the WSS-4 hardware response. It remained open through the Hartwell challenge and for hours afterward, until the preservation demand forced the signer into compromise control.

Drennan had carried the device.

Kessler had authorized the continuity exception.

The record did not show that Drennan selected the route, knew the message content, understood K-17, or possessed any authority beyond physical custody. It did not show what Kessler knew or who instructed her.

Vega asked Grant, “Would you still recommend against federal seizure?”

Grant looked at the closed pouch.

“If DCIS takes the signer, we gain the object and start a new custody fight. The office can leave the authorization behind and let the carrier become the only visible person attached to it. Under this hold, the device is frozen while the office assignment, LSS intake, Hartwell challenge, and WSS audit survive under four custodians.”

“The carrier is not the authorizer,” Vega said.

“And the authorizer is not yet the instruction source.”

Drennan looked at Grant for the first time without anger.

Vann placed the pouch inside a rigid secondary container, closed it, and applied the LSS hold seal. Two-person custody followed it through the rear control door. Grant never saw the signer itself. She saw the institution that had accepted responsibility for not using it.

Drennan remained at the transfer table after the device left.

“Am I in custody?” he asked.

“No,” Vega said. “You are directed to remain available under the office and LSS preservation notices. You may leave with counsel after your credential and route records are preserved.”

Drennan looked at Grant. “My name will be the one on every camera carrying the case.”

“Yes.”

“I did not choose the route.”

“The assignment record says Kessler authorized your custody. It does not yet identify the instruction source or your knowledge.”

“My job was to keep the case closed and get it through the security paths.”

“Then preserve that statement with your lawyer and the records that can test it.”

He glanced toward the door through which the signer had disappeared. “If I had refused, someone else would have carried it.”

Grant did not tell him whether refusal would have changed anything. “That is not a physical observation. Decide with counsel whether it belongs in your statement.”

Drennan nodded. The carrier would not be protected by turning him into a hero. He would be protected, if the records allowed it, by keeping movement, authorization, knowledge, and command separate.

Vega authorized a certified derivative from the transfer and office-assignment records. It identified the device, exterior condition, Drennan’s custody interval, Vann and Vega’s no-use hold, Kessler’s authorization, original-record custodians, withheld fields, and proof ceiling.

It made no claim about internal signer state, Drennan’s knowledge, Kessler’s instruction source, or Sterling’s personal command.

Grant signed only after those limits appeared on the first page.

Secure MPD Evidence Intake
Washington, D.C.

Grant returned to the incident channel before noon.

“The signer is under LSS no-use hold. Named custodian Martin Vann. Deputy authority Marisol Vega. Exterior serial confirmed as SSO-NS-004. Pouch and case remain closed. No technical examination.”

Alvarez accepted the derivative into incident 187463 without taking the office or LSS originals.

Hackett read the authorizer field. “Diane Kessler. Sterling’s deputy national-security director.”

“Office authority,” Julie said. “The record shows she authorized the exception. It does not say who instructed her.”

“She works for Sterling.”

“So do people who schedule meetings, carry briefing books, and secure rooms. Employment is not command evidence.”

Hackett looked frustrated by the distinction because the distinction was real.

Grant opened the next production request herself. The office would identify the initiating continuity record, its creation time, apparent requestor, inherited reference, and custody state without producing deliberative content. LSS would preserve the signer. Hartwell and WSS would preserve their original events. No one would open the device to substitute its silence for the chain that moved it.

The physical signer was no longer the most urgent object.
