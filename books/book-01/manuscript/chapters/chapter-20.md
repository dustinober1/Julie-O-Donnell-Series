# Chapter 20 — The Custody Exception

10:44:12 EDT / 20:14:12 IST

Secure MPD Evidence Intake
Washington, D.C.

At 10:44:12 Eastern Daylight Time, the first answer to the recovery request arrived as a refusal to transfer the signer.

Julie O’Donnell watched the Legislative Secure Services response populate beneath the question Alvarez had entered less than a minute earlier.

SSO-NS-004
DEVICE STATUS: LOCATED WITHIN LEGISLATIVE SECURITY CONTROL
FEDERAL TRANSFER: NOT AUTHORIZED
COMPROMISE-CONTROL INTAKE: PENDING

The words located and pending belonged to different clocks.

Located meant someone had found the office device after Hartwell returned it to the credentialed support path. Pending meant no named legislative-security custodian had yet accepted it, no exterior condition had been recorded, and no one had promised it would remain unused while the institutions argued over who could touch it.

On the muted television, Senator Sterling continued describing the morning’s events as an attack on American warning systems. The captions advanced beneath his image in clean white lines. He had moved from the arrests to the need for emergency protection of classified infrastructure without once naming the device registered to his office.

Julie remained in the camera-covered alcove. Her left wrist was still fixed to the front restraint. Her right forearm rested inside the padded support, swollen enough that the brace felt smaller than it had twenty minutes earlier. The hand would not close. She kept it still against her body and watched the response instead of the pain.

Officer Gabriel Ortiz stood beside the locked common incident chest. Officer Hannah Park remained at the witness position. Seven package references glowed on the custody display between them. Seven green seal states. No movement entries.

Special Agent Leila Grant was still on the secure Hartwell channel. Supervisory Special Agent Miriam Alvarez occupied the center window from the DCIS duty suite. General William Hackett appeared beside her, his image delayed by less than a second and his impatience by none.

“Located where?” Hackett asked.

Alvarez read the lower field. “Legislative Secure Services will not disclose a current transfer room over this circuit. Their duty authority says the office reported the signer into compromise control after our preservation demand reached the Senate security channel.”

“Then have them bring it here.”

Julie looked at him.

Hackett caught the expression. “The device is physical evidence in a defense-intelligence incident.”

“It is also legislative property under a separate security authority,” Alvarez said. “They are offering exterior verification and a no-use hold. They are not offering custody.”

“A no-use hold leaves it inside the institution under examination.”

“It leaves it under Legislative Secure Services,” Grant said from Hartwell. “Not the senator’s private office.”

Hackett turned toward her window. “And if the office retrieves it?”

“Then the hold failed,” Grant said. “That is why someone has to put a name on it.”

A second message entered the record.

LSS DUTY SUPERVISOR: DEPUTY DIRECTOR MARISOL VEGA
PROPOSED RECEIVING CUSTODIAN: LSS COMPROMISE-CONTROL OFFICER MARTIN VANN
PROPOSED CONTROL: EXTERIOR-ONLY INVENTORY / NO USE / NO POWER / NO NETWORK / NO REKEY

The second response supplied what the first had lacked: named people whose authority and physical responsibility could be reviewed later.

Alvarez said, “Deputy Director Vega will accept physical responsibility if we agree that DCIS is not asserting ownership, possession, or authority to operate the signer.”

“Agree,” Julie said.

The duty attorney beside Alvarez turned toward her. “That may preserve the device and leave us without access.”

“Access to do what?”

“To identify its state.”

“Hartwell already identified the serial and the accepted challenge. WSS identified the same device inside the earlier session. Opening it does not tell you who authorized either use.”

“It might contain records.”

“It might contain nothing we are entitled to read. And touching it gives the office a new argument about altered state.”

Hackett said, “We can image it under warrant authority later.”

“Later is different.” Julie looked at the four lines defining the proposed hold. “Right now the question is who put it into circulation.”

The word circulation was wrong as soon as she said it. It made the signer sound like a loose object passing from hand to hand without rules.

“It had rules,” she said. “Find the exception.”

Alvarez’s attention sharpened. “What exception?”

“The Hartwell record says a credentialed office-support path delivered and received the case. WSS has a sponsor record. The same device answered both challenges. Someone had authority to carry it across two secure domains. That authority should have a start time, an end time, a person assigned, and a person who approved the departure from normal custody.”

Grant said, “Legislative security may control the assignment framework even if the office controls the original authorization.”

“Then ask for the framework and the office exception separately.”

The duty attorney said, “The office is already asserting Speech or Debate and internal deliberative privilege.”

“Then do not ask what anyone discussed. Ask who changed custody, under what standing rule, for which serial, and for how long.”

Hackett leaned closer to his camera. “We have the device. We should secure it before counsel builds a wall around the paperwork.”

Julie watched Sterling’s captioned statement advance behind him.

“Seizing the device gives them one clean fight,” she said. “Property, privilege, custody, classification. The carrier becomes the person we caught holding it, and the authorization record becomes something they can call separate.”

“What do you propose?” Alvarez asked.

“Put the signer where no one can use it. Keep the original assignment record where the law says it belongs. Take certified derivatives from the office, LSS, WSS, and Hartwell. Then compare the same serial, carrier credential, and assignment interval without pretending one institution owns the others.”

The duty attorney looked unconvinced.

Grant did not. “That is the stronger chain.”

“It is also the slower one,” Hackett said.

“The public version is already ahead,” Julie replied. “Speed does not help if the first action makes the responsibility record weaker.”

Alvarez muted herself for nineteen seconds.

Julie could see her speaking to the attorney and another person outside the camera frame. No one in the MPD intake room moved toward the chest. Ortiz kept one hand near the custody key without touching it. Park’s body-camera light remained red.

At 10:47:03, Alvarez returned.

“DCIS accepts exterior-only verification and Legislative Secure Services physical custody under a named no-use hold,” she said. “No activation, challenge, connection, opening, imaging, decryption, rekey, zeroization, or operation. Original office and LSS records remain with their lawful custodians. Any production is derivative and source-limited.”

Hackett started to speak.

Alvarez continued. “My decision. Your office remains supporting authority, General.”

He stopped.

The distinction cost her something visible. She had taken the safer senior official’s urgency and placed her own name against a narrower course.

Grant said, “Send me the supplemental scope.”

Alvarez signed it.

The secure system recorded the time, source, and limits. Grant’s Hartwell window closed. Her next destination appeared only as LSS CONTROL INTAKE, with no street address, office suite, or route entered into the common incident display.

Before she disconnected, Julie said, “Do not let them call the person carrying it the person who authorized it.”

Grant looked back through the camera.

“I know the difference.”

“Put it in the first line.”

Grant’s expression remained professionally flat. “I said I know.”

Then her image vanished.

Legislative Secure Services Compromise-Control Intake
Washington, D.C.

The closed black case arrived at 10:54:26 inside a gray tamper-evident security pouch.

Special Agent Leila Grant stood behind a yellow floor line in a room designed to receive objects no one wanted carried farther than necessary. A fixed camera covered the stainless-steel transfer surface. A second camera faced the wall clock and the hands of every person within the marked area. No wireless device entered. No reader, cable, power supply, or diagnostic instrument sat on the table.

Deputy Director Marisol Vega watched from the control side of a glass partition. She wore a Legislative Secure Services credential and the expression of someone who had already spent the morning explaining that jurisdiction did not become simpler because the emergency was real.

Martin Vann, the named compromise-control officer, stood at the receiving position with both hands visible.

The transferor remained on the opposite side of the table.

His credential identified him as Samuel Drennan, secure-communications courier, Office of Senator Sterling. He was in his thirties, clean-shaven, wearing a dark office suit with a narrow strip of adhesive residue at one cuff where a temporary access band had been removed. Nothing about him suggested private power. He looked tired, angry, and acutely aware that every movement was being recorded.

Grant read the transfer instruction before anyone touched the pouch.

“State what you are surrendering.”

Drennan looked toward office counsel standing beyond the camera line.

Counsel said, “He can identify the container without waiving any privilege.”

Grant waited.

Drennan turned back to the table. “One closed legislative secure-communications pouch, pouch serial LSS-P-44091, containing one closed compact signer case assigned to my custody under office emergency continuity authorization.”

“Device serial?”

“Exterior case label reads SSO-NS-004.”

“Did you open the pouch after sealing?”

“No.”

“Did you activate, challenge, connect, mount, image, decrypt, rekey, zeroize, or operate the signer after Hartwell returned it?”

“No.”

Grant looked at Vann. “You are not adopting that statement as fact.”

“No,” he said. “I am receiving it as the transferor’s statement.”

“Good.”

The pouch had a clear evidence window no larger than a playing card. Through it, Grant could see the compact case’s exterior asset plate and one corner of a numbered closure band. She could not see the signer itself. She did not need to.

The asset plate read SSO-NS-004.

The closure band number matched the Hartwell departure field in HWA-CD-187463-01.

Grant read both numbers aloud. Vann repeated them. Drennan confirmed only that the pouch and case appeared in the same exterior condition in which he had delivered them.

Office counsel objected to the word same.

“Then state your limit,” Grant said.

“I did not continuously observe the case before it was assigned to Mr. Drennan.”

Grant changed the transfer statement. “Transferor confirms no change observed during his own custody interval. No claim before that interval.”

Drennan signed.

His hand shook once at the end of his name. Grant did not decide what the movement meant.

Vann inspected the pouch without opening it. He recorded the exterior seams, closure band visible through the window, pouch serial, case asset plate, transfer time, transferor, receiving custodian, room cameras, and wall-clock source.

At 10:57:18, he accepted physical custody.

“Legislative Secure Services no-use hold begins now,” he said. “Custodian Martin Vann. Deputy authority Marisol Vega. Device and pouch remain closed. Storage will be in compromise-control locker C-seven under two-person access. No release to the office, DCIS, MPD, Apex, Hartwell, Northbridge, or any other party without Deputy Director Vega’s written order and a recorded receiving authority.”

Vega spoke through the partition microphone. “Add that no order authorizes use.”

Vann added it.

The physical target had stopped moving.

Grant felt no relief. The stillness changed the problem rather than ending it.

“Now the assignment record,” she said.

Office counsel slid a single-page certification into a separate document tray. “The office will produce a limited custody certificate. The original remains in the office secure-communications ledger.”

Grant did not touch the page. Vann moved it beneath the overhead document camera.

The certificate named Drennan as assigned carrier for SSO-NS-004. The interval began at 06:54:00 EDT and ended with the 10:57:18 LSS intake. The stated purpose was EMERGENCY NATIONAL-SECURITY CONTINUITY SUPPORT. The named office authorizing authority was Diane Kessler, Deputy Director for National Security, Office of Senator Sterling.

No destination list appeared.

No operational purpose appeared.

No instruction source appeared.

Grant read the withheld-fields statement.

“Route details, internal requestor, substantive purpose, and office deliberations withheld pending privilege and classification review.”

“Correct,” counsel said.

“Who created the exception?”

“Kessler authorized it.”

“That was not the question.”

Counsel’s gaze hardened. “The certificate identifies the office authority.”

“It does not identify whether she initiated the exception or acted on an instruction.”

“That is outside today’s production.”

Grant looked at the interval.

06:54:00 began fourteen minutes before the WSS-4 hardware response.

It remained open through the 07:47:49 Hartwell challenge.

It remained open for more than three hours afterward, until the preservation demand forced the signer into compromise control.

Drennan had carried the device.

Kessler had authorized the continuity exception.

The record did not show that Drennan chose the route, knew the message content, understood K-17, or possessed any authority beyond physical custody. It did not show Kessler knew the operational purpose. It did not show who instructed her.

Vega said through the glass, “DCIS requested immediate federal seizure.”

“Hackett requested it,” Grant said.

“Would you still recommend against it?”

Grant looked at the closed pouch on the transfer surface.

If DCIS took the device, it would gain the object and a new custody fight. The office could characterize Drennan as the responsible possessor. The authorization certificate could remain behind a privilege wall, disconnected from the thing it had placed in his hands.

Under the LSS hold, the signer stayed frozen while the office’s original authorization, LSS transfer record, Hartwell original, and WSS local audit remained separately controlled. None could be replaced by the device itself.

“Yes,” Grant said. “The carrier is not the authorizer.”

Drennan looked at her for the first time without anger.

Grant continued. “And the authorizer is not yet the instruction source.”

Vega gave Vann permission to remove the pouch from the transfer surface.

He placed it inside a rigid secondary container, closed the lid, and applied LSS hold seal LSS-SL-90418. Two-person custody followed it through the rear control door. Grant never saw the signer. She saw the institution that had accepted responsibility for not using it.

Vega authorized a certified derivative from the transfer and office-assignment records.

LSS-CD-187463-01

It contained the device serial, pouch and visible closure identifiers, exterior condition, transferor, receiving custodian, witnesses, camera references, LSS clock basis, no-use restrictions, assignment interval, named office authorizing authority, original-record custodians, withheld fields, and proof ceiling.

It did not contain the signer’s internal state.

It did not contain office deliberations.

It did not claim Drennan knew why the device had been used.

It did not claim Kessler acted on Sterling’s instruction.

Grant signed only after those limits appeared on the first page.

Secure MPD Evidence Intake
Washington, D.C.

At 11:08:41, Grant’s image returned to the incident-control display.

Julie saw the change before Grant spoke. Not triumph. A narrower kind of certainty.

“The signer is under LSS no-use hold,” Grant said. “Named custodian Martin Vann. Deputy authority Marisol Vega. Pouch and compact case remain closed. Exterior serial confirmed as SSO-NS-004. No operation or technical examination.”

Ortiz read the chest status into the room record.

“All seven MPD packages remain stationary, separate, sealed, and under MPD physical custody. No package movement during the LSS action.”

Park confirmed continuous camera coverage.

Alvarez brought LSS-CD-187463-01 into DCIS-FR-187463-01 as a certified derivative. The source field identified the office secure-communications ledger and LSS compromise-control receipt separately. The original office authorization remained with the office. The original LSS intake and hold record remained with Legislative Secure Services.

Hackett read the authorizer field.

“Diane Kessler. Sterling’s deputy national-security director.”

“Office authority,” Julie said.

He looked at her.

“She authorized the exception,” Julie continued. “The record does not say who instructed her.”

“She works for Sterling.”

“So do people who schedule meetings, carry briefing books, and answer phones. Employment is not command evidence.”

Hackett’s jaw moved once. “You are protecting the distinction very aggressively.”

“I am protecting the part that survives counsel.”

Hackett said, “Then seize it now. We have the carrier.”

Grant answered before Julie could. “Legislative Secure Services can preserve or transfer. A transfer before the office production is complete changes custody while leaving the exception record behind.”

Alvarez looked toward Julie’s window. “What does immediate possession add?”

“An easier defendant,” Julie said. “Drennan carried a closed case under an assignment Kessler authorized. Take the signer now and every institution can point at him while the instruction behind the exception disappears into a custody fight.”

Alvarez made the authority choice herself. “The LSS no-use hold remains. No transfer to DCIS or MPD. Produce the office and WSS records first.”

Grant said, “The physical carrier was Samuel Drennan. His assignment began at 06:54 and remained active through WSS-4 and Hartwell. He surrendered the closed pouch when the preservation demand triggered LSS compromise control.”

“Did he present at WSS?” Alvarez asked.

“LSS cannot answer from the office record alone.”

“Then we need Northbridge.”

A renewed production request had already reached Nora Bell through counsel and the current lawful WSS-4 custodian. Bell’s answer arrived at 11:11:06.

She did not offer access to the suite.

She did not offer message plaintext.

She offered one certified derivative from the WSS local audit and sponsor record, produced under the existing preservation demand while the original remained in WSS control.

WSS4-CD-187463-01

The derivative identified the WSS original source, Bell’s sponsor event, the false service identities recorded at landlord and tenant control, local controller clock basis, session identifier NB-WSS4-0708-2217, device serial SSO-NS-004, full signer-state completion time, encrypted-block status, fields withheld, production authority, local seal reference, and proof ceiling. It did not identify Samuel Drennan as the WSS carrier.

It did not produce the encrypted message blocks.

It did not identify Drennan as the person who chose the session, understood K-17, or authorized Phase B.

It did not identify Bell, the WSS operator, or Northbridge as knowing participants in the larger operation.

Bell added one sentence in her own certification.

PRODUCTION IS MADE TO PRESERVE THE WSS-4 RECORD AND DOES NOT CONCEDE THE LAWFULNESS OF THE FALSE SERVICE ENTRY, THE REMOVAL OF INCIDENT MEDIA, OR ANY INTERPRETATION OFFERED BY DCIS, MPD, APEX, OR THE SUBJECTS.

Julie read it twice.

“Leave that sentence,” she said.

The duty attorney glanced toward her screen. “It is hostile.”

“It is honest.”

Bell did not have to like Julie to preserve the record. Northbridge did not have to accept the entire conspiracy to admit what its own controller had seen.

Alvarez linked the derivative without merging it with Hartwell’s.

The three independently controlled records did not share one clock, and Grant refused to let the matching times appear cleaner than their sources.

Hartwell’s controller time had already been reconciled to the DCIS reference by the offset documented in HWA-CD-187463-01. WSS-4’s derivative carried its own controller certificate and a recorded difference of less than two-tenths of a second from the DCIS time service at production. LSS used the wall clock visible in both intake cameras and recorded its last verification before the transfer. The office assignment ledger was less exact: its authorization began at 06:54 by minute, without a displayed seconds field. Alvarez made the difference explicit. The office record could establish that the assignment was active before the 07:08 WSS response and remained active through Hartwell. It could not establish the precise second Drennan received the pouch.

The Arjun acknowledgment remained on IST and supplied no new Washington event time. Its conversion stayed the fixed nine hours thirty minutes already recorded in the received incident, not a silent synchronization with any office system.

Only after every source retained its own clock basis did Alvarez allow the event order onto one comparison page.

The records aligned without sharing identical fields.

WSS fixed SSO-NS-004 inside the 07:08 session under Bell’s sponsor record.

Hartwell fixed the closed compact case inside the later 07:47:49 challenge-and-return path.

The office and LSS records fixed Samuel Drennan’s assignment and Diane Kessler’s authorization across an interval that covered both events.

WSS established the session-side use. Hartwell established the later challenge and return. LSS established present physical custody and the no-use hold. The office certificate established who had authorized the exception that placed the device in Drennan’s custody across the entire interval.

No record showed Sterling’s hand.

No record showed Kessler’s instruction source.

No record showed Drennan’s knowledge.

The absence no longer emptied the chain. It defined the next missing link.

Alvarez instructed her duty attorney to draft the reconciliation statement.

“Do not write that Kessler authorized WSS or Hartwell,” Julie said.

The attorney stopped.

“She authorized the custody exception that covered the interval,” Julie continued. “The separate site records show what happened during it.”

Grant nodded. “Authority to carry is not authority for every act the device later performed.”

Hackett said, “But without the exception, the device does not reach either site.”

“That is responsibility,” Alvarez said. “Not yet command.”

Office counsel objected from the audio-only line. “The certificate proves no operational nexus. Emergency continuity support is a routine protective function.”

Alvarez did not argue with the characterization. “Then the routine function will be easier to document.”

“It is already documented.”

“Partly. We have the authority and interval. We do not have the initiating request, the reason the normal custodian was bypassed, or the record that selected these two secure sites.”

“Those fields may be privileged.”

“They may be,” Alvarez said. “Preserve them while that is decided.”

The counsel line went silent for four seconds.

Julie watched Alvarez wait without filling the silence. An institution did not have to surrender a protected record merely because the record mattered. It did have to stop pretending a privilege dispute made the record nonexistent.

Office counsel returned. “The original initiating materials will be placed under internal legal hold. That does not concede production.”

“Entered as preservation only,” Alvarez said.

The answer gave them no instruction source. It prevented the source from becoming a future claim that no instruction record had ever existed.

She owned the words now.

The reconciliation statement recorded each original and derivative separately.

Hartwell retained HWA-LCA-1187.

HWA-CD-187463-01 remained a Hartwell-certified derivative.

WSS-4 retained its local audit and sponsor original.

WSS4-CD-187463-01 remained a narrow WSS derivative.

The Sterling office retained its original authorization and assignment record under preservation demand.

Legislative Secure Services retained the original intake and no-use-hold record and physical custody of the closed signer pouch.

LSS-CD-187463-01 remained the narrow office/LSS derivative.

Forward Post Arjun retained ARJ-K17-001, ARJ-K17-002, and ARJ-K17-003.

ARJ-K17-ACK-01 remained a bounded acknowledgment.

MPD retained MPD-901441 through MPD-901447, seven sealed packages in the same locked chest.

DCIS owned responsibility for the received incident and the new derivatives. It did not own every original.

At 11:18:22, Sterling’s statement ended.

The television returned to a studio panel discussing the danger of insider access. None of the speakers knew that the office signer had been placed under a no-use hold. None knew the name Diane Kessler. None knew that Samuel Drennan’s assignment interval covered both WSS-4 and Hartwell.

The public account had not weakened.

Inside the incident, it had become more expensive to maintain.

Hackett asked, “Can we interview Kessler now?”

Alvarez said, “We can request a voluntary interview and preserve her communications. Compulsory process requires a separate legal decision.”

“Can LSS question Drennan?”

“They can take a custody statement within their authority. Grant already took the transfer facts. Anything beyond that needs counsel and scope.”

“What about the signer?”

“It stays closed,” Alvarez said.

Hackett looked toward Julie. “You got the responsibility chain. Are you satisfied?”

The question carried more accusation than curiosity.

Julie looked through the reinforced glass at the locked chest. The case holding the first partial capture remained inside MPD-901441. Elias’s board remained inside MPD-901446, resealed beneath MPD-SL-551821. The WSS module remained untouched inside MPD-901447. Every object that had brought them this far was stationary while records outside the room moved toward one another.

Satisfied was the wrong measure.

“Drennan tells us who carried it,” she said. “Kessler tells us who authorized the custody exception. Neither tells us who instructed the exception or what outcome they expected.”

Hackett said, “You think Sterling instructed her.”

“I think his office owns the signer and his deputy approved the exception while he used the resulting crisis publicly. That is a reason to investigate. It is not the missing instruction.”

The duty attorney said, “What would be?”

“A contemporaneous request. A secure scheduling instruction. A written office direction. A witness with personal knowledge. Something that connects the decision to the operational chain without asking a certificate to read someone’s mind.”

Grant added, “And without requiring us to activate the device.”

Julie’s right hand pulsed inside the brace. She shifted the forearm less than an inch and stopped when the restraint tightened around the swelling.

Six years earlier, the official investigation had asked where the abort appeared in the surviving system and treated the missing four seconds as if no person had owned them. The current record was doing the opposite. It had named the carrier, the authorizer, the custodian, and the receiver while refusing to pretend any one of them answered for the instruction above them.

The refusal felt incomplete because it was.

It also created a place to continue.

“Eleven twenty-six thirty-two.”

“Who instructed Kessler to create and use the continuity exception,” she said, “and what lawful record connects that instruction to WSS, Hartwell, and K-seventeen without turning office responsibility into Sterling’s personal command?”
