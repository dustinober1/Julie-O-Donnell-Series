# Chapter 20 — The Custody Exception

October 15
09:06 Eastern Daylight Time

Secure MPD Evidence Intake
Washington, D.C.

The first useful answer arrived as a refusal to surrender the signer.

SSO-NS-004
STATUS: LOCATED WITHIN LEGISLATIVE SECURITY CONTROL
FEDERAL TRANSFER: NOT AUTHORIZED
COMPROMISE-CONTROL INTAKE: PENDING

Located and pending belonged to different clocks. Someone had found the office device after Hartwell returned it to the credentialed support path. No named custodian had yet accepted physical responsibility, and no record promised the device would remain unused while the institutions argued.

Julie watched from the observation alcove. Her right hand remained open inside the brace because it could not close. Through the glass, the common chest stayed locked under MPD control. Grant occupied the Hartwell channel. Alvarez appeared from DCIS. Hackett waited in a second window. On a muted television, Sterling continued calling the prior day’s events an attack on American warning systems.

A second legislative-security message appeared.

DUTY AUTHORITY: DEPUTY DIRECTOR MARISOL VEGA
PROPOSED CUSTODIAN: MARTIN VANN / COMPROMISE CONTROL
CONDITION: EXTERIOR INVENTORY / NO POWER / NO NETWORK / NO USE

The response supplied what the first lacked: names whose authority and physical responsibility could later be reviewed.

Hackett said, “Bring the device here.”

Alvarez shook her head. “Legislative Secure Services will accept a no-use hold. It will not transfer Senate property into executive custody without separate authority.”

“The device is evidence in a defense-intelligence incident.”

“It is also an office asset under legislative security. We can spend today fighting over possession or freeze it and preserve the custody records that put it in motion.”

The choice was hers, not Julie’s.

Grant asked for the assignment framework before any technical access. Hartwell had identified SSO-NS-004 as the accepted serial. WSS-4 had recorded the same device in the earlier session. Opening the signer would not identify who authorized either use and could create a new dispute about changed state.

Apex counsel supported immediate seizure on national-security grounds. Legislative counsel objected to executive intrusion. Both arguments made the object more important than the human decisions around it.

Alvarez signed a narrower order: exterior-only verification, named LSS custody, no activation, connection, challenge, imaging, decryption, rekey, zeroization, or operation. Original office and LSS records would stay with their lawful custodians. Productions would be certified derivatives.

“The carrier is not the authorizer,” Grant said before leaving Hartwell.

Julie almost reminded her to put it in the first line.

Grant’s expression stopped her. “I know.”

For once, Julie let another person carry the distinction without supervision.

---

Legislative Secure Services Compromise-Control Intake
Washington, D.C.

The closed black case arrived inside a gray tamper-evident pouch.

Grant stood behind a yellow floor line. No cable, reader, power source, or wireless device entered the room. Deputy Director Marisol Vega watched through glass. Martin Vann, the named compromise-control officer, stood at the receiving position with both hands visible.

The transferor was Samuel Drennan, secure-communications courier for Senator Sterling’s office. He wore a dark suit and the fatigue of a person who had spent a day being treated as both trusted staff and possible evidence.

“State what you are surrendering,” Grant said.

“One closed legislative secure-communications pouch containing one closed compact signer case assigned to me under emergency continuity authorization.”

“Exterior serial?”

“SSO-NS-004.”

“Did you open, activate, connect, challenge, image, rekey, zeroize, or operate it after Hartwell returned it?”

“No.”

Vann recorded the answer as Drennan’s statement, not as an adopted fact.

A clear window in the pouch exposed the compact case’s asset plate and one corner of its closure band. The serial matched Hartwell’s departure record. Grant did not ask anyone to open the pouch.

Drennan could confirm only his own custody interval. He had not continuously observed the case before it was assigned to him. Grant put that limitation into the transfer record before he signed.

At 09:42, Vann accepted physical custody.

“Device and pouch remain closed,” he said for the cameras. “Storage under two-person access. No release to the office, DCIS, MPD, Apex, Hartwell, Northbridge, or any other party without Deputy Director Vega’s written order, named receiving authority, and recorded transfer.”

Vega added, “No order authorizes use.”

The physical target stopped moving.

Grant turned to the assignment record.

Office counsel produced a one-page custody certificate. The original remained in the office secure-communications ledger. The derivative named Drennan as assigned carrier for SSO-NS-004 from 06:54 on October 13 until the LSS intake. The stated purpose was emergency national-security continuity support. The authorizing office authority was Diane Kessler, deputy director for national security in Sterling’s office.

The certificate withheld route details, internal requestor, substantive purpose, and office deliberations pending privilege and classification review.

“Who initiated the exception?” Grant asked.

“Kessler authorized it,” counsel said.

“That is the authorizer. I asked for the instruction source.”

“Outside today’s production.”

The interval began before the WSS-4 hardware response, remained open through the Hartwell challenge, and ended only after the preservation demand forced the signer into compromise control.

Drennan had carried the device.

Kessler had authorized the continuity exception.

The record did not show that Drennan selected the route, knew the message content, or understood K-17. It did not show what Kessler knew or who instructed her.

Vega asked whether Grant still recommended against federal seizure.

“Yes. Under this hold, the signer is frozen while four separately controlled records survive: the office authorization, LSS transfer, Hartwell challenge, and WSS audit. Taking the device gives us the object and leaves the responsibility chain behind a new custody fight.”

“The carrier is not the authorizer,” Vega said.

“And the authorizer is not yet the instruction source.”

Drennan looked at Grant for the first time without anger.

Vann moved the pouch into a rigid container under a single recorded LSS hold seal and two-person custody. Grant never saw the signer itself. She saw the institution that accepted responsibility for not using it.

The certified derivative identified the serial, exterior condition, Drennan’s custody interval, Vann and Vega’s hold, Kessler’s authorization, original-record custodians, and withheld fields. It made no claim about internal signer state, Drennan’s knowledge, Kessler’s instruction source, or Sterling’s personal command.

Grant returned to the MPD incident channel before noon.

“The signer is under LSS no-use hold,” she said. “Named custodian Martin Vann. Deputy authority Marisol Vega. Exterior serial confirmed. No technical examination.”

Alvarez accepted the certified derivative into incident 187463 without taking the office or LSS originals.

Hackett read the authorizer field. “Diane Kessler. Sterling’s deputy national-security director.”

“Office authority,” Julie said. “The record shows she authorized the exception. It does not say who instructed her.”

“She works for Sterling.”

“Employment is not command evidence.”

He looked frustrated by the distinction because the distinction was real.

Grant opened the next production request herself.

The office would identify the initiating continuity record, its creation time, apparent requestor, inherited reference, and custody state without producing deliberative content. LSS would preserve the signer. Hartwell and WSS would preserve their original events. No one would open the device to substitute its silence for the chain that moved it.

The physical signer was no longer the most urgent object.
