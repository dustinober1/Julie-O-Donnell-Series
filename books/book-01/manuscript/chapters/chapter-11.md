# Chapter 11 - Going Offensive

Decommissioned Communications Cutout
 Fairfax County, Virginia
 06:25 Eastern Daylight Time

The battery-backed clock advanced to 06:25:11.

No one moved.

Static breathed through the analog scanner. One fluorescent tube above the equipment table flickered at an interval too irregular to count. Beyond the wall, PCF-27 remained inside the service bay with its known plate, white contractor paint, and disconnected fleet module. The van was hidden from the alley. Nothing else about it had disappeared.

Marcus sat with his right leg extended beneath the table. Blood had begun to show through the lower edge of the dressing above his ear, a narrow dark line entering the gray at his temple. The cold pack around his thigh had gone soft. Cloth tape held the split heel of his boot in rough alignment, not enough to make the foot reliable. Each breath lifted only the upper part of his chest.

Elias still wore the wool blanket around his shoulders. His bandaged index finger rested against the administrator-token board in his lap as if contact alone could keep him from losing it.

Julie’s wrapped wrist lay against her body. The rigid plastic strip beneath the elastic had reduced the movement and taken most of the use of her right hand with it. The aluminum case stood closed at the end of the table. The tamper-bagged recovery cartridge and the paper custody log lay beside it in the order she had recorded them. The limited derivative remained sealed inside the locked storage cabinet.

Forty-two minutes and forty-nine seconds remained before the Northbridge window.

Marcus looked at the clock. “Then what?”

Julie heard the question beneath the question. If there was no recipient they could reach now, what did the word yet require them to do?

“We stop looking for someone to hand this to before seven-oh-eight,” she said.

Elias’s gaze lifted. “That sounds very much like deciding no one is honest.”

“It isn’t.” Julie pulled the paper summary toward her with her left hand. “An inspector general could be honest. FBI counterintelligence could be honest. The first officers who stop that van could be doing exactly what they should.”

“Detain us,” Marcus said.

“Yes. Separate us. Secure the case. Put Elias under protective custody because the alert calls him a hostage. Treat you as an armed accomplice and me as the person who corrupted Argus.”

Marcus’s mouth tightened at the word armed. They possessed no firearm. The bulletin did not need one to exist.

Julie continued. “Then someone finds a cleared technical team. Someone determines jurisdiction. Someone asks Apex for the system record. Vance gets to explain every file before anyone compares the certificate chains.”

“The inspector general could order a hold,” Marcus said.

“After intake. After authentication. After they know which inspector general owns the program. Northbridge’s synchronization will be gone before the first custody receipt is signed.”

Elias looked toward the locked storage cabinet. “A reporter?”

“Cannot safely publish the raw material and cannot authenticate it in forty minutes. If we give them the limited copy, Northbridge says its endpoint is part of a legitimate Apex contract. If we give them the case, we expose collection locations and the unresolved K-17 sensor record.”

“A congressional channel,” Marcus said.

Julie looked at the Northbridge certificate still open on the old workstation. “Sterling sits inside the oversight structure we would be asking for help. That does not make every staff member part of this. It means we cannot know where the warning goes before the evidence is secured.”

The scanner broke through the static with a county unit acknowledging the national lookout. No location followed. No confirmed sighting.

Marcus waited until the transmission ended. “So the problem isn’t trust.”

“The problem is sequence.” Julie touched the paper summary. “The first honest action is still custody. Understanding comes later.”

“And seven-oh-eight comes first,” Elias said.

Julie looked at the battery clock.

The three evidence sources could survive examination. They could not create the one record that did not yet exist.

“We make the missing evidence,” she said.

Marcus shifted in the chair. Pain stopped the movement halfway. “Define that before you turn it into a mission.”

Julie took the pen in her left hand.

“Northbridge Secure Suite Four receives a hardware-authenticated K-seventeen Phase B synchronization at seven-oh-eight. The suite holds the live session envelope for no more than ninety seconds. While it exists, the Northbridge endpoint, the external signer, the routing state, the hardware time, and the message buffer are all linked.”

“Ciphertext,” Marcus said.

“Probably.”

“So we may leave without the message.”

“We may leave with proof of who authenticated an exchange into the K-seventeen operational channel. Or proof that the signer belongs to someone we have not considered. Or proof that the exchange is less important than we think.”

Elias watched her write. “The full external certificate matters more than the name printed on it.”

Marcus looked at him.

“The certificate gives us the signing authority, device serial, trust chain, and the entity to which the device is registered,” Elias said. “The hardware signature shows that device actually answered the session challenge. It does not prove who was physically holding it.”

Julie wrote that distinction beneath the objective.

REGISTERED AUTHORITY IS NOT PHYSICAL POSSESSION.

“If the certificate resolves to Sterling?” Marcus asked.

“It still proves the device, not his hand,” Julie said. “If the content or routing adds his direction, we preserve it. We do not assume it.”

“And if it resolves to Northbridge counsel, an Apex executive, or a field contractor?”

“We preserve that too.”

“Who has the device now?” Marcus asked.

“The metadata does not say,” Elias replied.

Marcus watched the pen move. “K-seventeen?”

“Still unresolved. The field team’s last confirmed position is more than an hour old. The synchronization profile does not tell us whether they reached the relay, failed, withdrew, or are waiting for another instruction.”

He nodded once. The plan had not improved yet. It had only become honest.

Julie wrote the objective in one line.

REACH NORTHBRIDGE SECURE SUITE 4 BEFORE 07:08. CAPTURE THE LIVE BUFFER, ROUTING STATE, FULL SIGNING CHAIN, HARDWARE SIGNATURE, SESSION TIME, AND K17-PHASE-B LINKAGE. LEAVE.

Marcus read it.

“That is an intelligence requirement,” he said. “Not an entry plan.”

“No.”

“Where is the suite?”

Julie turned the workstation toward Elias. The Northbridge endpoint certificate filled the old display. They had examined the organizational subject and trust fingerprints. The lower fields remained collapsed beneath the service policy.

“Registered equipment location,” she said.

Elias used his left hand on the trackball. The first movement overshot the field. He corrected and expanded the certificate details.

Most of the entries were administrative: contract tenant, certification authority, inspection cycle, hardware class. Near the bottom, a location extension appeared beside the service-access profile.

REGISTERED EQUIPMENT SITE: FENWICK ANNEX
 TENANT: NORTHBRIDGE STRATEGIC INITIATIVES
 SERVICE LOCALITY: L STREET NW / WASHINGTON, DC
 FACILITIES ENTRY: REAR SERVICE VESTIBULE
 SECURE DOMAIN: WSS-4

Marcus leaned forward, then stopped when his ribs objected.

“The Fenwick Building,” he said.

“You know it?” Julie asked.

“I know the public side. L Street between Seventeenth and Eighteenth. Narrow commercial building, stone front, private offices above a ground-floor lobby. I attended two policy working groups there before Northbridge took the upper floors.”

“Secure annex?”

“Never saw it. There is a service alley behind the block and a landlord entrance off it. Deliveries, maintenance, cleaning crews. The public elevators are inside the L Street lobby.”

Nothing about the building, the public offices, or the existence of a secure contractor suite was illegal. The certificate made it a location. It did not make it a crime scene.

Elias expanded the next field.

CONTROLLER PROFILE: APX SECURE FACILITY 4.2
 MAINTENANCE ENROLLMENT: LOCAL / ESCORTED
 PRE-SESSION DIAGNOSTIC WINDOW: ENABLED
 LOCAL AUDIT INTERFACE: REQUIRED

He went still.

“What?” Julie asked.

“I helped design that profile.”

Marcus looked at him. “For Northbridge?”

“No. For Apex-certified contractor suites. Places that need compartmented communications without turning an ordinary office building into a federal facility.” Elias pulled the blanket away from his hands. “The landlord controls the outer doors. The tenant controller begins after the service vestibule. Public-office badges and secure-suite access are separate.”

“Current codes?” Julie asked.

“I do not know them.”

“Current staff?”

“No.”

“Cameras, guards, floor plan?”

“I know the minimum design requirements. I do not know what Northbridge installed or changed.”

“Good.”

Elias looked at her. “That is not usually the response to ignorance.”

“It is when the alternative is pretending.”

She pointed to the diagnostic-window field. “Explain.”

“A hardware-signer session creates a short local maintenance period before the scheduled exchange. A legitimately enrolled technician can test the suite certificate, the local clock, and the incident recorder without seeing message content. The enrollment has to happen at the building. The external signer may be somewhere else, but the Northbridge suite will be active and monitored by someone inside by seven-oh-eight.”

“Remote access?” Marcus asked.

“Not for this controller. That is the point. The service vestibule reads a tenant work order, the landlord guard verifies the contractor, and a Northbridge sponsor acknowledges the request. Then the controller can issue an escorted temporary session. The enrollment, the module serial, and every incident export become local audit events.”

“Can you make one?”

“No.” Elias answered before Marcus finished the question. “I can make something that looks like a work order. I can use the correct tenant identifier, service-call format, and diagnostic reason. The live system will know the ticket does not exist if anyone checks it.”

“How long before it knows?”

“That depends on the guard, the sponsor, and whether the controller is already in pre-session mode. It may reject us immediately.”

Julie looked at the service profile. “What does it buy?”

“A reason for someone inside to open the conversation. Perhaps a temporary intake screen. Seconds.”

“Not a door.”

“Not by itself.”

Marcus’s gaze remained on the certificate. “Where is the live buffer?”

“In volatile memory inside the communications controller. The operator sees a session status, not the full envelope. A protected audit port can export the session envelope to incident media if a local maintenance session is active.”

“Export what, exactly?” Julie asked.

Elias counted with his left thumb against his fingers. “Northbridge suite certificate. External signing certificate. Challenge and response. Hardware signature. Hardware time reference. Session identifier. Routing receipts. Task profile. Ciphertext buffer. The order in which they appeared.”

“Does it decrypt?”

“No.”

“Does it copy the rest of Northbridge?”

“No. It is deliberately narrow.”

“Can it open the secure suite?”

“No.”

Marcus sat back. “So we still need physical access to the room containing the port.”

“Yes.”

“And someone has to know when the external certificate is actually present.”

Elias looked at the screen.

Julie saw the answer before he gave it.

“The session can show open before the full chain loads,” he said. “The Northbridge certificate presents first. The external device may answer several seconds later. If you capture too early, you get Northbridge talking to an unidentified trust fingerprint. If you wait until the acknowledgment completes, the buffer may begin purging.”

“You can tell the difference,” Julie said.

“I know the state sequence.”

“I can write it down.”

“No.” The word came quietly. “You can memorize the labels. You will not know which delay is normal and which means the signer failed to present. The current software may not use the same colors or wording. The audit state is in the event order.”

Marcus looked from Elias to Julie. “Remote instructions are out.”

Elias’s bandaged finger pressed against the edge of the circuit board.

Julie said, “You stay with Marcus.”

His eyes lifted.

“I can take the module. You tell me the sequence now.”

“You just heard why that will not work.”

“I heard why it is less reliable.”

“It may be useless.”

“The alert still describes you as a hostage. The next camera that records you entering a Washington secure facility beside me will end that protection.”

“I know.”

“You may be arrested there. Your board may be seized. The incident capture may incriminate you again. You may not be able to walk back out on that hip.”

“I know.”

“I cannot promise I can get you out.”

Elias looked down at the board. The secure element that contradicted Vance’s deployment record sat beneath his thumb. His right hand trembled once and stopped when he closed it around the edge.

Julie waited.

“You can refuse,” she said.

“I know that too.”

“Then refuse if that is your decision.”

The scanner hissed. A truck changed gears somewhere beyond the alley. Marcus did not interrupt.

Elias drew the blanket tighter around his shoulders, then let it fall behind him onto the chair.

“You will not recognize the complete signer state,” he said. “And you will not know whether the incident export has preserved the event order until it seals.”

“That is an explanation.”

“I am going.”

His voice remained frightened. He did not make it louder to disguise that.

Julie looked at him for another second. “Voluntarily?”

“Yes.”

She opened the custody log to the next line and wrote the time.

Marcus watched her record it. “Now define everyone else.”

Julie put down the pen.

“You keep the originals.”

“The case stays outside the target?”

“The case never enters the building. Neither does the recovery cartridge or the custody log.”

“And me?”

“You stay with them and the van.”

Marcus’s expression hardened. “I can still control an entrance.”

“You cannot run, your balance is unreliable, and a deep breath is an argument. I am not putting you in a controlled corridor because you dislike sitting outside.”

“I can move far enough to pull you through a door.”

“Once.”

“That may be the useful one.”

Julie met his eyes. “The useful one is protecting the only evidence we already have.”

The words landed harder than an order would have. The aluminum case stood between them, intact because Marcus had refused to surrender it in Reston and carried it through every barrier after.

He looked away first.

“Where is the van?” he asked.

“Close enough for extraction. Not at the service door.”

“It is a known vehicle.”

“Yes.”

“If a plate reader catches it in Washington, response moves toward Northbridge whether they know the connection or not.”

“We use it because it is what we have. We enter through ordinary contractor traffic, park off the direct approach, and accept that every minute after the first camera is borrowed.”

“Who drives?”

“I do.”

“With that wrist.”

“With my left hand and the wheel braced against my forearm. You cannot work the pedals reliably.”

Marcus did not argue with the leg. “Route?”

“Rear industrial exit. One short diversion before we turn east. No toll road. Ordinary speed. Arlington Boulevard, then the Roosevelt Bridge unless traffic forces another choice.”

“Final approach?”

“You direct it from memory. We do not circle the target. We do not search for a perfect camera gap that does not exist.”

“Exit?”

“Same service route if it remains open. Ordinary fire stair to the public lobby if it does not. No deeper movement through Northbridge.”

“And if the service intake refuses?”

“We leave.”

“If the signer never appears?”

“We leave.”

“If the message proves less than expected?”

“We preserve exactly what it proves and leave.”

“If police arrive?”

Julie looked at both men. “We do not turn honest officers acting on a false alert into casualties. No firearms. No hostage-taking. No ramming a way out. If the only exit requires hurting someone who is not trying to kill us, the operation ends.”

Marcus studied her. “You are allowing arrest.”

“I am refusing murder as an escape plan.”

“And Northbridge security?”

“We do not attack them for doing their jobs. We use the service process. If deception fails, we disengage.”

Elias looked toward the workstation. “The local port is inside the secure domain.”

“Then we get there without making civilians pay for our evidence.”

Marcus continued testing the edges. “Search scope?”

“The live session only. No desks. No personnel files. No Northbridge archive. No attempt to take the communications controller.”

“Sterling?”

“We are not going there to find him.”

“If he is there?”

“We do not touch him.”

Marcus nodded once.

“Destruction?”

“None. The suite remains operational.”

“Disclosure?”

“No upload. No public release. We capture, preserve, and leave.”

“What time do I pull you?”

“Seven-ten.”

Elias looked at the clock. “The buffer should be gone by then.”

“The extra time is for us to get out, not to analyze it.”

“And if you are thirty seconds from sealing the export?” Marcus asked.

“Seven-ten.”

He held her gaze, measuring whether she believed her own abort condition.

Julie did. She did not know whether she would obey it. Command required setting the line before fear and proximity made every additional second feel necessary.

Marcus shifted the cold pack aside. “Civilian load?”

“Landlord security, cleaners, early policy staff, whoever Northbridge brought in for the regional crisis. We assume every person we see knows nothing about Payload Eighty-Eight.”

“That includes the person carrying the signer.”

“Yes.”

Elias said, “Even if the certificate belongs to Sterling’s office?”

“Even then. A certificate is evidence. It is not permission to decide what the person holding it deserves.”

Silence followed.

Marcus reached for the paper objective and turned it so he could read it again.

“Then we need a way to capture without risking the originals,” he said.

Julie opened the locked storage cabinet.

The derivative module remained inside its numbered antistatic envelope. The sealed partition held the recovery record, restored label map, provenance decision, Payload 88 index, checksum history, and the critical bridge fragments. It did not hold the bulk raw telemetry or the sensitive K-17 geography.

Elias examined the module through the clear side of the envelope. “It supports two separately sealed partitions.”

“You are sure?” Julie asked.

“The enclosure is standard field-evidence media. Partition one seals against its own manifest. Partition two can remain blank without changing the first.”

“Can the Northbridge audit port write to it?”

“If the controller still follows the 4.2 profile. The incident interface accepts encrypted removable media and writes a self-contained session package. It should not care what is on the other partition.”

“Should.”

“Yes.”

Julie returned to the workstation.

At 06:37:02, she broke the numbered envelope seal and recorded the action. Elias mounted the module through the hardware write blocker and verified the derivative partition. Its hashes matched the values entered in the paper log.

He created no new copy.

Instead, he used the module’s physical management utility to expose the unused second partition. The first remained hardware-locked and read-only. The second appeared as an empty encrypted incident volume with a separate serial and no files.

PARTITION A: SEALED DERIVATIVE
 INTEGRITY: VERIFIED
 WRITE STATE: LOCKED

PARTITION B: INCIDENT CAPTURE
 CONTENT: EMPTY
 WRITE STATE: ARMED ON LOCAL DEVICE

Julie recorded both states, the module serial, the wall-clock time, and the fact that the workstation clock remained wrong by eleven days. She had Elias verify Partition A again after the change.

MATCH.

Only then did she remove the module and seal it inside a fresh antistatic sleeve. The storage cabinet had no second numbered evidence envelope. She closed the sleeve with tamper tape from the medical cabinet and wrote the module serial across the seam.

“The sealed copy travels into Northbridge,” Marcus said.

“The originals stay with you.”

“If you lose the module?”

“We lose the limited duplicate and whatever we capture. The case, board record, and recovery cartridge remain.”

“The board goes inside,” he said.

Elias closed his hand around it. “The board stays with me.”

Julie wrote the exception into the log. The board was both original evidence and the only reliable proof of the distinction between Elias’s replayed identity and his voluntary emergency use. It had remained in his custody through every documented step. Taking it away now would not protect the chain. It would create another transfer and separate Elias from the evidence bound to his identity.

She transferred the aluminum case to Marcus at 06:39:16. He checked both latches without opening them and signed beneath the serial. The display remained green. The 136 sealed files remained sealed; the 47 incomplete files remained incomplete; the 311 excluded files remained absent.

The tamper-bagged recovery cartridge went into the waterproof document folder with the custody log. Marcus accepted both and signed again. Julie added the dead fleet transponder in its antistatic bag as an ancillary item. The originals would ride together without being connected to anything.

No evidence remained in the cutout’s storage cabinet.

Apex Building Three
 Reston, Virginia
 06:39 Eastern Daylight Time

Sarah Chen received the capture directive and the lethal-force directive forty-three seconds apart.

The first came through the joint incident desk established after the Building Three fire. It carried federal and county routing numbers, medical instructions, and language selected by people who expected their decisions to be reviewed.

APPREHEND IF LOCATED.
 SEPARATE SUBJECTS.
 SECURE CLASSIFIED MATERIAL.
 PRESERVE LIFE AND EVIDENCE.

The second appeared on the Apex protective-services channel under Arthur Vance’s executive authority.

CONTINUING HOSTILE SABOTAGE TEAM.
 PREVENT TRANSFER OF TELEMETRY MATERIAL.
 DEADLY FORCE AUTHORIZED IF LOSS OF MATERIAL IS IMMINENT.

Sarah read the last condition twice.

Deadly force was justified by an immediate threat to life, not by the possibility that evidence might leave the company’s control. Vance had replaced the legal standard with an operational preference and sent it around her command chain.

Daniel Mercer’s unit acknowledgment remained pending.

Sarah opened the voice channel. “Mercer.”

“Here.”

“You received the executive addendum?”

“Yes.”

“It does not alter my rules of force. Capture priority. Less-lethal where feasible. Firearms only for an identified deadly threat with a clear background.”

A pause. “Director Vance’s office marked telemetry loss as a national-security emergency.”

“Telemetry does not shoot back.”

Another pause, shorter this time. “Understood.”

Sarah did not order him to stop looking. She did not remove Julie’s alert or tell any agency that the subjects were harmless. Julie had breached a production enclave, removed classified material, triggered a fire, and fled in a stolen contractor vehicle. Sarah still intended to put all three into controlled custody.

She also intended to know whose order Mercer followed when he found them.

The traffic desk had no current plate hit for PCF-27. The last confirmed image placed it inside a cluster of contractor vehicles before the route split across several private camera systems. Analysts were requesting footage one owner at a time. A separate search had begun against retired technical-support leases associated with Marcus Reed’s former assignments. The first response was not due for twelve minutes.

No system had found the cutout yet.

The media pane on Sarah’s console updated.

A prepared statement from Senator Sterling’s office had entered the morning news pool. Its summary described Julie O’Donnell as the suspected architect of a deliberate Argus corruption attempt.

Sarah opened her own incident report.

It said suspected sabotage.

The technical source review had not assigned authorship. The enclave record had not been recovered. No federal examiner had concluded who introduced the poisoned data.

The public statement had reached a conclusion before the investigation reached the evidence.

Sarah marked the time, preserved both directives in the incident file, and sent the regional lookout again.

She did not warn Julie.

Decommissioned Communications Cutout
 Fairfax County, Virginia
 06:42 Eastern Daylight Time

The work order came from a carbon-copy service book in PCF-27’s glove compartment.

Potomac Critical Facilities used the forms for jobs that began before a dispatcher entered them into the fleet system. The paper carried the company name, the van identifier, and boxes for a tenant code, service site, reported fault, and authorization number.

The form was genuine.

The call was not.

Elias dictated the tenant identifier from the Northbridge certificate. Julie filled the boxes with her left hand, the letters slanting the wrong direction whenever she tried to write too quickly.

SITE: FENWICK ANNEX / WSS-4
 TENANT: NORTHBRIDGE STRATEGIC INITIATIVES
 CALL TYPE: LOCAL TRUST-CHAIN DIAGNOSTIC
 REPORTED CONDITION: PRE-SESSION AUDIT BUFFER RETENTION FAULT
 SERVICE WINDOW: 06:45–07:20

For the authorization number, Elias used the Apex service format embedded in the controller policy and a sequence derived from the tenant certificate.

“It will pass a casual glance,” he said. “It may pass the landlord intake field because the number has the right length and checksum.”

“Northbridge verification?” Marcus asked.

“Fails. There is no live ticket.”

“Then say that on the plan.”

Julie did.

The work order could begin the service workflow. It could not complete it.

They cleaned what they could.

Julie washed dried suppressant residue from her face and retaped the wrist support beneath the sleeve of her gray coveralls. The fabric still carried a dark smear at one knee and the RANDALL patch above the pocket. She left it. A perfect uniform would draw as much attention as a damaged one.

Elias found a Potomac rain shell in the van’s cargo rack. It was too large and smelled of rubber and old coffee. It covered his wrinkled office shirt and the outline of the cartridge no longer carried there. His hip remained stiff. His first three steps across the bay shortened progressively before he stopped and forced the leg straight. When he turned too quickly toward the workbench, the room shifted and he steadied himself against it until the dizziness passed.

Julie changed the dressing on his cut finger. The wound had not reopened deeply, but the fingertip remained clumsy. He could seat the incident module if he braced his hand. He could not be expected to work quickly while someone pulled him away from the port.

Marcus’s scalp dressing had bled through at one edge. Julie removed it, cleaned the skin again, and applied fresh pressure. He closed his eyes while she worked but answered every orientation question correctly. The swelling in his thigh had spread below the wrap. The taped heel of his boot was beginning to separate again. His ribs still stopped a full breath.

“You remain in the van,” she said.

“I heard you the first time.”

“I am checking whether the blood changed your answer.”

“It did not improve my personality.”

She secured the new dressing. “Nothing has.”

The exchange almost became familiar. Neither of them allowed it to.

Marcus checked the primary entrance, the ordinary fire exit, and the observation-room window. No vehicle waited in the alley. No footsteps stopped outside. The absence proved only that the search had not reached this door yet.

Julie shut down the forensic workstation through its normal process. She recorded the shutdown time and removed every evidence module. No file had been intentionally written to the workstation’s local storage. She did not wipe it. An examiner might later recover temporary system traces and determine exactly what hardware they had used.

The failed blank evidence module remained in the cabinet, labeled FAILED SELF-TEST and empty. The medical supplies stayed where they were. Nothing burned. Nothing was broken for the sake of making departure feel final.

Marcus locked the storage cabinet anyway.

“You expect to come back?” Elias asked.

“No,” Julie said.

The cutout’s archived lease history connected it to Marcus. The van’s route might connect the alley to the lookout. Every minute they remained converted a temporary shelter into a location someone could reconstruct.

Julie looked once at the obsolete racks, the bound log’s empty shelf, and the flickering fluorescent tube.

The place had done what Marcus said it would do.

It had bought time.

At 06:47:08, Marcus raised the service-bay door by hand. The metal rattled through the quiet alley.

Julie waited behind the wheel until the opening was high enough. Her left hand held the rim. Her right forearm rested against the lower spoke without closing around it. The aluminum case was wedged behind the passenger seat. The recovery cartridge remained sealed in its tamper bag, and the paper log lay beside it inside the waterproof folder.

Elias sat in the cargo opening behind them. The administrator board was inside a padded sleeve against his chest. The incident module and false work order rested in a small canvas tool pouch beside him.

Marcus checked both directions from the pedestrian entrance and signaled.

Julie eased PCF-27 into the alley.

Marcus lowered the bay door from inside, came through the pedestrian entrance, and locked it. By the time he reached the van, his limp had become worse with every step he tried to hide. He settled into the passenger seat with the waterproof folder beneath his jacket and turned the mounted service radio low enough to hear without filling the alley.

When the door closed, the cutout became an ordinary gray service bay again.

For anyone who knew where to look.

Northern Virginia
 06:47 Eastern Daylight Time

Julie drove west for two blocks before turning east.

The diversion did not confuse a tracking system. It kept the first camera outside the cutout from seeing a direct movement toward Washington if that camera existed and if anyone later recovered it. One turn. One minute. No theater.

Early contractor traffic had begun filling the commercial roads. Box trucks backed into bakery docks. Utility vans waited at a wholesale electrical supplier. PCF-27 moved among them at the speed of a vehicle whose driver wanted to reach a job, not escape a manhunt.

Marcus navigated from memory and a folded road map found in the van. He kept the route off the toll road and away from the most obvious federal approaches. He did not claim any road lacked cameras.

“Arlington Boulevard,” he said. “Then Roosevelt if it is moving. We make the final decision before the river.”

“Parking?” Julie asked.

“Commercial loading lane west of the Fenwick block. Morning contractors use it before office restrictions begin. Known van, ordinary context.”

“Plate readers?”

“Possible at every garage and major intersection.”

“Then no garage.”

“We park on the street and accept the clock.”

Elias leaned forward. “How far from the service entrance?”

“Two blocks if the loading lane is open. Three if it isn’t.”

“Marcus cannot walk that.”

“Marcus is not walking it,” Julie said.

He looked out the window. “I can see the rear approach from the van if we use the west end of the lane.”

“And the radio?”

“County, federal interop if they move the search into the District, and whatever the service set picks up. No private channel to you.”

Julie nodded. “Fixed clock. Seven-ten.”

“I know.”

The mounted service set broke into a regional traffic advisory and returned to law-enforcement traffic. Marcus turned up the van’s ordinary dashboard radio. A local news anchor repeated the Building Three fire, the stolen classified material, and Julie’s name. The language had changed.

Suspected sabotage became suspected architecture of the Argus corruption.

Elias heard it too. “They are making the conclusion stronger each time.”

“Because repetition feels like corroboration,” Julie said.

Traffic slowed near a signal lined with glass office buildings and one twenty-four-hour hotel. Julie stopped behind a plumbing truck. Her right wrist throbbed where the wheel’s vibration passed through the rigid support.

Inside the hotel lobby, a television hung above the reception desk.

Senator Sterling stepped toward a bank of microphones.

The screen divided. Sterling occupied the left half beneath flags and a committee seal. Julie’s consultant photograph filled the right, taken at Apex before the lower-tier descent. Her face looked tired and irritated, which the red banner beneath it converted into intent.

FORMER ARMY ANALYST SOUGHT
 SUSPECTED ARGUS SABOTAGE ARCHITECT

The van radio carried Sterling’s voice half a second after his mouth moved on the lobby screen.

“This morning, responders in Northern Virginia confronted a grave attempted compromise of American defense infrastructure. Authorities are seeking a former Army intelligence officer, Julie O’Donnell, in connection with the deliberate corruption of the Argus warning architecture and the removal of classified material.”

His delivery was measured. No anger. No demand for punishment.

“I urge the public not to approach the suspects and to allow law enforcement to perform its work. We should also remember the professionalism of the responders who prevented further harm. At a moment of evolving regional danger, the United States cannot permit insider manipulation to weaken the systems protecting our personnel, our allies, and the public trust.”
