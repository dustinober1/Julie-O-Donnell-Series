# Chapter 12 — The Sterling Trap

Northern Virginia
06:50 Eastern Daylight Time

Sterling’s accusation followed PCF-27 toward the Roosevelt Bridge. Julie held the wheel high with her left hand. Her right forearm rested against the lower spoke because the braced wrist could no longer close around it. “They know we’re eastbound,” Elias said.

“They know we were,” Marcus replied. “The systems still have to agree on where.”

A patrol car passed two lanes to the left. The officer’s gaze moved across the Potomac logo and returned to traffic. Julie did not change speed. No lights. No turn. Nothing she could call recognition. The radio anchor replaced Sterling’s measured language with something harder. Suspected architect became accused architect. Attempted compromise became attack. The bridge rose ahead. A camera housing waited above the District end. Elias saw it. “That one gets the plate.”

“Maybe,” Marcus said.

Julie kept the van inside its lane. The camera could capture them without stopping them. Someone still had to retrieve the image, compare the plate, decide who owned the alert, and reach an officer close enough to act. Separate systems. Borrowed minutes. At 06:55 the service radio changed.

“District units, be advised, suspect contractor vehicle may be entering the District. No confirmed location. Coordinate before stop. Classified material believed present.”

“Not yet,” Marcus said.

They crossed into Washington. The commercial loading lane west of the Fenwick block was already full. A bakery truck held two spaces. A refuse vehicle blocked the service gate behind it. “Past the light. Right. West-side lane.”

“That moves us half a block from the service entrance,” Julie said.

“It gives me the alley mouth and the east fire exit.”

Julie made the turn. The second lane was narrow, crowded, and open. She parked behind a laundry truck with linen carts stacked inside its rear doors. “Seven-ten is abort. Seven-eleven, you move between exits. Seven-thirteen, you leave with the originals.”

Marcus’s expression did not change. “I heard it.”

“You preserve the case.”

“I heard that too.”

“You do not come inside.”

His eyes met hers. “You designed the operation. Don’t let getting inside become the reason you stay.” Julie turned toward Elias. He opened the canvas pouch. The tamper tape on the incident module remained intact. The serial across the seam matched the false work order. Partition A held the sealed derivative. Partition B remained empty and write-armed. “Failure condition,” Julie said.

“If they check the live Northbridge queue, there is no ticket.”

“The paper?”

“Genuine Potomac form. Correct tenant code, service format, authorization length, and checksum. False call.”

“What does it buy?”

“Intake.”

“What does it not buy?”

“Access.”

Marcus opened the passenger door. Cold air entered with diesel and bakery exhaust.

“Seven-ten,” he said.

Julie climbed out using her left hand on the door frame. Elias followed through the side door. His injured hip took the first step badly and forced him still until the dizziness passed. Marcus remained with one hand against the case.

Julie leaned toward the open window. “If response moves early—”

“I change position. I don’t change the abort.”

She nodded and turned away.
