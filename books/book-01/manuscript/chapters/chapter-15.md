Park checked the wrist brace again and called the injury into her camera. Standard rear-cuffing would place the right hand behind Julie’s back, rotate the damaged wrist, and require grip she did not possess. Ortiz authorized a front transport belt after the paramedic documented the restriction.

Park secured Julie’s left wrist in a double-locked cuff attached to the belt. The arrangement still restrained her.

It simply did not pretend injury was defiance.

Park slid a property receipt for Julie’s personal key into a separate ordinary-property envelope. It did not enter the evidence chest and did not share an item number with the originals. Julie noticed because the distinction would matter later. Evidence, property, and restrained person were three different custody problems, even when the same officer controlled all of them.

Ortiz told dispatch the three medical destinations would be assigned separately and that no questioning beyond identity, medical status, and immediate safety should delay transport. That precision made the scene less dramatic to anyone listening for a confession.

It made it more dangerous to anyone depending on the acts being blurred together.

Julie could no longer touch any part of it—or either man who had helped keep it alive.
