# Chapter 15 — The Split Record

October 13
07:49 Eastern Daylight Time

Commercial Garage West of Hartwell
Washington, D.C.

Officer Gabriel Ortiz made the scene smaller before he moved anyone.

“Ms. O’Donnell, left hand on the roof. Right arm stays against the brace.”

Julie faced PCF-27 and placed her left palm above the driver’s door. Officer Hannah Park searched her on camera, naming every contact before she made it. The mechanical key to Julie’s Ford went onto the hood. Nothing else came out of her pockets.

“No weapon,” Park said. “No phone. No electronic device.”

She examined the support around Julie’s wrist without loosening it. “Close your hand.”

Julie tried. Her fingers moved. The thumb stopped before useful pressure formed.

“Can’t.”

“Can you lift the aluminum case?”

“No.”

“Help either man if he falls?”

Julie looked at Marcus in the passenger opening and Elias braced against the side panel. “No.”

Park recorded the answers and stepped away. The admission changed more than Julie’s argument about Hartwell ever could. She could describe the chain. She could no longer carry it.

Two paramedics came up the east ramp with oxygen and a folded stretcher. They went first to Marcus. Fresh blood had entered the dressing above his ear. His right leg trembled when he shifted, and each breath stopped high in his chest.

The lead medic clipped a pulse oximeter to his finger.

“Ninety.” She fitted a nasal cannula. “You’re going to the hospital.”

“I need the transfer completed.”

“You need oxygen. You get the minimum record I allow, sitting down, and then you move.”

Medical authority had entered the garage with its own clock.

The second medic checked Elias. His hip accepted weight only in short intervals. The cut on his right index finger had opened beneath the dressing, and dizziness followed every quick turn of his head. He refused the stretcher but agreed to remain against the van.

Ortiz stood where both body cameras could see him. Before patrol, he had spent six years rotating through MPD’s digital-evidence and federal-liaison unit. He did not understand Argus or the signer architecture. He understood what disappeared when one incident was split before a receiving officer put a name on it.

“Nothing gets opened here,” he said. “We identify what is physically present, seal each object separately, and move all of it under one incident number until a named authority accepts otherwise.”

Mercer remained one concrete column away with two Apex operators. “The aluminum case and electronic media are classified company and program property. Apex requests immediate protective recovery.”

“Who receives criminal custody?” Ortiz asked.

“Apex is the designated facility-security authority.”

“For Building Three. Name the officer accepting these objects now.”

Mercer touched his radio.

Arthur Vance answered through the open channel. “The material is strategic telemetry removed during continuing hostile sabotage. Secure it before O’Donnell directs another protected-system action.”

Ortiz looked at Julie. “Any device connected?”

“No.”

Elias held the dual-partition module against his chest. “Offline. Both partitions sealed. The button writes only a local handling marker.”

Vance said, “Mr. Thorne is a possible hostage and cannot authenticate his own condition.”

Elias’s face tightened. “I am not a hostage.”

Sarah Chen entered the channel before Vance could answer.

“Incident coordination confirms Metropolitan Police scene authority. Capture remains active. No media is to be opened, connected, altered, destroyed, or transferred without a named receiving custodian and itemized receipt. Executive recovery instruction and force directives are preserved in the incident file.”

She had not released them. She had also denied Vance an undocumented handoff.

Julie had spent the morning treating Sarah as the polished voice of Apex containment. She had been wrong about the size of that alignment. Sarah still intended to put them in custody; she had also preserved the order that could prove why custody mattered.

Ortiz looked at Mercer. “Which instruction are you operating under?”

“MPD scene control. Apex recovery claim documented. No contact without your direction.”

That was not allegiance. It was a professional choosing the order he could defend in front of public cameras.

Ortiz began with Marcus.

The aluminum incident-capture case stayed between his boots. He read the etched serial without touching the latches. Then he described the waterproof folder against his thigh: the sealed recovery cartridge, paper custody log, and disconnected PCF-27 fleet module. He stated that the case held 136 sealed files, with 47 incomplete and 311 excluded during finalization. He did not list every file again.

“Current custodian?” Ortiz asked.

“Marcus L. Reed.”

“Will you release the objects to MPD under recorded receipt?”

“Yes. Separately. No opening.”

“Will you physically resist?”

“No.”

Park prepared the first four packages on the hood of the evidence vehicle. The case remained closed while she photographed both latches, the display, and the serial. The recovery cartridge stayed inside its tamper bag. The paper log went into a document sleeve without being copied. The dead fleet module remained ancillary evidence, not proof of the route by itself.

Marcus signed each transfer with his left hand. His writing deteriorated by the fourth line. The medic stopped him before he could rewrite any of it.

“Good enough,” she said. “Now oxygen and transport.”

The words frustrated him because they were true.

Elias came next. He identified the administrator-token board against his body and the dual-partition field module in his hands. The board, he explained, carried the hardware history distinguishing the original replayed deployment identity from his later voluntary gate and recovery acts. Partition A of the module held a sealed derivative made before Northbridge. Partition B held the sealed WSS-4 incident package.

“What does either object prove about Hartwell?” Ortiz asked.

“Nothing alone,” Elias said. “Hartwell’s record is held there. This module proves what WSS-4 received and when.”

“Did O’Donnell force you to enter Northbridge or hold the module past her abort time?”

“No.” Fear shortened the answer without changing it. “Those were my acts.”

“Are you willing to transfer both objects to MPD under separate seals?”

Elias looked at Julie.

She kept her left hand on the roof. “Your decision.”

He looked back at Ortiz. “Yes, if the receipt records current condition, serial, partition states, and that the board remained in my possession until transfer.”

“It will.”

Park prepared two more packages. Elias watched the board enter an antistatic enclosure and the module enter a separate shielded bag. He read each serial aloud. Ortiz repeated them. Park sealed them in camera view and handed Elias the receipts before the medic guided him to a chair.

Six objects sat apart.

The mechanical key from Julie’s pocket became the seventh. Ortiz initially treated it as personal property. Julie asked him to preserve it because it established that the Ford—not the tracked contractor van—remained her ordinary vehicle and because Apex’s first report had described her as arriving prepared for sabotage.

Ortiz considered the request, then sealed the key as ancillary context rather than classified evidence.

Seven packages. One incident. No claim that proximity made them one technical object.

Park placed them in a locked common chest bolted to the MPD evidence vehicle. Each package retained its own label and seal. The chest received a separate transport seal. Its camera showed the seven packages before the lid closed.

Mercer said, “The electronics require separate classified environmental control.”

“They may,” Ortiz replied. “A named federal receiver can make that decision after accepting what exists now.”

The Hartwell preservation request returned over dispatch while the chest was being locked.

“Unit Three, Hartwell duty security confirms an administrative hold on the local-challenge record, access logs, controlled-door record, and relevant camera segments for zero-seven-forty-six through zero-seven-forty-eight-thirty. No disclosure authorized to scene personnel.”

Ortiz repeated the hold number. Marcus, already on the stretcher, asked Park to enter it in the paper log before she sealed the document sleeve. Park did so in her own hand and stated that the entry was hers.

The record no longer depended on Marcus remaining conscious.

At 08:03, the medical team moved him toward the ramp. He caught Julie’s eyes above the oxygen tubing.

“Don’t let them divide it.”

Ortiz answered before Julie could. “That is my problem now.”

Marcus accepted the correction.

Elias was placed in a separate ambulance under guard, not because police had decided he was a hostage, but because his hip and dizziness required a different treatment team. Before the doors closed, he asked who controlled future contact.

“You and your lawyer,” Julie said.

Ortiz heard it. “Lawyers come after intake.”

“The boundary exists before one arrives.”

Elias nodded once and let the doors close.

Park secured Julie’s left wrist to the front transport belt. The padded medical restraint held her injured right forearm against her body without pulling it behind her. Julie tested neither cuff. She had demanded a named authority; she had one.

Vance’s claim, Sarah’s directives, Mercer’s observations, the Hartwell hold, Marcus’s transfer, Elias’s voluntary statement, and the seven seals now existed on police systems outside Apex.

None established who had held SSO-NS-004.

None established who had ordered the original 02:14 deployment.

They established something more immediate: the evidence had survived the moment when the people carrying it could no longer keep carrying it.

At 08:07, Ortiz closed the rear door.

For the first time since Reston, the truth moved without Julie’s hands on it.
