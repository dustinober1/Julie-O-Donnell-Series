# Chapter 9 - The Life-Safety Override

Apex Building Three
Reston, Virginia
05:01 Eastern Daylight Time

The air tightened against Julie’s ears.

A valve struck open above the cabinets. Another answered farther down the ceiling, and the low warning tone beside the gate changed from a steady pulse to two descending notes. Cooling air that had moved almost without sound began drawing toward the ceiling nozzles in uneven currents.

The suppression display showed:

INERT AGENT RELEASE ARMED
 OCCUPIED-ROOM STATUS: OVERRIDDEN
 DISCHARGE IN 01:23

Julie kept her left hand beneath the raised cover of the abort control and read everything around it before touching anything else.

MANUAL ABORT DISABLED
 EXTERNAL PHYSICAL RESET REQUIRED

A small contact indicator beneath the control remained lit. The switch had registered her action; the hardened incident controller had rejected it downstream. A smaller line identified the reset as a keyed device in the manifold service corridor outside the armored gate.

Below the message, a printed instruction plate showed two parallel control paths. The detector loops fed Apex’s programmable suppression controller on one side and, through separate supervised contacts, a red box labeled HARDWIRED LIFE-SAFETY RELAY on the other.

A red metal conduit left that box and disappeared directly into the gate frame.

Vance had made the room empty in software. The physical detectors still had a path to the locks.

“Elias.”

He was still at the recovery console, one hand braced on its edge to keep weight off his injured hip. “I see it.”

“What does the red path do?”

He looked from the diagram to the environmental display. Fear made him speak too quickly, but it did not make him guess.

“Fire alarm, smoke control, egress. It doesn’t share the incident controller. It can remove a security hold from the gate and release protected doors outside the enclave.”

“Can Vance disable it?”

“Not through the same channel. The relay is supervised point to point. A remote bypass would report as a life-safety fault outside the security network.”

“Can it stop suppression?” Marcus asked.

He had pulled himself upright against a hardened cabinet near the gate. His damaged boot rested at an angle, heel peeled away from the sole. He watched the seam between the steel leaves and the status strip beside it.

“No,” Elias said. “The release latch is already set. Life safety can let us out. It cannot cancel what he armed.”

The display reached 01:14.

Julie followed the red conduit with her eyes. It entered a narrow fire-interface cabinet beside the gate, separate from the protected abort control. Three hardwired indicators were dark beneath a clear cover.

ZONE H-3 — POWER DISTRIBUTION HEAT
 ZONE S-4 — RECOVERY AISLE SMOKE
 VERIFIED FIRE — EGRESS RELAY

“Two zones,” she said.

Elias nodded. “Independent confirmation. A single detector gives prealarm. A test input or a detector fault won’t release the gate. Heat in the distribution bay and smoke in the recovery aisle will.”

“Real heat and real smoke.”

“Yes.”

Marcus glanced toward the gate. “And when it opens, Mercer will be on the other side.”

“No pump,” Julie said.

The hydraulic strain that had worked against the six bolts moments earlier was gone. Beyond the steel, she heard only one boot scrape and a radio click farther back than before.

“They cleared the threshold,” Marcus said. “Not the level.”

Julie crossed to the recovery console. The aluminum case remained open beneath it, the evidence drive seated in its foam cradle and the shielded cable locked into the protected forensic port. She could not carry it until the read-only session was closed.

“What preserves the sealed recovery record if we leave?” she asked.

“The enclave log.”

“Inside the enclave.”

Elias looked at the lower service panel. “There should be a physical incident recorder. Post-event investigators use it if the enclave loses network and power.”

“Should?”

“I have never removed one during an active suppression countdown.”

“Find it.”

Julie brought up the media controls. The display showed the case still mounted READ-ONLY and the reconciliation workspace already sealed.

CLOSE INCIDENT INPUT SESSION?

She selected YES.

The console began committing the final media state to the case manifest. No gray wheel appeared. Instead, five lines checked themselves in sequence.

READ-ONLY BOUNDARY CONFIRMED
 SEALED BLOCK COUNT CONFIRMED: 136
 INCOMPLETE BLOCK COUNT RECORDED: 47
 EXCLUDED BLOCK COUNT RECORDED: 311
 RECOVERY SESSION REFERENCE ATTACHED

The countdown above the gate reached 00:59.

Marcus pushed away from the cabinet and came to the case. The first step on his right leg almost failed. He caught the console with one forearm, waited for the muscle in his thigh to stop jumping, then crouched without bending his ribs more than necessary.

“Tell me when.”

The media display changed.

INCIDENT INPUT UNMOUNTED
 SAFE TO DISCONNECT

“Now.”

Marcus released the locking collar with both thumbs. Julie held the socket steady with her left hand while he drew the connector straight out. The cable came free without resistance.

She coiled it into the lid compartment. Her right hand could guide the loop but not tighten it. Marcus pressed the retaining strap closed.

Elias had opened a recessed panel beneath the recovery console. Inside, a black metal cartridge sat behind a tamper strip and a mechanical release lever. Its face carried no capacity marking, only a serial number and three green indicators.

LOCAL HARDWARE SIGNATURE
 RECOVERY RECORD SEALED
 PHYSICAL REMOVAL AVAILABLE

“That is it,” he said.

“What is on it?” Julie asked.

“Only the incident chain. My emergency access, the restored Payload Eighty-Eight labels, the packet boundary you approved, the provenance failure, the suspended external commit, and the mandatory-review result.”

“Not the full production archive.”

“No.”

“Release it.”

Elias put his cut right index finger against the lever and could not depress the spring catch. The finger slipped, leaving a dark smear on the metal.

“Use your left.”

“The catch and lever have to move together.”

The display showed 00:46.

Julie reached toward it, then stopped. She did not know the mechanism. Elias did.

He changed his grip, braced the heel of his right hand against the console, and used his left thumbnail to hold the recessed catch. His hands shook hard enough that the cartridge rattled in its guides.

The catch moved.

He pulled the lever.

The tamper strip divided cleanly along a numbered seam. The cartridge extended two inches.

Elias took it and slid it into the inside pocket of his shirt beside the narrow administrator-token circuit board. He pressed both objects flat against his chest as if their weight might pull him over.

The cartridge preserved what they had stopped. It did nothing for the five or six people still moving toward Relay K-17.

Julie closed the case. The left latch seated. The right resisted when she pushed with her damaged hand. Pain traveled into her forearm and her grip opened involuntarily.

Marcus reached across and drove the latch down with the heel of his hand.

“Carry left,” he said.

“I know.”

The display reached 00:39.

Elias brought up the fire-zone matrix. A small plan of the enclave appeared, divided into two colored rectangles. H-3 covered a redundant power cabinet on the wall opposite the recovery console. S-4 covered the console aisle and the return-air sampling tube above it.

“The B-side distribution unit feeds the empty expansion bay,” he said. “The recovery console is on A-side. The evidence interface is isolated from both.”

Julie crossed to the cabinet and read its labels.

PDU-B
 REDUNDANT SERVICE FEED
 EXPANSION BAY / MAINTENANCE LOAD BANK

A protected selector beneath the service cover offered OFF, NOMINAL TEST, and OVERLOAD TEST — ATTENDED.

Beside it, a warning stated that the load bank required its vent cover and active cooling.

Julie looked at the perforated blanking plate over the exhaust. Four quarter-turn fasteners held it in place.

“Does overload trip the branch?” she asked.

“At one hundred twenty-five percent, eventually. The load elements get hot first. It is for thermal inspection.”

“How hot?”

“Hot enough to fail if the vent is blocked.”

“That is not a number.”

“I did not commission the cabinet.”

Marcus had already moved beside her. “What comes off?”

“The plate.” Julie pointed to the fasteners. “Keep it. We need the airflow.”

He turned the first fastener with his fingertips. The second resisted. He wedged the edge of his damaged boot beneath the plate and used the heel of his hand on the fastener. The movement pulled across his ribs. His breath stopped, then returned in a shallow line.

The plate came free.

Behind it, a bank of ceramic load elements sat inside a metal duct. Cooling air moved across them toward a grated exhaust near the ceiling. A cable bundle serving the empty expansion cabinet ran beside the duct in a gray polymer sleeve.

Julie looked back at the case. The foam cradle around the evidence drive had protected it through two escapes. The lid compartment contained a separate foam spacer that had held the cable collar in place.

She tore the spacer free with her left hand.

“What are you doing?” Elias asked.

“Making particulate.”

“That foam will produce toxic smoke.”

“So will the cable jacket if we let the cabinet choose.” She tore off a strip no longer than two fingers. “This is smaller, and we control where it goes.”

The countdown showed 00:27.

Julie placed the strip against the metal lip above the load elements, close enough to heat without touching an energized conductor. She turned the selector to OVERLOAD TEST.

The cabinet gave a low electrical hum.

Its cooling fan accelerated.

Marcus held the blanking plate at an angle, turning the exhaust away from the power bay and across the recovery aisle. The plate shook in his hands. He braced one edge against the cabinet so his ribs did not have to take the full force.

The load elements darkened from gray to a faint, uneven red.

A detector inside the exhaust plenum changed state.

ZONE H-3 — RATE OF RISE
 PREALARM

The hardwired relay remained dark.

“Smoke,” Julie said.

The foam softened at the edge. A thin black line formed where it touched the heated lip. Then a thread of gray smoke lifted into the exhaust.

Marcus adjusted the plate by inches. The airflow caught the smoke and carried it across the aisle toward a row of small red sampling ports in the overhead return tube.

The smoke thinned too quickly.

“Lower,” Elias said. “The S-4 intake is the second port. The first belongs to the cabinet bay.”

Marcus lowered the plate. His damaged boot folded under him.

He struck one knee on the raised floor and nearly lost the baffle. Julie caught the upper edge with her right hand. The weight pulled her wrist backward. Her fingers opened at once.

The plate dropped against the cabinet with a metallic crack.

Marcus seized it before it fell flat and forced it back into position with his forearms.

The countdown showed 00:18.

Smoke crossed the aisle in a denser ribbon.

The second sampling port drew it in.

ZONE S-4 — PARTICULATE DETECTED
 ALARM VERIFICATION

For one second, nothing else happened.

Then the third hardwired indicator turned red.

VERIFIED FIRE
 LIFE-SAFETY RELAY ACTIVE

Apex Security Command Center
 05:02 Eastern Daylight Time

The first detector reached Sarah Chen as a yellow point inside the production enclave.

H-3 POWER DISTRIBUTION HEAT — PREALARM

Mercer’s team had cleared the gate’s movement envelope and established a position at the production junction twenty feet beyond it. Two operators covered the main approach. A third watched the normally sealed service egress. No one stood where the armored leaves or a pressure release could catch them.

Arthur Vance saw the prealarm.

“Isolate the cabinet,” he said.

“The enclave is already isolated.” Sarah opened the fire matrix. “This is a physical detector, not a software status.”

“Then they are damaging the production environment.”

The second detector appeared.

S-4 RECOVERY AISLE SMOKE — CONFIRMED

The fire panel did not ask Apex for permission.

VERIFIED FIRE
 HARDWIRED EVACUATION CONFIGURATION
 EXTERNAL MONITORING SIGNAL TRANSMITTED

Across the command center, white fire strobes replaced the amber suppression lights. A building alarm began on the floors above them. The fire system reported its own transmission path: campus emergency control, contracted monitoring center, county dispatch interface.

Sarah selected the security hold on the enclave gate.

The command returned:

SECURITY SEAL SUBORDINATE
 VERIFIED FIRE EGRESS IN PROGRESS

Vance moved to the adjacent console. “Reassert the hold through executive control.”

“It is not on executive control.”

“Every actuator in this building is on a control path.”

“Not one you own from here.”

The words came out sharper than she intended.

Vance looked at her.

Sarah keyed Mercer’s channel.

“Containment One, gate entering automatic egress. Remain clear of leaf and pressure zones. Establish custody line at Junction Delta. Weapons controlled. Do not fire into agent discharge or moving fire partitions. No entry until the suppression zone clears.”

Mercer answered immediately. “Service egress is releasing.”

“Cover it. Do not obstruct the fire lane.”

She opened the second-team channel. “Containment Two, reposition to Loading Three and the south mechanical ramp. Preserve access for fire response. Subjects remain capture priority. Telemetry case has priority over all other property.”

Vance said, “Over the occupants?”

Sarah did not repeat his language.

“Lethal force is authorized once they clear sensitive machinery,” he said.

She kept both channels open. “Ballistic force remains restricted to an identified deadly threat with a clear background. Less-lethal first. Recover all three and the case.”

Vance’s expression changed by less than a degree.

He opened the hardened incident-report channel and selected HOSTILE INSIDER SABOTAGE. O’Donnell’s name populated the primary-subject field before the gate completed its first bolt cycle.

He had lost command of the gate.

He had not lost command of the report that would explain why it opened.

Production Enclave
 05:02 Eastern Daylight Time

The room changed state all at once.

White strobes fired from the ceiling. Emergency power dropped from PDU-B, killing the load bank before the foam strip could flare. The smoke-control fans reversed, drawing the remaining gray particulate toward the fire-zone return.

The environmental display replaced the countdown header.

VERIFIED FIRE
 EVACUATION CONFIGURATION ACTIVE
 SUPPRESSION RELEASE LATCHED
 DISCHARGE IN 00:12

The six gate bolts began withdrawing.

The first struck inside the wall with enough force to move the air. The second followed from the opposite side. Then the third and fourth.

Julie lifted the aluminum case with her left hand. Its weight pulled her shoulder down. Marcus got beneath the opposite lower edge with one forearm.

“Elias first,” she said.

He stared at the recovery console. The open recorder bay showed an empty slot and the broken numbered tamper strip.

“Elias.”

“The local log—”

“Is sealed. You have the recorder.”

He pressed a hand against his shirt, confirming both the cartridge and the circuit board remained there. Then he limped toward the gate.

The fifth bolt withdrew.

The sixth stopped halfway.

A status line appeared.

PRESSURE DIFFERENTIAL ABOVE EGRESS LIMIT
 EQUALIZATION ACTIVE

A vent opened over the gate. Air moved outward through it in a hard, cold stream.

The suppression clock reached 00:07.

“Why is the bolt stopped?” Marcus asked.

“Safety load,” Elias said. “It will clear when pressure falls.”

“Before or after we stop breathing?”

The pressure number dropped.

38 Pa.

The sixth bolt slammed free.

The gate motors engaged. The two steel leaves shifted backward into their hinge housings, then began rotating inward.

A black seam opened between them.

The suppression display reached 00:02.

“Move with the gap,” Julie said.

The seam widened to the width of a hand.

One second.

The manifold released.

The roar struck from every ceiling nozzle at once.

The agent drove downward hard enough to turn condensation into white turbulence. Loose maintenance labels lifted from the console and spun across the room. Mist flashed along cabinet edges. The pressure wave hit Julie’s face and forced air from her mouth.

The smolder at PDU-B disappeared beneath the flood.

The gate kept opening.

Elias turned sideways and pushed into the gap before it reached the marked personnel width. His shoulder caught the left leaf. He forced through, dragging his injured leg after him, and vanished into the white-strobed approach.

Julie and Marcus shoved the case edge-first behind him. Elias caught the handle with both hands and almost lost it when his cut finger failed. He lowered the case to the floor instead of trying to lift it.

Julie followed.

The air near the gate still carried oxygen from the corridor, but each breath gave her less than the one before. Her attention narrowed to the steel edge beside her face, the case beyond it, and Marcus behind her.

She cleared the threshold and turned.

Marcus had one hand on the frame. His damaged boot folded as he stepped between the leaves. The torn heel rolled under his foot, and his struck thigh failed at the same moment.

He went down inside the opening.

The agent discharge hammered his back and filled the space around him with white mist.

Julie reached with her right hand by reflex. Pain broke her grip before she closed it around his wrist.

She changed position, hooked her left forearm beneath his elbow, and pulled.

Marcus moved six inches. His damaged heel caught in the raised threshold channel.

Elias left the case and came back.

Every part of him wanted the corridor. Julie could see it in the angle of his body and the panicked effort to draw a full breath. He returned anyway, caught the back of Marcus’s coveralls, and pulled with both hands.

Marcus kicked free. The sole tore farther away but released from the channel.

They dragged him into the approach.

Behind them, the enclave disappeared into condensation and agent turbulence. The gate leaves continued opening because the hardwired egress cycle had priority. The roar followed them through the widening seam.

“Up,” Marcus said.

He could not make his body obey the word.

Julie and Elias got him onto one knee. He found the wall and used it to stand.

At the far end of the twenty-foot approach, Daniel Mercer waited behind the corner of Junction Delta with three operators. Their rifles remained lowered. One carried a compact shield. Another held an electroshock weapon close to the body.

Mercer’s face was uncovered. A strip of medical tape still crossed his wrist.

“Stop where you are,” he called. “Set down the case and move away from the fire zone.”

The service egress door to Julie’s right had released. Green floor lights ran through it toward a rising concrete ramp. Above the main corridor, a smoke-control partition was descending between the gate approach and Mercer’s team.

Mercer saw her look.

“Do not enter the service corridor.”

White condensation rolled along the floor behind them. It spilled from the gate in a low expanding cloud, carrying knife-cold air and the last trace of burned polymer.

The containment team gave ground by procedure, not fear. The shield moved left to preserve a clear line. Mercer kept the rifles behind it and out of the agent cloud.

The descending partition passed shoulder height.

Julie picked up the case with her left hand.

Mercer moved.

He came under the partition before it dropped below his chest, leaving the shield team on the far side. He did not reach for Julie. He reached for the aluminum handle.

His taped hand closed over it above hers.

Julie’s right wrist could not help. She turned her body, keeping the handle locked in her left fist, but Mercer had the better angle.

Marcus drove his shoulder into the edge of the descending partition.

The safety strip detected the obstruction and stopped the motor. The impact folded him around his ribs, but it also forced Mercer to choose between the case and the steel lowering across his back.

“Clear the partition,” one of his operators warned.

Marcus shoved again, not at Mercer, but at the flexible lower edge. The partition rose six inches to release the obstruction.

Elias pulled the service door wide.

Julie stepped backward through it with the case.

Mercer held on until the agent cloud reached his boots and the partition began descending again. He released the handle rather than let the closing barrier trap his arm.

Marcus came through last. The partition struck the floor between him and Mercer with a heavy fire-rated seal.

The containment team did not batter it blindly. Julie heard Mercer ordering a parallel route before the alarm swallowed his voice.

The service door closed behind them.

A protected egress corridor rose ahead in a long concrete grade. White strobes flashed above green floor arrows. The public-address system alternated between two commands.

FIRE EMERGENCY. PROCEED TO THE NEAREST MARKED EXIT.

SECURITY EVENT. REMAIN IN DESIGNATED SAFE AREAS.

“The building is arguing with itself,” Elias said.

“Follow the system that has to let us out,” Julie replied.

They moved up the ramp.

The service corridor was pressurized against smoke. Clean air pushed past them toward the lower tier, but the first breaths did not feel clean. Julie’s hands weakened around the case. Elias struck the wall with one shoulder and corrected without stopping. Marcus’s rib injury turned every inhalation into a measured choice.

At the first landing, a fire map showed the route.

PRODUCTION EGRESS
 MECHANICAL BAY TWO
 LOADING DOCK THREE
 SOUTH SERVICE PERIMETER

A red security door blocked the straight path. Its badge reader showed ACCESS SUSPENDED.

The crash bar beneath it glowed green.

Julie hit it with her left forearm. The door released mechanically.

On the other side, the lower mechanical level had entered full evacuation. Exhaust fans started and stopped in different zones. Fire dampers moved inside the walls with hard metallic impacts. Condensation shook from overhead pipes onto the floor, making the painted walkways slick.

Two facilities technicians in gray coveralls came out of a chiller bay carrying emergency respirators. One saw the blood in Marcus’s hair and pointed toward the ramp behind them.

“South dock is the fire exit. Move.”

“We are,” Julie said.

The technician’s eyes dropped to the aluminum case. Before suspicion became a question, a radio on his shoulder called for all mechanical personnel to clear the lower tier. He turned away to answer it.

Julie kept moving.

A camera above the next junction rotated and centered on her face.

She did not look away. Apex already had her image. Hiding it now would cost time without changing the record.

Marcus touched her shoulder. “Parallel corridor.”

Through a wired-glass panel on the left, dark uniforms moved at the same pace behind a fire partition. Mercer’s team had found the alternate route. They remained one wall away, matching the trio toward the loading level.

“Door ahead,” Marcus said.

A motorized smoke barrier began closing across their corridor.

The gap beneath it was still five feet.

Elias tried to run. His injured hip shortened the stride into a lurch.

Julie reached the barrier first and pushed the case under. It slid across the wet floor and struck the wall beyond. She dropped to one knee and followed, protecting her right wrist against her chest.

Elias went next.

Marcus arrived as the gap fell below three feet. His damaged heel caught the raised sill. He hit both hands on the floor and stopped beneath the moving steel.

Julie seized the back of his coveralls with her left hand. Elias caught his sleeve.

They pulled him through as the safety edge brushed his shoulder and reversed long enough to clear the obstruction.

The barrier descended behind his boots.

Marcus remained on his hands and knees.

“Ribs?” Julie asked.

“Still attached.”

“That wasn’t useful the first time.”

“It’s what I have.”

She got one arm beneath his left shoulder. Elias took the other side. Marcus stood between them, heavier than either could manage alone and unwilling to make them carry all of him.

A wall clock above the mechanical-bay doors showed 05:06:41.

The loading level lay one ramp above.

Behind the wired glass, Mercer’s team stopped at a closed fire barrier and split without hesitation. Two reversed toward the cross-passage. The others continued for another intercept.

“They’re going around,” Marcus said.

“We only need one route they don’t reach first.”

The ramp opened into a broad service bay lined with pumps, electrical switchgear, and parked maintenance carts. Building Three’s full alarm was louder here. Employees moved toward the south dock in small groups, badges visible, coats pulled over work clothes. No one knew which emergency to obey. Some looked toward the lower tier. Others followed the white strobes toward daylight.

A campus emergency coordinator stood at the junction waving people toward the dock.

“Out through South Service. Do not stop for vehicles or personal property.”

Julie angled the case so her body hid most of it. Marcus kept his bloody side away from the coordinator. Elias lowered his head and moved with the evacuees.

For twenty yards, they were three injured people leaving a fire.

Then the coordinator’s radio spoke Julie’s name.

He looked up.

Marcus caught the change first. “Right.”

They broke from the evacuation line into Loading Dock Three.

The dock doors were open in emergency position. Predawn air moved through the bay, wet and cold. Beyond the concrete apron, service roads curved between utility buildings and the southern fence. White fire strobes reflected from puddles left by the night rain.

A row of contractor vehicles stood along the dock wall: two utility pickups, a refrigeration truck, and a white maintenance van marked POTOMAC CRITICAL FACILITIES. The van faced the open service lane. Its rear doors were shut. A temporary Apex access placard remained fixed inside the windshield.

A red cabinet beside the dispatch board displayed:

EMERGENCY VEHICLE RELOCATION KEYS
 MAGNETIC LOCK RELEASED ON FIRE ALARM

Julie opened it.

Each key hung beneath a vehicle number and contractor name. She took the one tagged PCF-27.

Marcus looked at the van. “Leg won’t work the pedals.”

“I’m driving.”

“Your wrist?”

“Steering wheel is larger than a gate latch.”

A security door opened at the far end of the dock.

Mercer’s second team entered in controlled formation, six people spread around parked equipment rather than clustered in the doorway. No one fired across the evacuating workers.

“Vehicle,” Julie said.

Elias opened the side door. Marcus lifted the aluminum case with both forearms and pushed it into the cargo bay. The effort drew a sound from him that he tried to turn into a cough.

Julie caught the case before it slid and wedged it behind the passenger seat. Its small display remained green.

CAPTURE SEALED
 LOCAL MEDIA INTACT

Elias climbed in beside it.

Marcus took the front passenger seat, using the door frame to lower himself without bending his ribs. Julie slid behind the wheel and started the engine.

The dashboard came alive with a contractor fleet screen.

APEX CAMPUS TELEMATICS ACTIVE
 VEHICLE PCF-27
 SOUTH SERVICE ROUTE AUTHORIZED

“Tracked,” Marcus said.

Elias leaned forward from the cargo bay. “The module is behind the passenger kick panel. Apex requires the same installation on every contractor vehicle with lower-tier access.”

Marcus looked down at his right leg. “Of course they do.”

“Can you remove it?” Julie asked.

“I can identify it. Someone else has to pull the panel.”

Marcus hooked his fingers beneath the plastic trim. The first pull did nothing. He braced his good boot against the floor and tore the panel free with both hands.

A black module the size of a deck of cards sat above the fuse block, secured by a steel clip and one locking connector.

The first containment operator reached the dock lane and raised a less-lethal launcher.

Julie put the van in gear.

A foam baton struck the rear door with a flat metallic blow.

She drove through the open dock entrance.

The service apron curved right toward the south utility gate. Evacuating contractor vehicles moved in the same direction ahead of them, guided by flashing arrows. Behind, the containment team spread into two security vehicles rather than chasing on foot.

“Connector has a center tab,” Elias said. He had climbed between the seats and was bent over the open passenger footwell. “Gray lock first. Then black release.”

His cut finger slipped off the gray tab.

Marcus reached down but could not fold far enough without his ribs stopping him.

“Tool,” Elias said.

Julie opened the small dashboard compartment with her left hand while steering with her right forearm. A tire gauge, registration sleeve, and flat service screwdriver slid onto the console.

Marcus passed the screwdriver back.

Elias used its tip to lift the gray lock. His hands were still shaking. The module’s status light blinked green every two seconds, transmitting their location through the campus fleet network.

“Not yet,” Julie said.

Elias looked up. “What?”

“Wait until we know the gate mode. If the placard and transponder are opening the service perimeter, killing it here may stop us inside.”

He withdrew the screwdriver but kept one hand on the connector.

The fleet screen flashed.

SECURITY BULLETIN — PRIORITY

A photograph appeared from Julie’s temporary consultant record.

JULIE M. O’DONNELL
 ARMED INSIDER THREAT
 SUSPECTED SABOTAGE OF NATIONAL-SECURITY INFRASTRUCTURE

Below it:

SUBJECT BELIEVED TO HAVE INITIATED MALICIOUS ARGUS SOURCE CORRUPTION AND DELIBERATE FIRE IN BUILDING THREE.

MARCUS L. REED — ARMED ACCOMPLICE
 ELIAS M. THORNE — POSSIBLE HOSTAGE
 RECOVER STOLEN ALUMINUM TELEMETRY CASE

Elias stared at the word HOSTAGE.

The black audit cartridge pressed a square outline against his shirt beside the circuit board that proved he had authenticated every choice himself.

“They made me the victim,” he said.

“For now,” Marcus replied. “Later they’ll use the same record to call you the attacker.”

Julie kept her eyes on the service road.

Six years earlier, the machine had been wrong and the surviving timeline had made her late. This time she had stopped the weapon, preserved the source record, and carried the evidence out in a government case.

The first official account said she had put the lie into Argus herself.

Ahead, the south utility gate stood open beneath white emergency lights. Its inbound lane was reserved for fire response. The outbound barrier remained raised while two contractor pickups cleared the campus. A security officer at the booth was lowering a manual swing arm across the lane beyond them.

“Gate is closing,” Marcus said.

The first pickup passed.

The second accelerated through as the swing arm began moving.

Julie pressed the accelerator.

Her right wrist objected when the service road bent left. She trapped the steering wheel between her forearm and palm, pulled with her left hand, and kept the van inside the narrow lane.

A county engine entered the separated inbound fire lane, lights reflecting across the wet pavement. Campus security could not block that lane. They could block Julie’s.

The officer at the booth saw the fleet bulletin on his terminal and stepped into the outbound lane with one hand raised.

Marcus lowered his window.

“Clear the fire route!” he shouted with the authority of a man who still sounded like a colonel even while bleeding through borrowed coveralls.

The officer hesitated long enough to look at the smoke alarms flashing across Building Three.

Julie did not wait for him to decide.

The swing arm had crossed three quarters of the lane. She steered toward its flexible outer section and struck it at little more than walking speed.

The arm folded against the windshield pillar, scraped along the roof, and snapped free of its pivot. The officer jumped back.

The van cleared the gate at 05:14:36.

Behind them, a security vehicle reached the utility road. Outbound contractor traffic and the broken swing arm stopped it short of the lane.

“Now,” Julie said.

Elias drove the screwdriver beneath the black release and pulled.

The locking connector came free.

The fleet module’s green light died. The dashboard reported FLEET COMMUNICATIONS FAULT, then the contractor route map vanished.

A service radio mounted beneath the dash remained alive, still switched to the county fire-interoperability channel. A dispatcher’s voice broke through the fire traffic.

“All units, intelligence bulletin follows. Primary subject Julie O’Donnell, former Army intelligence officer, considered armed. Suspected deliberate sabotage at Apex Building Three and theft of classified national-security material. Associated white contractor van, Virginia commercial registration—”

Marcus reached for the radio control, then stopped.

Turning it off would not stop the bulletin. It would only make them deaf to it.

The utility road carried them away from the campus into the wet pre-dawn darkness. Building Three’s strobes receded in the mirrors. The aluminum case remained wedged behind Julie’s seat. Elias held the signed recovery cartridge against his chest. Marcus watched the security vehicle clear the gate behind them.

“They have the plate,” he said. “Every camera from the dock to the fence. Pursuit will be on us as soon as they clear the fire lane.”

Julie guided the van onto the first public road with her left hand fixed at the top of the wheel.

No one asked where they were going yet.

Elias pulled the dead transponder module from its clip and held it in his shaking hand while the radio repeated Julie’s name.
