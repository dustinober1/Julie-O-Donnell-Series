# Chapter 16 — The Hold Order

October 13
08:18 Eastern Daylight Time

Secure MPD Evidence Intake
Washington, D.C.

The first federal demand reached Ortiz without a federal name attached to it.

Julie heard the request through the open rear door of the marked transport. Her left wrist remained double-locked to the front belt. The padded restraint held her braced right forearm across her body. Beyond the door, the MPD evidence vehicle waited inside the intake cage with the common chest still bolted to its deck.

A Defense Department recovery desk wanted three packages removed immediately: the aluminum case, Elias’s board, and the WSS-4 module. Different cleared teams would receive them at different facilities. Apex representatives would assist with classification.

One incident would become three before anyone had accepted all seven objects.

Ortiz asked the dispatcher for the receiving officers.

The desk would identify them on arrival.

“Who accepts the current seals?”

That information could not be transmitted over the channel.

“Then they don’t have custody yet.”

The answer did not settle federal authority. It kept an unnamed request from becoming a physical fact.

The evidence vehicle moved through the first cage and stopped while both gates closed behind it. Park stood beside the chest with her camera centered on the transport seal. The driver kept his hands above the wheel. No one released the chest from its anchor.

Julie watched through two fences and a strip of yellow floor paint.

Ortiz came to the transport door. “Do you know the recovery office?”

“No.”

“You were told to surrender federal material.”

“We surrendered it to you.”

“That wasn’t the question.”

“No. I do not know who is asking.”

He held her gaze. “You understand I may be ordered to transfer it.”

“Yes. Make them own the order.”

A second request entered through General Hackett’s office. This one carried names.

Supervisory Special Agent Miriam Alvarez of the Defense Criminal Investigative Service had opened a preliminary inquiry limited to preservation, classification coordination, and independent technical assistance. Special Agent Leila Grant was designated examiner. Hackett requested that MPD maintain the seven packages together until Alvarez could review the intake record.

The request did not authorize an examination. It asked for time to create one.

Alvarez joined by recorded secure voice.

“MPD retains physical custody,” she said. “DCIS is not requesting ownership, opening, or movement of any package today. I am asking for the chest to enter a secure intake room under its current seal while we obtain court, classification, and agency authorities.”

Ortiz looked toward the chest. “Why not examine the board now?”

“Because the board may contain classified hardware identity, because its owner is represented only by an emergency duty lawyer who has not met him, and because the first useful question is narrow enough to survive until tomorrow.”

Julie said, “Physical signing history.”

Alvarez heard her through the open door. “That is the proposed first question. Whether Elias Thorne’s board physically performed the original deployment signature, and whether its later emergency signatures are distinguishable. Nothing else.”

The restraint pressed against Julie’s swollen wrist. She shifted her elbow and stopped when Park looked over.

“Who chose the question?” Ortiz asked.

“Grant proposed it after reading the MPD inventory and Hackett’s assistance request,” Alvarez said. “O’Donnell does not control the examination.”

That mattered. Julie would have asked the same thing. The method no longer depended on her being the only person who could.

Apex counsel objected over a separate channel. The board was company property, the capture case contained restricted systems data, and DCIS lacked jurisdiction over legislative and foreign records tied to the same incident.

Alvarez did not argue the whole case.

“I am not claiming the whole case. I am claiming authority to investigate possible defense-contract fraud, obstruction, and misuse of classified program infrastructure. MPD may keep the other packages closed while jurisdiction is resolved.”

“Your proposed examination cannot be interpreted without Apex architecture.”

“It can answer whether a hardware counter advanced.”

“The counter’s meaning requires vendor testimony.”

“Then preserve that objection.”

Apex counsel did.

Ortiz ordered the chest moved into secure intake as one unit. The transport officer read the anchor release, chest seal, package count, and arrival time into the fixed cameras. Park confirmed no latch event. Two evidence technicians rolled the chest through the inner gate without opening it.

Julie remained in the vehicle until the chest disappeared behind the secure door.

The inability to see it felt like loss even though custody had improved.

A message from Sarah Chen arrived through the joint incident channel. It was not addressed to Julie. It was a source-native preservation notice identifying three Apex records under hold: Vance’s executive recovery order, Sarah’s refusal to authorize deadly force for loss of material, and the later clean-agent suppression override under APX-DIR-0019.

The notice also preserved Mercer’s field reports and Sarah’s instruction that no media be transferred without named custody.

Apex counsel objected that Chen lacked authority to characterize contested executive decisions.

Sarah answered in writing rather than debate: she was preserving what she had directly received and issued. Motive and legality remained uncharacterized.

Julie read the summary on Ortiz’s printed page.

She had assumed Sarah’s compliance language existed only to protect Apex. Some of it did. The same habit had also preserved the boundary Vance crossed.

Julie said, “I misread her.”

Ortiz folded the page. “You can tell her after counsel clears contact.”

“I’m not asking for contact.”

“Good.”

Medical staff entered the intake bay. Julie’s fingers had cooled inside the cuff, and swelling had increased beneath the temporary brace. The nurse assessed circulation, loosened only the medical support, and documented that the police restraint remained double-locked. Julie was cleared for continued custody only if moved to a monitored hospital room within the hour.

Marcus had already reached the trauma unit. His oxygen saturation had improved but remained low, and imaging was pending. Elias was under guarded observation for the hip injury, dizziness, cold exposure, and the cut on his signing finger. Neither was available for questioning.

Alvarez stated that no substantive interview would occur that day.

Hackett objected. “We have an active foreign incident and a political office already making public claims.”

“We also have injured represented subjects, seven sealed packages, an unresolved classification boundary, foreign military records we do not possess, and a congressional device outside executive custody,” Alvarez said. “Speed does not make those problems smaller.”

“What happens overnight?”

“MPD keeps the chest sealed. DCIS obtains a written examination order and a classified handling plan. DIA identifies Price’s status and preserves his authentic request record. Legislative Secure Services is asked to locate SSO-NS-004 without powering or challenging it. Hartwell maintains the event hold. Apex preserves its source-native logs. India is asked through military channels to preserve, not transmit, the K-17 and firing records.”

“And if someone destroys something?”

“Then we will know which preservation demand they violated.”

Hackett did not like the answer. He accepted it because it named the cost of moving faster.

At 08:41, Ortiz entered the secure room and read the chest state through the fixed camera.

Seven packages. Seven seals. Common transport seal intact. No connection. No opening.

He returned without the key in his hand.

“Property control has it under dual access,” he told Julie. “My name remains on the intake. Park’s name remains on the witness record. No package leaves without both the release authority and a new receipt.”

Julie looked at the closed secure door. “And the first examination?”

“Tomorrow, if the paperwork exists.”

The word should have felt intolerable after a morning ruled by seconds.

Instead it felt like a correction.

The immediate weapons clock had ended. The evidence did not become more honest because everyone kept behaving as though another countdown justified skipping ownership.

A hospital transport team brought a wheelchair. Park unlocked Julie from the vehicle belt only after the chair’s medical restraint was ready and the transfer camera was recording. Julie signed the movement acknowledgment with her left hand. The letters leaned backward.

Before the elevator doors closed, the intake monitor changed from provisional to held.

INCIDENT 187463
SEVEN PACKAGES — COMMON CUSTODY
OPENING AUTHORITY: PENDING
FIRST EXAMINATION: NO EARLIER THAN OCTOBER 14

The investigation had finally acquired something the crisis never had.

A night in which no one was permitted to turn urgency into permission.
