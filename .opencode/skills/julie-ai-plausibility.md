# Skill: Julie AI Plausibility

## Purpose

Keep the Julie O'Donnell series technically credible while remaining accessible to mainstream thriller readers.

Use this skill whenever a chapter, book plan, clue, antagonist scheme, or reveal involves AI, machine learning, data pipelines, cyber operations, automation, analytics, targeting, surveillance, risk scoring, or institutional decision systems.

## Core Rule

The AI is not magic. The danger comes from interaction among data, models, incentives, contractors, access, authority, automation bias, and human self-protection.

## Allowed Threat Patterns

Use or adapt threat patterns such as:

- data poisoning;
- provenance laundering;
- feedback-loop escalation;
- confidence inflation;
- self-corroborating evidence;
- model drift;
- brittle proxy variables;
- hidden objective mismatch;
- adversarial inputs;
- vendor-supplied enrichment abuse;
- automation bias;
- stale model assumptions;
- risk-score overreach;
- synthetic evidence injection;
- audit-log suppression;
- whitelisted integration abuse;
- human-in-the-loop rubber-stamping.

## Banned Shortcuts

Do not rely on:

- sentient AI as the villain;
- one-key universal hacking;
- impossible decryption on demand;
- omniscient surveillance without infrastructure;
- perfect predictive models;
- dashboards that reveal the whole plot;
- jargon as a substitute for causal mechanism;
- "the algorithm decided" without explaining who designed, tuned, deployed, trusted, or exploited it.

## Reader Comprehension Standard

A nontechnical reader should understand:

- what the system thinks is true;
- why that belief is wrong;
- who benefits from the wrong belief;
- what evidence would correct it;
- why the institution resists correction.

A technical reader should not feel the story depends on impossible magic.

## Reveal Ladder

Stage the technical mystery in layers:

1. anomalous output;
2. repeated pattern;
3. source/provenance question;
4. incentive or access clue;
5. mechanism hypothesis;
6. proof under pressure;
7. human accountability.

## Julie's Technical Voice

Julie should explain systems through pressure and consequence, not lectures.

She should ask questions like:

- Who supplied that packet?
- Why does the confidence rise when independent sources disappear?
- What does the system reward?
- Who can touch that endpoint without tripping alarms?
- What would the model ignore because it was trained to see that as normal?
- Who benefits if uncertainty gets converted into certainty?

## Book 1 Mechanism Guardrails

Book 1 uses continuity premium, vendor-enriched provenance, normalization hiding self-corroboration, whitelisted integration endpoint abuse, vendor relay/log purge, and confidence collapse after contaminated packets are isolated.

Do not replace this with a louder but weaker "hack." The exploit should feel subtle, plausible, and institutionally protected.

## Output Requirements

When auditing a chapter, report:

- plausible elements;
- implausible or overdramatic elements;
- missing causal links;
- suggested fixes that preserve reader clarity;
- whether the exploit has become too obvious or too obscure.
