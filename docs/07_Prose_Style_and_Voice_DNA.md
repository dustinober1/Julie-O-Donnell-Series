# 07 — Prose Style and Voice DNA

## Purpose

This document defines the prose target so drafting agents do not create generic AI-thriller sludge. The series should sound grounded, tense, specific, and commercially readable.

## Style target

A modern intelligence thriller with military pressure, institutional dread, and clean prose. The writing should be sharp and cinematic without becoming overwritten.

## Reader experience

The reader should feel:

- pressure in every scene;
- trust in Julie's expertise;
- unease around clean dashboards and confident officials;
- frustration with institutions that confuse speed with truth;
- emotional investment in Julie's damaged but disciplined interior life.

## Prose principles

### 1. Specific beats beat abstract emotion

Weak:

> Julie felt traumatized by the terrible memories.

Better:

> Julie checked the fence wire twice before she realized she was counting seconds between radio bursts that were not there.

### 2. Technical detail must create pressure

Weak:

> The system used complex machine learning to analyze large volumes of intelligence data.

Better:

> The confidence score had climbed twelve points without a new source entering the chain.

### 3. Institutions speak in polished evasions

Officials and contractors should not monologue like villains. They should use procedural, legal, and risk-management language that sounds reasonable while narrowing options.

### 4. Julie cuts through euphemism

Julie should often translate abstract language into consequences.

Example:

> "You're not asking whether it's true," Julie said. "You're asking whether it is safe to call it true before the window closes."

### 5. Sensory detail should be selective

Use vivid details at moments of pressure. Do not decorate every room.

The prologue's sensory lane is strong: ozone, hot canvas, sweat, monitor glow, dust, fatigue, rotor/package pressure, voices too loud in enclosed space.

The Culpeper lane should contrast: damp wood, fence wire, horse breath, gravel, old boards, quiet routines, weather.

## Julie voice

Julie is:

- precise;
- dry;
- direct;
- controlled until pushed;
- allergic to bureaucratic abstraction;
- more likely to say the hard thing than the comforting thing.

She should not sound like a quippy superhero. Her humor, when present, should be dry and pressure-born.

## Julie interiority

Write Julie's interior life through practical cognition:

- what she notices;
- what she refuses to notice;
- what her body remembers before she names it;
- how she compares current pressure to prior command pressure;
- how she tests her own fear against evidence.

Avoid long trauma explanation. Let behavior reveal it.

## Dialogue style

### Good dialogue traits

- Subtext under procedural language.
- Short lines during pressure.
- Longer explanations only when the reader needs a technical concept.
- Characters defend their incentives without admitting them.
- Julie asks questions that expose assumptions.

### Avoid

- Characters explaining what they already know.
- AI ethics lectures.
- Villains announcing corruption.
- Analysts talking like press releases.
- Overuse of acronyms without human stakes.

## Scene construction rhythm

A strong scene should include:

1. Clear location and pressure.
2. Julie's immediate objective.
3. A system/institution/person blocking her.
4. A concrete clue, contradiction, or escalation.
5. A cost for pushing.
6. A hook into the next scene.

## Chapter endings

End chapters on:

- new information;
- a forced choice;
- loss of access;
- a deadline tightening;
- a personal wound reopened;
- a human decision hidden behind system language;
- a contradiction Julie cannot ignore.

Do not end too many chapters with vague dread.

## AI-writing indicators to avoid

- "a chill ran down her spine" overuse;
- "little did she know";
- repeated throat-tightening and jaw-clenching;
- generic "the stakes had never been higher" language;
- exposition disguised as dialogue;
- obvious rhetorical questions;
- scenes summarizing conflict instead of dramatizing it;
- characters saying the theme out loud too cleanly;
- invented tech phrases that do not affect plot.

## Description palette

### Prologue / FOB

- hot canvas
- ozone
- dust
- stale coffee
- monitor glow
- sweat under body armor
- rotor thump / distant aircraft pressure
- radio fragments
- time stamps
- red eyes from no sleep

### Culpeper / farmhouse

- split rails
- fence staples
- damp grass
- horse breath
- feed buckets
- gravel drive
- cold kitchen light
- old barn wood
- mud on boots
- wind through empty pasture

### Government / contractor spaces

- glass walls
- secure badges
- conference-room coffee
- silent screens
- polished tables
- legal pads with nothing useful written down
- dashboards too clean to trust
- people speaking as if verbs create accountability

## Example micro-style

Use this only as a tonal sample, not canon prose:

> The dashboard looked clean. That was the first problem.
>
> Real intelligence arrived limping. It contradicted itself. It dragged bad timestamps and missing context behind it like broken wire. This feed marched in a straight line, every packet exactly where it needed to be, every confidence bump arriving on cue.
>
> Julie stared at the sequence until the old heat gathered behind her ribs.
>
> "No," she said.

## Revision checklist

Before accepting a prose chapter:

- Remove generic thriller cliches.
- Replace abstract fear with specific behavior.
- Cut redundant internal statements.
- Verify every technical explanation has a story consequence.
- Make every authority figure sound like their role, not like the author.
- Make Julie's competence visible on the page.
- Ensure the chapter ends with propulsion.
