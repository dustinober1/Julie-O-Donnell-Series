DIA Administrative Review Unit
Joint Base Anacostia-Bolling
11:43 Eastern Daylight Time

Leland Price had spent most of the morning in a room designed to contain neither secrets nor people, only the movement between them.

The table was fixed to the floor. The terminal had no network connection until the records custodian enabled one source at a time. A camera covered the door, the table, and his hands. His counsel, Rina Shah, sat to his left with a yellow pad she had not written on. Across from them, a DIA administrative-review attorney occupied the only chair with a clear view of the wall clock.

Price’s credentials had been suspended for seventeen hours and thirty-nine minutes. He knew because the notice had given him nothing else precise enough to trust.

The DCIS interviewer appeared on the terminal and identified herself as Leila Grant. She did not tell him Julie O’Donnell had demanded his original. She did not describe the evidence recovered from Apex, the physical signer, Hartwell, or K-17. She stated that a later continuity request carried his name and referenced his DIA source-audit request.

Price felt the first response in his stomach and kept it out of his voice.

“What later request?”

Grant did not answer the question he was not entitled to ask. “Did you submit, authorize, or direct any continuity request after your active access ended?”

“No.”

Shah raised one hand. “We will authenticate his original conduct and the access chronology. We are not stipulating to the accuracy or lawfulness of a later office record we have not received.”

“Understood,” Grant said.

The custodian placed DIA-SAR-PRICE-01 on the read-only terminal. Price had written the subject line the previous afternoon after Argus displayed one hundred percent confidence for fourteen seconds and then replaced the value with ninety-nine-point-eight. The correction had not changed the underlying state. It had changed only what ordinary users could see.

His request had asked DIA systems audit to preserve the transient state and permit read-only review of the raw source packets, source-integrity flags, calibration event, model-ingestion lineage, and display correction. It prohibited downstream operational use of any retained audit copy. It contained no assignment of a signer, no continuity route, and no authority outside the source-review process.

Price remembered the moment he had selected the expedited flag. The program liaison had told him the display correction was a calibration artifact and that a coordinated review would follow. Price had watched the underlying state begin to age out of the ordinary audit view while the liaison spoke. He had chosen preservation before permission, not because he knew a conspiracy existed, but because an ephemeral system state could become an argument no one could reproduce.

That choice had been professionally reckless enough without anyone improving it for him.

The review notice on the table used the phrase unauthorized persistence attempt. Price thought of his single rejected access request, the red denial field, and the way two security officers had appeared at his desk before he could call Reed. The phrase was harsher than his memory and not wholly false. He had tried to reach a state after being told to stop. He had not been entitled to decide that the instruction was illegitimate merely because it frightened him.

The later office request did something else. It took the reason he had crossed that line and converted it into permission for an operation he had never seen.

Price read every line before saying it was his.

Grant asked how he had authenticated it.

“Active DIA audit credential, local workstation session, PIV challenge, and repository receipt.”

The custodian confirmed all four events existed in the source original and that the original had not left DIA custody.

Price’s signature was real because the system had required an act only he could perform inside a session he still controlled. That fact did not protect him from the rest of the record.

The request included an expedited-retention flag he had invoked after the program liaison told him to wait for a coordinated review. The flag existed for imminent source loss. Price had used it because the one-hundred-percent state was already disappearing from ordinary views. His audit role allowed him to request preservation. It did not clearly allow him to override the normal coordination sequence.

“That is the conduct under review?” Grant asked.

“One part of it,” the administrative attorney said. “Mr. Price also attempted to reopen the transient-state cache after the program office placed the item under security review.”

Price looked at Shah.

She gave him the smallest nod.

“I attempted read-only access once,” he said. “The repository rejected it. I did not alter a source, export material, or assign an operational action.”

“You knew the security hold had been entered?”

“I knew the program office said the item was under review. The formal suspension notice arrived later.”

The distinction might save nothing. Price left it in the record anyway.

The custodian produced DIA-AR-PRICE-01. Its clock was independent of the office workflow clock and carried its own synchronization statement. The events did not depend on subsecond comparison.

Price’s last active audit session closed at 17:58:09 the previous day.

His classified credential authority was suspended at 18:03:42.

His extension was disabled at 18:04:01.

The later office request had been created at 06:41:18 that morning.

Grant displayed only the fields required for the comparison. Price saw his name, his office, and DIA-SAR-PRICE-01 attached to a record class he had never used.

NSB-EMERGENCY CONTINUITY REQUEST.

He stared at it long enough for Shah to touch the edge of his sleeve.

“Did you create that record?” Grant asked.

“No.”

“Did you assign SSO-NS-004?”

“No.”

“Did your original request name WSS-4, Hartwell, K-17, Phase B, or source reconstruction?”

“No.”

“Did you ask another person or automated service to add those fields after your access ended?”

“No.”

Shah stopped the questioning there. Price had authenticated what he had done and denied a defined set of acts. He had not waived unrelated personnel issues, speculated about a constructor, or accepted anyone else’s theory.

Grant asked whether he would sign a voluntary statement attaching the complete original request and the suspension chronology, including his expedited-retention flag and rejected access attempt.

Price understood the choice. If he refused, the later record would continue using his name while the only authenticated version of his conduct remained inside a personnel system built to judge him. If he agreed, he preserved the limit that separated his request from the operational route—and gave DIA a clean record of the rule he had knowingly pressed.

He looked at the camera rather than Grant.

“Include the rejected access attempt.”

Shah turned toward him. “Leland.”

“They’ll use it whether I include it or not.” His mouth had gone dry. “I want the sequence complete.”

At 11:48:26, Price authenticated the bounded statement with a new voluntary review-session receipt. He did not ask to speak to Julie O’Donnell. He did not thank her. He did not offer to help.

He placed his real request where the borrowed one could no longer replace it.
