A classified enclave could disappear from a public map. Its heat could not.

Apex Security Command Center
 04:53 Eastern Daylight Time

Sarah Chen watched the lower-tier diagram lose detail one system at a time.

Camera feeds had frozen when the production quarantine severed the network bridges. Badge readers no longer reported identities. The map still showed life-safety states, air pressure, fire partitions, and the crude red outline of an environmental fault moving through Building Three’s lowest level.

The fault at Shutter L3-7 had been real.

Its closure time exceeded nominal by 1.8 seconds, consistent with an obstruction beneath the lower seal. The local beam counter registered three crossings before the shutter locked.

Julie O’Donnell. Marcus Reed. Elias Thorne.

Sarah enlarged the production-tier schematic. The marked approach to the Argus enclave had already entered secure mode. No further door events appeared along it.

Julie had either stopped moving or chosen a route the central system could no longer observe.

Sarah did not believe she had stopped.

“Containment One, enter through the south service stair,” she said. “Containment Two holds the production vestibule. Closed-circuit radios only. Do not connect equipment to the quarantined network.”

The team leader acknowledged with two clicks.

“Objective is to secure the enclave, recover the raw telemetry, and take all three subjects into custody. Preserve the production environment. Weapons remain controlled unless there is an immediate threat to life.”

Professional language. Defensible language. The kind that survived an inquiry because it described a security operation rather than a hunt.

Arthur Vance stood behind her, reflected in the dead camera wall.

“They are not trying to escape,” he said.

Sarah kept her attention on the schematic. “They cannot escape from the lower tier.”

Sarah’s hand stopped above the console.

There.

Cooling Gallery Two
 04:53 Eastern Daylight Time

The passage dropped beneath the main service level and opened onto a steel gantry suspended above the chiller plant.

The chiller plant fell away two stories beneath them. Six cylindrical compressor housings occupied the floor in paired rows, each surrounded by pipes, valves, and vibration mounts. Red strobes revolved along the walls. Their light caught the mist hanging above the machinery and turned it the color of smoke.

A grated bridge crossed the gap to the far platform.

It was moving.

The near end had already withdrawn three feet into the wall. A yellow drive chain pulled the bridge toward a recess on the opposite side, isolating the active machinery from the service corridor. The gap widened another inch while Julie watched.

“Why does a bridge retract during quarantine?” Marcus asked.

“To keep an intruder from reaching both halves of the cooling plant,” Elias said. “And to prevent maintenance staff from crossing while the pressure zones isolate.”

“How long until it’s gone?”

“Less than a minute.”

The bridge still reached their platform, but only by eighteen inches. The joint vibrated each time the drive chain advanced.

Julie gave Marcus the telemetry case and stepped onto the grating.

“Wait,” Elias said. “The surface—”

Her boot slipped half an inch on clear condensate.

Julie lowered her center of gravity and moved along the handrail. Cold air rose through the grating. Beneath her, a compressor shaft turned inside a screened housing, its deep rotational hum transmitting through the steel.

The bridge shifted.

The last overlap at the near platform fell to twelve inches.

Julie crossed the midpoint and reached the far side. “Case.”

Marcus held it low and sent it sliding along the wet grating. Julie stopped it with her boot before it struck the drive housing.

Elias stared at the bridge.

“Go,” Marcus said.

“I’m going.”

He put one foot onto the grating, then stopped when the chain clanked.

White light appeared in the cooling passage behind Marcus.

It vanished with the next red strobe.

Then returned lower, split into two disciplined beams.

Marcus looked back once. “Team’s in the corridor. Four, maybe six. Tight spacing.”

No shouted commands. No running. The containment unit knew the bridge was retracting and understood that speed mattered less than closing every alternative.

Elias stepped onto the grating.

The bridge withdrew another three inches.

He moved too upright, one hand locked around the rail. At the midpoint his shoe struck a wet patch. His feet went out from beneath him.

He hit the grating on one hip. His left leg slipped through the rail opening and hung above the machinery.

Julie started back.

Marcus was closer. He stepped onto the remaining overlap, reached across the gap, and caught Elias by the back of his belt.

The bridge moved.

Its near end pulled free of the platform.

For a second, Marcus stood with one foot on concrete and one on a steel span moving away from him.

He shoved Elias forward.

“Move your hands.”

Elias crawled. Julie caught his forearm and dragged him across the last yards. Marcus jumped as the gap opened beneath him, struck the far rail with his chest, and folded over it.

Julie seized his coveralls. Elias grabbed the back of his arm. Together they pulled him onto the platform.

The bridge disappeared into the opposite wall with a heavy steel crash.

Across the opening, two white weapon lights reached the abandoned platform.

They stopped at the edge.

Marcus remained on one knee for a moment, testing his ribs where the rail had caught him. “They won’t cross that.”

A metallic click sounded on the far side.

Elias looked toward the drive housing. “There’s a maintenance extension.”
