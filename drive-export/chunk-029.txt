Mercer’s team took another step.

Elias looked at the case between Julie’s boots. Then at Marcus, bent around his damaged ribs beside an empty extinguisher, waiting for men with armor and weapons to reach him.

His hand shook hard enough that the tip of his finger moved in a small circle above the glass.

He pressed CONFIRM.

The panel gave a low two-note tone.

EMERGENCY ACCESS COMMITTED
 AUDIT SEAL WRITTEN
 GATE RELEASE SEQUENCE ACTIVE

The first locking bolt retracted inside the wall with a metallic impact that Julie felt through the floor.

Then the second.

Then the third.

Six bolts withdrew from alternating sides of the frame, each separated by a deliberate pause. The gate did not move.

“Why isn’t it opening?” Marcus asked.

“Independent power check,” Elias said.

The panel advanced.

LOCK ARRAY CLEAR
 ACTUATOR POWER CONFIRMED
 PRESSURE EQUALIZATION ACTIVE

A vent opened above the gate. Cold dry air moved outward across Julie’s face, carrying the faint smell of metal and filtered dust. On the panel, the pressure difference fell one number at a time.

42 Pa.

The shield came forward.

Marcus planted his good boot behind the empty extinguisher and waited.

“Reed,” Mercer said. “Last chance.”

Marcus looked at the shield. “You’ve said that twice.”

The lead operator drove forward.

The extinguisher rolled beneath the shield’s lower edge. It did not knock the operator down. It forced him to shorten his stride and lift the shield just enough to clear the cylinder.

Marcus moved during that adjustment.

He caught the shield’s left rim with both hands and drove it into the inside wall of the corridor. The operator behind it absorbed the impact and kept his feet. Mercer stepped in from the right, using his shoulder to square the shield again.

The electroshock weapon appeared over the opposite edge.

“Left high,” Julie called.

Marcus released the shield and struck the operator’s wrist with his forearm. The cartridge discharged into the armored gate frame. Two barbed probes snapped against the steel and hung there on silver wires, sparking uselessly.

Marcus backed two steps before the shield hit him.

Its upper edge caught the ribs he had bruised on the bridge.

He folded around the impact. The lead operator kept pressure on him, using the shield as a moving wall rather than trying to strike past it. Mercer reached around the edge for Marcus’s right arm.

Marcus turned with the grip instead of fighting it. He pulled Mercer half a step across the shield operator’s path and drove his own shoulder into the shield’s side.

For a moment, all three men occupied the same narrow space.

That was all Marcus needed.

He dropped his weight, twisted free, and retreated behind the armored gate frame.

He did not come away clean. A baton struck the outside of his right thigh as he moved. His torn boot slipped when the heel folded under him. He hit one knee beside Julie, one hand pressed hard against his ribs.

The team recovered its formation immediately.

No one chased him alone.

On the panel, the pressure difference reached zero.

A heavy motor engaged behind the gate.

The two steel slabs shifted backward into their hinge housings by less than an inch. Then they began to pivot inward.

A black seam appeared between them.

Two inches.

Four.

White floor light from inside the enclave cut through the opening.

Too narrow for a hand carrying the case.

Too narrow for a person.

The panel displayed:

ENTRY APERTURE: 102 MM
 FULL SERVICE OPEN: 44 SECONDS

Marcus looked at the team regrouping six feet away.

“We don’t have forty-four.”

“We don’t need full open,” Julie said.

She studied the gate’s movement. Each slab rotated into a recessed arc inside the enclave. Yellow clearance lines glowed along the threshold. A second line on the panel showed the minimum safe aperture for personnel passage.

560 MM.

The gate would reach it in twenty-one seconds.

Mercer saw the opening and changed the order.

“Advance to the frame. Control Reed. Do not enter the hinge arcs.”

Professional. Specific. He understood the gate as well as Elias did.

Julie moved the aluminum case to her left side and crouched beside Marcus.

“Can you stand?”

“Yes.”

“That wasn’t the question.”

He put his good foot under him. “I can stand once.”

“Make it the useful one.”

The shield drove toward them again.

Marcus rose into it with his left shoulder against the gate frame. He did not try to stop the shield head-on. He let its edge pass him, caught the operator’s forearm when it appeared, and turned the arm into the steel jamb.

The movement cost him. The shield drove across his chest and pinned him long enough for Mercer to seize the back of his coveralls.

Julie saw the next operator preparing to come around the shield’s left side.

“Two behind,” she said. “Second one low.”

Marcus stamped backward with the boot whose heel still held. His sole struck the lead operator’s shin, breaking the pressure for an instant. He tore free of Mercer’s grip and fell inside the depth of the gate frame.

The shield followed.

Its top corner crossed the yellow clearance line.

A warning sounded, and the gate stopped.

HINGE ARC OBSTRUCTION
 ENTRY CYCLE HOLD

The operator pulled the shield clear. Two seconds later, the slabs resumed opening, but the entire front rank had been forced to stop and reset outside the frame.

Julie understood the yellow lines now. They were not decoration. They marked the volume the armored leaves required to move. The team could control the corridor or enter the frame. It could not do both while carrying the shield.

“Make them choose,” she told Marcus. “Shield or threshold.”

The aperture passed three hundred millimeters.

On the inner face of the right slab, a protected green paddle came into view.

EMERGENCY CYCLE CONTROL

Julie reached through the gap. Pain flared through her injured wrist when the moving steel forced her arm sideways. She got two fingers beneath the cover and flipped it open.

“Close control,” she said. “Once we’re through, I can reverse the cycle.”

“Not before,” Elias warned. “If it senses anyone in the threshold, it holds.”

Julie looked at the shield.

That was the team’s next move. Put equipment or a body across the optical line, and the gate could not seal.

The aperture reached four hundred millimeters.

Mercer abandoned the shield.

He shoved it flat onto the floor instead of letting it block his own team, then stepped over it with the electroshock operator on his shoulder. The rifle stayed back. Chen’s ballistic hold was still in force.

Without the shield, they moved faster.

Marcus met Mercer at the frame. He redirected the first reaching hand, caught the second at the wrist, and used the jamb to keep both men from turning their bodies fully into the fight. There was no room for punches with weight behind them. The struggle became forearms, shoulders, and boots searching for traction on the smooth floor.

Mercer’s taped wrist bent against the frame. He released and drove his other forearm into Marcus’s damaged ribs.

Marcus lost his footing.

The electroshock operator came past Mercer with a fresh cartridge.

Julie stepped in once, keeping the case tight against her left leg.

She caught the operator’s weapon hand with her injured right forearm and drove it down against the top edge of the discarded shield. The cartridge fired. One probe buried itself in the shield’s fabric cover. The other struck the floor and skittered beneath the gate.

Pain flashed from Julie’s thumb to her elbow. Her grip on the operator failed, but she backed away with the case still in her left hand. She had bought Marcus less than a second.

He used it.

Marcus hooked one foot behind the abandoned shield and kicked it sideways. It rotated across the corridor, forcing Mercer and the electroshock operator to step back or be trapped against the frame. The rear team caught them and kept the formation from collapsing.

The gate reached five hundred millimeters.
