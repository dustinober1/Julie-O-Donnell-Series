“I’m arguing package identity. The receipt says one incident, seven separate packages, no technical conclusion.”

Ortiz watched her for a second, then turned toward the evidence vehicle. “They don’t get three.”

The answer did not make him her ally. It made the earlier receipt continue to mean what he had signed.

An intake officer opened the outer cage. The transport vehicle moved forward until both gates could close behind it. Park read the arrival time into her camera. Ortiz read the seven package references in order without describing their contents beyond the receipt language. The chest remained locked while the vehicle anchor, chest seal, and transport record were compared.

Julie stayed inside the marked vehicle.

The next request arrived through Hackett’s hold channel rather than the federal recovery desk. Ortiz put it on speaker because the wording changed what MPD could permit.

A woman identified herself as Supervisory Special Agent Miriam Alvarez, Defense Criminal Investigative Service, Mid-Atlantic Field Office. Her voice carried the flat precision of someone reading from a signed authorization rather than negotiating.

“DCIS has opened a preliminary inquiry limited to preservation and independent technical assistance,” Alvarez said. “Special Agent Leila Grant is designated examiner. Access requested on-site under Metropolitan Police custody. No transfer of ownership or physical custody. Initial scope is one sealed package, MPD-901446, administrator-token board EAT-0881147.”

Ortiz looked at Julie. “You ask for that?”

“I asked for a physical question.”

Alvarez continued. “The written authorization is signed by me at zero-eight-eleven Eastern. General Hackett’s written assistance request and MPD consent are separate required instruments. Agent Grant departed the DCIS Alexandria duty office at zero-eight-zero-three with a serialized standalone kit after the oral designation. She is moving north on Interstate 395. Estimated arrival zero-eight-twenty-eight.”

The times made the route real. Hackett’s office had asked before anyone knew whether DCIS would agree. Alvarez had narrowed the request before Grant reached the building. No examiner had materialized inside the intake bay merely because Hackett wanted one.

Ortiz asked, “Why one package?”

“Because one package may answer one question. If it cannot, the examination stops.”

“What question?”

There was a pause long enough for Julie to hear paper move on the other end.

Alvarez read it.

“Does physical administrator-token board EAT-0881147 contain a secure-element signing event, monotonic-counter transition, or local release record corresponding to the original deployment event attributed to Elias Thorne’s physical token?”

Nothing about innocence. Nothing about Payload 88. Nothing about Sterling, Vance, Tariq, K-17, Phase B, or the three encrypted WSS blocks.

The question was narrower than Julie had wanted when she first saw the board’s history in the cutout. It was also the first question someone outside the trio and Apex had agreed to test.

Ortiz said, “What’s excluded?”

“Every other package. Any live network. Any Apex hardware, software, credential, or observer. Any biometric or private-key challenge. Any operation that changes the board or increments its counter. Any conclusion about a human operator.”

Julie felt the last phrase settle where hope had been trying to become certainty.

Ortiz asked Alvarez to send the signed authority into the MPD incident record. Then he muted the call and looked at Julie.

“That your smaller question?”

“Yes.”

“What does a no answer prove?”

“That this physical board did not perform the attributed signing act in the same way it performed later authenticated acts.”

“And what doesn’t it prove?”

“Who created the attribution. Who used the mirrored identity. Who touched a workstation. Whether Elias committed some other crime. Whether anything in the other six packages is true.”

Ortiz unmuted the call. “Send it.”

While the authorization moved into the incident system, the intake officer completed the chest check. The transport seal matched. The chest opened under two cameras, revealing seven separate MPD packages in their recorded positions. Nothing inside resembled the dramatic evidence Sterling’s broadcasts had described. Brown paper, antistatic packaging, rigid containers, printed labels, seals.

Ordinary materials doing honest work.

Ortiz checked each reference against the intake list. MPD-901441 through MPD-901447. Park confirmed the sequence. The chest closed again.

Apex counsel called before Grant arrived.

The attorney did not demand all seven packages. He demanded only the electronics.

His argument was more developed than the federal recovery request. The administrator board and dual-partition module were Apex-designed classified devices. A valid examination, he said, required Apex-certified tools, current revocation data, proprietary event parsers, and a company observer capable of identifying false interpretations before they entered a criminal record. Separating those electronics from the paper and vehicle material was not fragmentation. It was technical necessity.

Ortiz listened without interrupting.

Julie watched his face rather than the speaker. The argument gave him lawful concerns: classification, contamination, incorrect tooling, an examiner misunderstanding a device built for a specialized environment. Apex did not need every official to be compromised. It needed the legitimate risks to point toward Apex control.

When the attorney finished, Ortiz said, “Who receives physical custody?”

“A cleared federal technical facility can receive—”

“Name the officer.”

“That designation is being coordinated.”

“Then you have a claim, not a transfer.”

“The board cannot be responsibly examined without proprietary support.”

“That’s for the examiner to decide.”

“We require observer access.”

“Send the authority for that requirement.”

The attorney changed nouns. Requirement became request. Custody became protective handling. Fragmentation became technical segregation.

Ortiz recorded each version.

Julie said nothing until the call ended. Then she asked, “Can I see the room?”

“No.”

“Can I see the examiner?”

“When she gets here.”

“That wasn’t my question.”

Ortiz looked toward a glass wall beyond the intake desk. Behind it, a short corridor led to two digital-evidence rooms and a secure interview alcove. One room had a fixed observation window opening into the alcove.

“You stay restrained,” he said. “You stay outside the technical work area. You don’t touch a package, tool, door, or control. Park remains with you.”

Julie tested the six inches of chain at her left wrist. “Move the cuff before I stand.”

Park stepped closer. She checked the right forearm support first, then the swelling above the brace. Under camera, she unlocked the left cuff from the vehicle belt and transferred it to a short front chain on a fixed transport harness. The second cuff remained empty but double-locked to the harness ring, keeping Julie’s left hand forward without forcing the right wrist into ordinary restraint position.

The procedure gave her enough balance to stand.

It gave her no useful freedom.

A medical notice reached the incident desk while Park walked her into the intake alcove. Marcus remained in guarded emergency care on four liters of oxygen, saturation holding between ninety-two and ninety-three percent. Elias remained in separate guarded medical intake, alert but dizzy, with no evidence access and no authorization for questioning.

No messages passed among them.

At 08:25:30, Ortiz placed three documents on the counter outside the examination corridor: Alvarez’s signed preliminary-inquiry and technical-assistance authorization, Hackett’s written request for independent assistance under the preservation hold, and MPD’s written consent to one on-site examination while retaining custody.

The documents agreed on the package.
