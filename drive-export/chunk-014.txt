Julie kept her hands away from Elias and the panel. Six years earlier, men with more authority had demanded that she turn doubt into permission. She would not make the same demand now and call it courage.

“It has to be yours,” she said. “It also has to be now.”

Elias looked at the gate, at the panel, and finally at the thin Apex badge hanging against his shirt.

His fingers missed the clasp on the first attempt.

He caught it on the second and pulled the badge free. The plastic casing had already been opened and pressed back together so many times that one edge no longer sat flush. He worked a thumbnail into the seam. His hands were wet from the ladder and cold enough to tremble against the plastic.

The casing separated.

Inside lay the narrow circuit board he had carried through the lower tier. Copper contacts ran along one end. A dark secure-element package occupied the center beside a thumbnail-sized monochrome window. One corner bore a fresh scrape from the cooling gallery.

Elias turned the board over twice, checking it.

“What?” Julie asked.

“If the secure element cracked when I fell, it won’t release anything.”

“Can you tell?”

“Only by asking it.”

He moved to the wall panel. His left leg dragged slightly over the last step, and he caught himself against the armored frame when the injured hip failed to take his weight cleanly.

The panel’s lower cover had no latch. Elias pressed two recessed points at once. A narrow slot opened beneath the display with the slow precision of a protected instrument.

He aligned the board.

The copper edge struck the slot and slipped aside.

“Damn it.”

He tried again. The tremor in his hand worsened as a third white beam reached the bend and separated from the first two. The containment team was arranging itself below the shaft.

Julie set the aluminum case upright between her boots. She caught Elias’s wrist with her good hand, steadied it against the frame, then released him.

“You put it in,” she said.

He swallowed and guided the contacts home.

The panel changed.

HARDWARE CERTIFICATE DETECTED
 THORNE, ELIAS M.

For one second, relief moved across his face.

Then the next lines appeared.

EMPLOYEE STATUS: REVOKED
 STANDARD ACCESS: DENIED

The slot ejected the board half an inch.

Elias stared at it.

“No,” he said. “That’s the corporate identity.”

“Can you select another?” Julie asked.

“I should be able to. The enclave role is offline. It isn’t supposed to inherit an employment revocation.”

“Supposed to?” Marcus said from the bend.

Elias ignored him. He pushed the board back into the slot and held a contact on its upper edge. The tiny window came alive.

SELECT IDENTITY DOMAIN

Two entries appeared.

APEX EMPLOYEE
 ENCLAVE EMERGENCY ADMINISTRATOR

Elias hesitated over the second.

Julie understood why. The first identity was the person Apex had hired and could fire. The second was authority designed to survive the company, the network, and any later claim that he had acted by mistake.

He selected it.

The panel paused long enough for Julie to hear the controlled scrape of equipment at the corridor bend.

ENCLAVE ROLE RECOGNIZED
 LOCAL QUARANTINE AUTHORITY VALID
 ROTATING PIN REQUIRED

A six-digit field opened on the screen.

The board’s small display showed another six digits and a shrinking bar beneath them.

“Fourteen seconds,” Elias said.

“Enough?”

“It would be if my hand worked.”

He entered the first number. His fingertip struck the edge of the second key and registered the wrong digit.

He cleared the field.

The bar fell below halfway.

A helmet appeared at the bend.

White light flooded the corridor, hard and level. Marcus withdrew one step toward the gate frame. The team did not follow immediately. A compact shield entered first, matte black and narrow enough for the industrial passage. Behind it, Julie saw the taped wrist and dark uniform of Daniel Mercer. A compact rifle remained close to the shoulder of the operator behind him, muzzle held below the line of Elias and the control panel.

Four people were visible. More remained in the shaft approach.

They had arrived with eleven seconds left on Elias’s code.

Mercer’s voice carried clearly through the corridor.

“Thorne, remove your hands from the access panel. O’Donnell, place the aluminum case on the floor and step away from it. Reed, move to the left wall and keep your hands visible.”

Elias’s finger stopped above the fourth digit.

Julie watched the team’s geometry. Shield at the front. Mercer behind its right edge. A second operator over the left shoulder with an electroshock weapon held close to the body. Rifle coverage from the rear, controlled so no muzzle crossed the gate hardware. They had left the bend open behind them for another pair to move up.

They were building a single safe direction and expecting obedience to complete it. The corridor left no room for another outcome.

“Keep entering,” Julie said.

Mercer shifted his attention to her. “Do not complete the authentication.”

“We’re preserving evidence of a contaminated strategic assessment.”

“You are attempting unauthorized entry into a classified production enclave.”

“Then preserve the case and call federal investigators.”

“Place it on the deck.”

“So you can separate it from the witnesses?”

“The case goes down. You move away from Thorne. Reed faces the wall.”

There it was. Three commands, three positions, no chance for them to remain a group.

Julie kept one boot against the case. “No.”

The bar on Elias’s token disappeared.

He pressed the final digit a fraction too late.

PIN WINDOW EXPIRED

The panel cleared the field.

Elias made a small sound that did not become a word.

Mercer raised one hand. The shield advanced a measured step.

“Elias,” Julie said.

“The next code doesn’t generate for six seconds.” He was talking too quickly now. “It prevents overlap. If I pull the token, the emergency role resets, and if the certificate counter increments again the panel may impose a delay.”

“Then leave it in.”

“They’re fifteen feet away.”

“I know.”

Marcus looked back once. “I can give you fifteen feet. I can’t give you a minute.”

“You need forty seconds,” Elias said.

“Then type fast.”

The token display went blank.

Mercer’s team took another step.

“Final instruction,” he said. “Hands away from the panel. Case on the floor. No one will be harmed if you comply now.”

Marcus’s gaze moved to the shield, the weapons behind it, and the five-foot corridor that denied them room to pass one another. Then he looked at the recessed fire cabinet beside the armored gate frame.

Julie saw the same red pictogram beneath its clear cover.

CO2. Approved for energized equipment.

It could not defeat the team. It could give them a few seconds of cold white blindness in a corridor where Chen’s people could not fire without seeing the gate hardware.

“Marcus,” she said. “Left cabinet.”

He nodded once.

The token window lit.

A new six-digit number appeared.

Elias began entering it.

He struck each key with exaggerated care.

Three.

One.

Seven.

Mercer’s shield moved again.

Marcus opened the fire cabinet. The clear cover swung wide without an alarm. He pulled the extinguisher free with both hands, and the weight brought a sharp contraction across his bruised ribs. He stopped for half a second, forehead against the cylinder, then straightened with it braced against his thigh.

Nine.

Four.

The electroshock operator shifted toward the shield’s right edge.

Julie pointed. “Right side.”

Marcus saw the movement and repositioned behind the inside corner of the gate frame, leaving only his left arm and the extinguisher horn exposed.

Two.

Elias pressed ENTER.

PIN ACCEPTED
 LIVE-FINGER CONFIRMATION REQUIRED

A glass pad extended from the panel.

Elias looked at his right hand. Condensation shone across his fingers. A shallow cut near the base of his index finger had opened again.

“Use the other one,” Julie said.

“The key is bound to my right index.”

Mercer gave the signal to advance.

Marcus pulled the extinguisher pin and squeezed the handles.

White vapor struck the front of the shield and rolled back through the corridor. The weapon lights became solid walls of glare. The shield operator stopped instead of walking blind into the gate approach. Mercer called a hold. No one fired.

The extinguisher’s discharge filled the narrow space with a dense, freezing cloud. Marcus swept it low, forcing the team to keep its footing while the carbon dioxide boiled across the floor.

“Now,” Julie said.

Elias pressed his finger to the glass.

The panel displayed a gray outline of his print, then a red warning.

LIVENESS INDETERMINATE
 SURFACE TEMPERATURE BELOW THRESHOLD
 MAINTAIN CONTACT

“It checks capillary response,” Elias said. “Temperature, blood flow, pressure variation. It won’t take a dead print or a cold one.”

“Keep it there.”

“My finger is numb.”

“Then make it not numb.”

He rubbed his hand against his shirt, struck his fingertips into his palm, and tried again. His injured hip pressed against the wall to keep his weight off the left leg. The extinguisher hissed behind Julie. Its pressure began to fall.

The scan outline built from the center outward.

Mercer’s voice came through the cloud. “Reed, put down the cylinder.”

Marcus kept discharging.

The team changed tactics. The shield descended until its lower edge nearly touched the floor, then advanced through the vapor by inches. One operator used the wall as a guide. Another called distances over the closed circuit in clipped numbers.

They did not need to see Marcus. They knew where the gate was.

The biometric outline reached Elias’s first knuckle and stopped.

APPLY STEADY PRESSURE

His finger shifted.

The image broke apart.

“Don’t move,” Julie said.

“I’m trying.”

“Try without talking.”

He stared at the glass, shoulders rigid, and held the finger in place.

The extinguisher coughed.

Marcus released the handles. The last white vapor drifted around him. He set the cylinder lengthwise against the inside corner where the corridor turned into the gate approach. It would not stop the shield. It would make the first operator step around it instead of driving straight through.

The panel chimed.

LIVE BIOMETRIC CONFIRMED
 TOKEN PRIVATE KEY UNLOCKED

The circuit board’s display changed from the rotating digits to a single line.

SIGNING LOCAL CHALLENGE

Elias exhaled through his teeth. “The private key never leaves the token. The panel sends a challenge. The secure element signs it only after the live match.”

Julie watched the white cloud thin. “How long?”

“It should already—”

CHALLENGE RESPONSE VERIFIED

ADMINISTRATOR: THORNE, ELIAS M.

CONFIRM / CANCEL

“Authorize lethal force,” he said.

Sarah kept the channel open. “No.”
