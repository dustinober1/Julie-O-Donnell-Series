Julie looked through the glass at the board.

“The later events are authentic physical-token events.”

“Yes.”

“They support that Elias voluntarily opened the gate and authenticated the reconciliation.”

“They support that this board performed those signing acts through the recorded local release states. The legal meaning of his participation is outside my report.”

“But the acts go in.”

Grant watched her carefully. “Do you want them omitted?”

“No.”

The word came without delay. The cost arrived after it.

The hostile public account gave Elias two shapes: coerced hostage or malicious saboteur. The board rejected the first as a complete explanation of his later conduct. It did not make the second true. It preserved the harder fact that Elias had chosen actions under pressure and attached his identity to them.

He had demanded ownership of those choices in the cutout.

Julie could not protect him by erasing them now.

“Both go in,” she said.

Grant nodded once and turned toward the intake counter.

The Apex call had been joined by a second claimant. A federal security official wanted the examination suspended until the dual-partition module could be opened and compared. Apex counsel argued that without proprietary parsing, the missing original event might reflect version migration, counter abstraction, or an archived identity domain not exposed by the DCIS reader. Hackett’s office asked whether Grant could broaden the observation to say the deployment attribution was invalid.

Every interested party wanted more.

The federal claimant wanted another package.

Apex wanted another tool and an observer.

Hackett wanted a stronger sentence.

Grant wanted a report she could sign.

She took the calls through the recorded intake line with Julie able to hear from the alcove. Ortiz remained in the examination room with the open package. Park stayed beside the board. The other six packages remained in the locked incident chest under the intake officer’s continuous control.

Grant addressed the federal claimant first.

“The module is outside my written scope. Opening it would create a second evidence event and is not necessary to answer the physical-token question.”

“You cannot interpret the board without the associated session record.”

“I can interpret whether this secure element recorded a signing transition.”

“That is incomplete.”

“Yes.”

The official paused.

Grant continued. “Incomplete is not the same as invalid.”

Apex counsel spoke next. “Your reader cannot account for Apex identity-domain virtualization.”

“It does not need to. My observation is limited to physical history on the board.”

“The event may be represented through an archived domain unavailable in generic evidence mode.”

“Then preserve the documentation and validation material establishing that claim.”

“We require an Apex engineer to inspect the tool state.”

“No.”

“The device is proprietary.”

“The package is in MPD custody. Proprietary interest does not grant observer access to an active criminal examination.”

“You risk publishing a false negative.”

“I am not publishing anything. I am recording the absence of a physical event in the protected history exposed through a verified read-only interface, along with the limits of that observation.”

The attorney changed direction. “At minimum, the report must state that the later events demonstrate the token was compromised while in the subjects’ control.”

Julie heard the causal move. A genuine later event converted into an explanation for an earlier absent one.

Grant did too.

“The later events demonstrate later physical use,” she said. “Compromise is not a field on the board.”

Hackett’s representative came last. He did not demand Apex access. He asked for the sentence that would matter inside government.

“Can Agent Grant state that Elias Thorne did not deploy Payload Eighty-Eight?”

Grant looked through the glass at Julie before answering.

“No.”

“Can she state that his physical token was not used?”

“I can state that the examined physical board contains no corresponding private-key signing event, counter transition, or local release record for the deployment attribution preserved on the board. I can state that later physical uses do contain those records. I can state that the original attribution cannot be explained as ordinary physical use of this board in the same manner as the later events.”

“That is going to be called equivocation.”

“It is going to be called accurate.”

The representative asked for language directing immediate seizure of Apex mirror-service and executive-authority records.

“Preservation,” Grant said. “Not seizure. My observation may support a preservation request. It does not establish probable cause by itself, and I am not writing a warrant application from this room.”

Julie felt the old anger rise, not at Grant but at the speed of the public story compared with the care required for one defensible sentence. Sterling had needed minutes to call her the architect of sabotage. Grant required photographs, seals, clean images, controls, repeat reads, and limitations to say the physical board did not contain an event.

Truth moved more slowly because it carried its own weight.

That did not make the weight optional.

Grant returned to her worksheet. She wrote by hand first, then entered the same language on the standalone report system. Park watched the transfer from notes to report. The DCIS log medium remained separate from the board and contained only Grant’s generated logs and screenshots.

At 09:01:14, Grant read the preliminary observation aloud.

“On July thirteenth, under DCIS preliminary-inquiry and technical-assistance authority and with Metropolitan Police consent, I conducted a read-only examination of physical administrator-token board EAT-0881147 removed from MPD package 901446 under recorded seal continuity. The board’s declared evidence interface exposed secure-element identity, protected monotonic signing history, preserved partial deployment-reference fields, and later authenticated event records without private-key operation, biometric input, network access, or write activity.”

She paused at the finding.

“The examined board did not record a physical private-key signing event, corresponding monotonic-counter transition, or local release record for the original deployment attribution preserved on the board. Later emergency gate-access and provenance-reconciliation events did contain matching secure-element transitions and local authenticated records.”

The room remained quiet.

“The original deployment attribution therefore cannot be explained as ordinary physical use of this board in the same manner as the later events.”

Grant continued into the limitations before anyone could turn the sentence into more.

“This preliminary observation does not determine the alternative identity path, identify a human operator, authenticate any unexamined package or external system record, establish the complete deployment history, determine intent, establish guilt or innocence, confer immunity, resolve admissibility, or reach any conclusion regarding Hartwell, WSS-4 message content, K-17, Phase B, Senator Sterling, Arthur Vance, Major Tariq, or field truth. Separately held records require independent preservation and examination.”

Julie listened for Elias’s name in the conclusion.

Grant had used it only where necessary to identify the later recorded acts. The report neither cleared him nor reduced him to the company’s version.

“Add one thing,” Julie said.

Grant looked up. “You don’t edit my report.”

“No. Put my request in the incident record.”

“What request?”

“That no one characterize the later events as contamination merely because they complicate the original attribution. They are part of the result.”

Grant considered it. “That is already implicit.”

“Implicit is where records lose people.”

Ortiz had returned to the alcove door. He heard the sentence.
