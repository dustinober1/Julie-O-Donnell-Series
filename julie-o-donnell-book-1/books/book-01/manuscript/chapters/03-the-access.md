# The Access

She had been reading for an hour.

The raw feed filled the screen in columns that hadn't changed format since Argus Enterprise Build 4.3 — the same layout she had worked in for four years, the same color coding, the same timestamp convention. The machine's interface hadn't changed. The institution's preferences for how data should look hadn't changed. Only the analyst had changed.

Julie worked with the pencil and the pad Copley had allowed through the door — he had inspected both, confirmed they were not electronic, and logged them. She wrote in block letters, not her usual hand, because block letters were harder to attribute. She didn't know whether Copley was literate enough to read her notes. She didn't intend to find out.

The raw signal confirmed what the printout had suggested. Three independent indicators — interval scatter, noise-floor stability, bearing drift — all showed the same synthetic pattern. The processing artifacts were identical at every pipeline stage. The data had never been collected. It had been manufactured.

She had written it down. CONFIRMED: SYNTHETIC PATTERN. CLONED ARTIFACTS AT ALL STAGES. NO SOURCE VARIATION. Then she had crossed it out and written it again in block letters, because the first version was in her own hand and the second version was not, and the difference mattered when every page she wrote would be logged and possibly photographed before it left the building.

She needed more. The raw feed proved the pattern was synthetic. It did not prove where the pattern had entered the pipeline. To do that she needed the collection metadata — the records of which antenna had received the signal, which satellite had relayed it, which ground station had processed it. She needed the pipeline logs: the intermediate stages between collection and confidence scoring, including the source-diversity analysis that Argus used to confirm a track was not a replay. She needed the confidence-score audit trail — the specific chain of automated approvals that had moved the track from collection to validated to one-hundred-percent confidence without a human checkpoint.

The console had the raw feed. It did not have the pipeline.

She looked at the screen. The data sat there in its columns, patient and incriminating, offering her the proof that the feed was synthetic and withholding the proof of who had made it synthetic and how it had entered the system and whether it could be stopped. The difference between those two things — knowing the feed was a lie and knowing how to prove it to someone who mattered — was the difference between suspicion and evidence, and suspicion had been what the institution had used to destroy her the last time.

She was not going to give them suspicion again.

She set the pencil down and pressed her fingers against her temples. The headache had started thirty minutes ago, behind the eyes, the kind that came from sustained focus in fluorescent light. The room was sixty-eight degrees. The vent hummed at its designed frequency. The air smelled of recycled nothing. She had not eaten since the farm. She was not going to eat until she had something to show for the hunger.

A knock at the door. Two taps. Copley's knock.

"Visitor," Copley said through the door. He did not open it.

"Send them."

The door opened a few inches. Marcus stepped in. Copley closed it behind him and resumed his position in the hallway. Through the gap before the latch caught, Julie could see the corridor — beige, empty, the vinyl tile unmarked by anyone who had reason to linger.

Marcus stood just inside the door. He had taken off his coat. His shirtsleeves were rolled to the elbow. He looked like a man who had been sitting in a waiting room and was now delivering news he had been rehearsing.

"The advisory went out at fourteen-seventeen," he said.

She had been reading for an hour. The advisory was supposed to transfer at sixteen-thirty. It had gone early.

"Why."

"The Indians requested early transfer. They said their operational tempo was accelerating and they needed the advisory window to begin pre-positioning. The validation authority approved the early request."

"The validation authority is the machine."

"The validation authority is the machine's recommendation endorsed by the shift supervisor."

"Endorsed."

"Signed."

Julie looked at the screen. The raw feed sat in its columns, unchanged. The advisory was out. The Indian side had it. They were pre-positioning. The clock was running, and it was running faster than it had been an hour ago.

"What does that change," she said.

"It moves the commit window. If the Indian Northern Command confirms the track before their midnight, the executable support package commits automatically at zero-four-thirty. That's their local. Zero-four-thirty Pakistan standard time, which is eleven-thirty our time tonight."

She had come here thinking she had until dawn. She had until midnight.

"The package can be recalled after commit," she said. She was not asking. She was testing whether Marcus knew what she knew.

"It can be recalled within the first twelve minutes. After that the flight time makes recall impractical."

"Twelve minutes."

"Twelve minutes. After zero-four-thirty, there's a twelve-minute window where anyone with the recall authority can pull it back."

"Who has recall authority."

"The combatant commander. The Joint Chiefs liaison. The shift supervisor at this facility."

"Not the analyst reading the feed."

"Not the analyst reading the feed."

Julie set the pencil down. The headache pressed behind her eyes. The advisory was out. The Indians were pre-positioning. The commit window was midnight, not dawn. She had less than ten hours to prove that the data behind the advisory was synthetic, and the proof had to reach someone with recall authority before zero-four-thirty Pakistan time, and the room she was sitting in was owned by the institution that had already certified the data at one hundred percent confidence.

"I need the pipeline logs," she said.

"The console has the raw feed."

"The raw feed tells me the pattern is synthetic. It doesn't tell me where the pattern entered the pipeline. I need the collection metadata — antenna IDs, satellite relay chain, ground station processing records. I need the source-diversity analysis that Argus used to confirm the track. I need the confidence-score audit trail. Without those I can write a suspicion note. I can't write evidence."

Marcus looked at the screen. He didn't read it the way she did. He saw columns of numbers. He didn't see the straight line where the ragged line should have been, or the cloned artifacts, or the bearing that held at two-seventeen-point-three for four and a half minutes. He saw data, and data was his business the way fences were hers: he knew it mattered without being able to read it himself.

"The pipeline logs are on a different system," he said. "They're not on this console."

"I know."

"Access to the pipeline system requires a separate authorization."

"I know that too, Marcus."

He met her eyes. She could see him deciding how much of the next part to say. His jaw worked the way it worked when he was calculating risk — not the operational risk he could brief in a chain of command, but the personal risk he could only carry himself.

"I can request pipeline access," he said. "The authorization would go through the same office. Director's approval. It would take time."

"How much time."

"Hours. Maybe less if I push it through the validation authority directly."

"The same validation authority that signed off on the early advisory transfer."

"Yes."

"And if the validation authority asks why I need pipeline access."

"I tell them you're completing the assessment the director authorized."

"And if they ask what the assessment says so far."

Marcus said nothing.

The silence held. The vent hummed. The screen glowed. Julie could hear Copley shifting his weight in the hallway outside the door — not listening, she was almost certain, but present, which was the institution's way of listening without listening.

"They're going to ask," she said. "The moment you request pipeline access for a supervised analyst who was processed out three years ago, someone is going to ask what she's finding. And you're going to have to decide what to tell them."

"I know."

"And if you tell them the truth — that the feed is synthetic, that the confidence score is wrong, that the advisory was sent on fabricated data — then the validation authority has a problem. Because they signed off on the early transfer. Because they endorsed the machine's recommendation. Because the advisory is already out and the Indians are already pre-positioning and the recall window is twelve minutes."

"I know all of this, Julie."

"Then you know what asking for pipeline access means. It means you're telling the building that something is wrong with the data it just certified. And the building is not going to like that."

"The building is not going to like a lot of things."

"Marcus."

He set his jaw. The operational mask was back — the one she had seen a thousand times across a thousand screens and one real room, the mask that said he was managing risk rather than feeling it. But his thumbnail was chewed to the quick, and the thumbnail was the thing he had never been able to train out of himself, and it told her what the mask wouldn't.

"I'll request it," he said. "Direct to the validation authority. I'll frame it as a routine extension of the authorized assessment. I won't say what you've found. Not yet."

"And when they ask."

"When they ask, I'll tell them the assessment is ongoing and the analyst needs the full pipeline to complete it. That's not a lie. That's what the authorization says you're here to do."

"And when they push."

"Then I push back. And if they push harder, we'll deal with it."

She looked at him. He stood in the small room with his rolled sleeves and his chewed thumbnail and his operational mask, and she understood that he was offering her something the institution could not: a person who would lie to the building on her behalf, at personal cost, without knowing whether the lie would hold.

"What does this cost you," she said.

"What does it cost me if I don't do it."

"That's not what I asked."

"It's the answer."

She held his gaze for a long moment. The vent hummed. The screen glowed. The headache pressed. Outside the door, Copley shifted again.

"The assessment I write goes to the director's office," she said. "And then to the validation authority. And then to you. And then somewhere the confidence score doesn't set the agenda."

"Yes."

"What happens if the validation authority suppresses the assessment."

"Then I take it somewhere else."

"You take it somewhere else, or you try to take it somewhere else. There's a difference."

Marcus looked at the screen. At the columns of numbers he couldn't read. At the analyst who could.

"I know the difference," he said. "I'm telling you I'll try."

The word try was the most honest thing he had said. Not I'll take it somewhere else. Not I have a channel. Not I know a person. Just try. An admission that the institutional machinery might grind his attempt into nothing, and that he was going to attempt it anyway.

Julie picked up the pencil.

"I need something else," she said.

"What."

"I need to know who certified the confidence score. Not the machine. The human being whose name is on the chain of approval between the machine's recommendation and the advisory going out."

Marcus was quiet for a moment. "The shift supervisor at the time of validation was Ellen Poole."

"I don't know Poole."

"Poole is twenty-six years in the building. She signs what the machine recommends because in twenty-six years the machine has never recommended something she couldn't defend."

"The machine recommended a strike based on synthetic data."

"The machine recommended a strike based on data it scored at one hundred percent confidence. Poole signed the validation because the confidence score met the threshold. She didn't check the raw feed. She didn't need to. The system doesn't require it."

"The system doesn't require a human to check the feed before authorizing a strike."

"The system requires a human to confirm the confidence score. The confidence score was one hundred percent. One hundred percent meets the threshold. Poole confirmed the threshold. The advisory went out."

Julie set the pencil down again. She understood now. Not the technical failure — she had understood that on the porch. The institutional failure. The system was not broken. The system was working exactly as designed. A machine scored data at one hundred percent confidence. A human confirmed the score without examining the data. The advisory went out. The Indians began pre-positioning. The executable package would commit at zero-four-thirty Pakistan time, and the recall window was twelve minutes, and the person who could pull it back was the person who had signed the advisory in the first place, and the institution's design was precisely this: a machine that decided and a human who rubber-stamped and no one in the chain who was authorized or inclined or trained to ask whether the machine was lying.

"Poole didn't fail," she said. "The system didn't fail. The system worked. The system is designed to trust the machine, and the machine was wrong, and the system ran exactly the way it was built to run."

Marcus didn't argue. He stood in the room with his rolled sleeves and his chewed thumbnail and let her say it, because she was right and they both knew she was right and neither of them knew what to do about it except what they were already doing.

"I need the pipeline logs," she said again. "I need the collection metadata. I need the source-diversity analysis. I need to trace where the synthetic data entered the system and how it got past the validation checks. Without that I have a suspicion note. With that I have evidence. And evidence is the only thing that survives contact with this building."

"I'll request it."

"How long."

"If I push it through the validation authority directly — two hours. Maybe less."

"I have until midnight."

"You have until midnight if the assessment reaches someone with recall authority before zero-four-thirty."

"The assessment has to reach someone with recall authority. It has to be read. It has to be believed. It has to survive contact with a chain of command that has already certified the data. And the person who certified it is twenty-six years in the building and has never been wrong before, because the machine has never lied before, and she's not going to believe a processed-out analyst with a pencil and a Virginia driver's license."

"No," Marcus said. "She's not."

"So the assessment needs to be more than a suspicion note."

"It needs to be evidence."

"It needs to be evidence that can survive the first five minutes in the director's office."

Marcus looked at her. She could see him understanding the full scope of what she was asking. Not just pipeline access. Not just time. A path from the console room to the recall authority that didn't pass through the people who had already decided the machine was right.

"I'll get you the pipeline," he said. "I'll frame the request as routine. I'll buy you the time. But what happens after you have the pipeline — what you find, what you write, where it goes — that's the part I can't control. The building controls the room. The room controls the data. The data controls the clock. You're working inside their system, and every keystroke belongs to them."

"I know."

"Do you."

She looked at the console. The screen. The columns of data. The pencil. The graph paper. The pad of block-letter notes. The room was small and sound-dampened and sixty-eight degrees and owned by the institution that had destroyed her. Every keystroke was logged. Every page she wrote was inspected before it left. Every minute she spent was counted against the forty-eight-hour authorization that the building had designed to expire before it mattered.

She had been here before. The chair was the same. The lighting was the same. The institutional air was the same. The last time she had sat in this chair with a question about a feed, the question had been right and the building had made her wrong, and the cost had been her career and her clearance and three years of exile on a farm she had chosen precisely because the building could not reach it.

She was back in the chair. The question was the same. The feed was different. The stakes were higher.

"I know," she said. "I was here three years ago, Marcus. I know exactly what this room owns."

He held her gaze. Something passed between them — not trust, because trust was a word that required a history they didn't share anymore, and not agreement, because they hadn't agreed on anything yet except that the advisory was out and the clock was running. What passed between them was recognition. He recognized that she knew what she was walking into. She recognized that he knew what he was offering. Both of them knew the cost, and neither of them was pretending otherwise.

"Two hours for the pipeline," he said.

"I'll be here."

He knocked twice on the door. Copley opened it. Marcus stepped out. The door closed.

Julie sat in the chair and looked at the screen. The raw feed waited. The pipeline was coming. The clock was running. The advisory was out. The Indians were pre-positioning. The commit window was midnight. The recall authority was three people she had never met, in a chain of command that had already certified the data, in a building that owned the room and the console and every page she wrote.

She was the only person who had looked at the feed and seen the straight line where the ragged line should have been.

She picked up the pencil.

She went back to reading.
