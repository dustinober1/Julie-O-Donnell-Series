# Book 1 Style Calibration — Julie O’Donnell House Style v2.0

**Status:** Calibration only  
**Source scope:** Accepted Prologue and Chapters 1–12 at repository baseline `66a7aeab21b32dcf64d90ce313dd9c710ca311c3`  
**Manuscript effect:** None. These samples do not replace, revise, or overwrite accepted chapter prose.

## Calibration purpose

The samples below test whether House Style v2.0 can increase forward movement and emotional pressure without weakening the manuscript’s canon, procedural realism, evidence limits, POV discipline, or physical consequences.

Each sample is deliberately narrower than its source scene. It demonstrates a revision direction rather than proposing a final replacement.

# Calibration 1 — Quiet Character Suspense

## Source

**Accepted chapter:** Chapter 1 — *The Official Version*  
**POV:** Julie  
**Scene:** Marcus arrives at Julie’s farm and asks her to examine the new Argus pattern.

## Minimum source quote

> “You already used six years.”

> “I came because it might be wrong in the same way.”

## Dramatic objective

Marcus must persuade Julie to look at the telemetry before she sends him away. Julie must decide whether the new pattern is important enough to reenter the system that destroyed the truth about the Pakistan strike.

## What must remain

- Close third-person Julie POV.
- The farm as Julie’s chosen world of visible cause and effect.
- Marcus arrives alone in a black Suburban.
- Julie meets him with a shotgun available but not aimed at him.
- Six years of estrangement and his defensible testimony remain active between them.
- Marcus brings sanitized telemetry on paper.
- The sequence is exactly 11.2 seconds with almost no signal variation.
- The source is Pakistan and weather should have degraded it.
- Price’s raw-access request led to suspended credentials.
- The assessment is headed toward Indian Northern Command.
- Julie does not trust Argus confidence as proof.
- Julie’s decision is based on the pattern and the institutional pressure, not forgiveness.

## What slows the accepted passage

- The farm and property inventory extends beyond what the immediate confrontation needs.
- The repeated phone-call setup delays Marcus’s arrival.
- Several exchanges reiterate that Marcus expects Julie to help before the telemetry appears.
- Some emotional history is explained after the dialogue has already made the betrayal clear.
- The scene can move more quickly from physical safety to the 11.2-second pattern.

## Revised calibration sample

The fence post had failed below the soil line.

Julie had cut away the rot, set the replacement, and packed the Virginia clay until the carpenter’s level held center. Cause. Effect. Evidence. The mare had leaned for better grass. The cedar had given way. Nothing in the failure needed a confidence score.

An engine moved through the trees beyond the gravel drive.

Julie left the level on the top rail.

The black Suburban appeared at the mailbox and continued toward the house at a speed careful enough to be deliberate. No government plates. Clean bodywork. Tinted rear glass.

She crossed to the tack room and took the twelve-gauge from its rack.

The vehicle stopped beside her truck.

Marcus Reed stepped out alone.

Civilian clothes did not hide twenty-seven years in uniform. He looked first at the barn, the porch, and the tree line, then at Julie and the shotgun resting against her leg.

“You’ve got nerve,” she said.

“I tried calling.”

“I noticed.”

“Are you planning to shoot me?”

“Not if you leave.”

He absorbed the answer without the old instinct to improve it.

“I need ten minutes.”

“You already used six years.”

The wind moved dry leaves along the fence. Marcus looked older than he had outside Fort Belvoir, but age did not make the sentence he had given the panel less exact.

There was confusion in the room.

A sentence built to survive review.

“I know,” he said.

Julie had expected justification. Agreement unsettled her more.

“Say what you came to say.”

Marcus reached into his jacket slowly and removed a folded sheet of paper.

“No phone,” he said. “No tablet. Sanitized telemetry.”

“You brought classified material onto my property?”

“No sources. No collection methods.”

“That was not the question.”

“No.”

He stopped at the bottom porch step and held out the page. He did not try to close the distance.

Julie leaned the shotgun against the column and took the paper.

Timestamps. Signal strength. No headings or location.

The first interval was 11.2 seconds.

So was the second.

And the third.

By the fourth, she no longer heard the mare working the bit of grass beside the broken post.

The signal varied by less than a tenth of a decibel.

“Where?”

“Pakistan.”

“Which relay?”

“I can’t tell you here.”

She offered the page back.

Marcus did not take it.

“The relay was operating through weather severe enough to knock two reconnaissance platforms offline.”

Julie looked again at the numbers.

No drift. No missed packet. No physical world.

“Calibration?”

“Passed.”

“Replay?”

“Argus says no.”

“I didn’t ask what Argus says.”

“We don’t know.”

That answer sounded different in his mouth. Six years ago he would have wrapped it in probability, sample size, and command necessity.

“Who is we?”

“A three-person review cell inside Army Intelligence.”

“Three?”

“Two now.”

Julie looked up.

“Leland Price requested the raw feed,” Marcus said. “His credentials were suspended pending a counterintelligence review.”

The paper remained between them.

Not proof. Pressure.

“What did Argus call it?”

“State-sponsored preparation for a cross-border strike. Ninety-nine-point-eight percent.”

“Confidence is not evidence.”

“I know.”

Too fast. Too clean.

“What does the warning do?”

“Final certification enters the bilateral channel as a validated United States assessment. Indian Northern Command decides what follows.”

“When?”

“Sixteen-thirty Eastern.”

Julie watched the exact intervals march down the page. The weather should have bent them. Equipment should have marked them. Distance should have made them imperfect.

Six years earlier, a training signal had worn live encryption and eleven people had died beneath the official version.

“What do you want?”

“Come to Reston. Look at the unparsed feed. Tell me whether it exists in the physical world.”

“You have analysts.”

“I have analysts working inside an environment managed by Apex.”

There it was without needing the phrase. The company that built the system controlled the room in which the system would be challenged.

“Raw telemetry,” Julie said. “I choose the comparisons. No product manager explaining the output. If I find manipulation, we preserve the hashes through a government-controlled channel.”

“I can support that.”

“Can you guarantee the channel?”

Marcus looked at her.

“No.”

The honesty irritated her because it left no easy reason to refuse.

Julie handed him the paper. She could still see the numbers after he folded it.

11.2.

11.2.

11.2.

“I need to put the horses in and call the neighbor,” she said.

Marcus’s shoulders lowered.

“That sounds like yes.”

“It sounds like you have forty minutes to get the access conditions in writing.”

She picked up the shotgun and carried it into the house.

Forgiveness had nothing to do with it.

The pattern was too clean.

## Comparison

### Forward momentum

Improved. The sample reaches Marcus, the paper, the institutional pressure, and Julie’s decision with fewer neutral setup beats.

### Clarity

Improved without simplifying the stakes. The 11.2-second anomaly, Price’s suspension, Apex control, and the Indian certification path remain distinct.

### Emotional pressure

Improved. The six-year rupture is carried through dialogue, Marcus’s changed answers, and Julie’s resistance rather than a long retrospective explanation.

### Technical credibility

Preserved. The signal interval, lack of environmental variation, sanitized telemetry, raw-feed requirement, and confidence/evidence distinction remain intact.

### Character voice

Preserved. Julie reasons from physical causality and refuses both emotional and evidentiary shortcuts. Marcus remains careful but more honest than he was six years earlier.

### Originality

Preserved. The sample derives its rhythm and imagery from Julie’s established fence/evidence motif and the manuscript’s own institutional conflict.

### Continuity safety

Safe. No facts, knowledge, decisions, times, relationships, or evidence states are changed.

# Calibration 2 — Dense Technical Evidence

## Source

**Accepted chapter:** Chapter 10 — *The Capital Connection*  
**POV:** Julie  
**Scene:** At the decommissioned cutout, Julie, Marcus, and Elias compare the aluminum case, Elias’s administrator-token board, and the enclave recovery cartridge.

## Minimum source quote

> “Say exactly what it proves.”

> “APX-DIR-0019 was invoked through an executive hardware authority registered to Arthur Vance.”

## Dramatic objective

The trio must determine whether three vulnerable evidence objects can form a chain strong enough to survive hostile explanation. The immediate discovery is that Vance’s registered executive authority controlled or authorized the Payload 88 bridge, while his personal keystrokes and physical possession remain unproved. The same chain then reveals Northbridge as an operational endpoint.

## What must remain

- Close third-person Julie POV.
- Marcus remains physically impaired and holds no normal tactical role.
- Julie’s right wrist is braced and has little useful grip.
- Elias retains custody of the administrator-token board.
- The case, board, and cartridge are examined separately before combination.
- The case contains 136 sealed files, 47 incomplete files, and a manifest of 311 excluded files.
- Hashes prove post-capture integrity only.
- The physical board did not sign the original deployment.
- Elias’s later emergency actions are live, voluntary, and authenticated.
- The recovery cartridge contains the protected historical certificate chain.
- APX-DIR-0019 is bound through B3-EXEC-01 to registered custodian Arthur R. Vance.
- The chain proves registered executive authority controlled or authorized the bridge.
- It does not prove Vance personally typed the command or held the device.
- No ordinary administrator delegation appears.
- Northbridge WSS-4 appears in an authenticated routing receipt.
- Northbridge has legitimate Apex work and public ties to Sterling’s network.
- Contact does not prove Sterling directed the operation.
- The message body is absent or encrypted.
- No new plot information may be added.

## What slows the accepted passage

- The full evidence inventory is narrated before the characters challenge its meaning.
- Several limitations are stated once in the paper log, once in narration, and again in dialogue.
- The certificate hierarchy is technically precise but can create more pressure if revealed through Marcus’s desire to accuse Vance and Julie’s refusal to overclaim.
- The Northbridge/Sterling inference is explained at length after the distinction is already understood.

## Revised calibration sample

Julie drew three columns on the first clean sheet in the bound log.

CASE.

BOARD.

CARTRIDGE.

Marcus watched from the observation-room doorway because his right leg had stopped pretending it would support him for long. Blood showed again beneath the tape above his ear. He held one arm against his ribs when he breathed.

Elias sat at the certificate reader with the wool blanket around his shoulders. The administrator-token board remained in his hands.

“Nothing combines until it survives alone,” Julie said.

She started with the aluminum case.

The write blocker read the integrity manifest without opening the source files. The imager compared the stored hashes.

136 of 136 matched.

Marcus looked at the green result. “Untouched.”

“Since the capture sealed,” Julie said.

“That qualification is going in the log?”

“It is the fact.”

She wrote:

POST-CAPTURE INTEGRITY VERIFIED.

ORIGINAL SOURCE TRUTH NOT ESTABLISHED.

The case still carried forty-seven incomplete files. The manifest still named three hundred eleven excluded files that were not present.

Nothing in the room could turn absence into evidence.

Elias moved the board toward the reader but did not release it.

“Custody stays with me.”

Julie recorded that before the copper contacts entered the slot.

The secure element returned its hardware identity, author certificate, and monotonic signing history. The production record claimed Elias’s token had authorized the original bridge at 02:14.

The hardware counter contained no matching event.

The later gate access was there.

So was the provenance reconciliation.

Each later act carried the live-biometric release bound to his right index finger.

Marcus leaned forward and stopped when the movement caught his ribs. “So the deployment signature was fake.”

“The workstation identity was replayed or mirrored,” Elias said. “The physical token did not sign at 02:14.”

“And the later acts?”

“Mine.”

His bandaged finger rested against the edge of the board.

Julie wrote:

ORIGINAL PHYSICAL TOKEN USE: NOT PRESENT.

LATER EMERGENCY USE: LIVE, VOLUNTARY, AUTHENTICATED.

The distinction protected him and exposed him at the same time. The record that contradicted Vance also preserved every unauthorized act Elias had chosen after the framing began.

Julie mounted the recovery cartridge last.

Its local hardware seal verified against the enclave trust root captured when the record closed. The old workstation could not ask a network whether any certificate had since been revoked. Julie put that limitation in the log before they opened the historical chain.

The case fragment ended at APX-DIR-0019.

The board supplied the signer serial.

The cartridge supplied the authority above both.

The chain populated one field at a time.

APEX EXECUTIVE AUTHORITY ROOT.

BUILDING THREE EXECUTIVE OPERATIONS CA.

B3-EXEC-01.

SERVICE BINDING: APX-DIR-0019.

REGISTERED CUSTODIAN: VANCE, ARTHUR R.

Marcus read the final line.

“Vance.”

Julie kept her eyes on the certificate serial. It matched the bridge fragment. The validity window covered the event. No engineering subcertificate appeared below the executive hardware authority.

“Say exactly what it proves,” she said.

Elias pulled the blanket tighter with his left hand.

“APX-DIR-0019 was invoked through an executive hardware authority registered to Arthur Vance. That authority controlled or authorized the bridge that moved the post-archive Payload Eighty-Eight object into production.”

“His command?”

“No.”

“His hand on the device?”

“No.”

“His keystrokes?”

“No.”

Marcus pushed away from the doorway. The bad leg made it one step before he caught the desk.

“He restricted Julie, erased the review environment, suspended me, framed Elias, and tried to recover the case. How much separation do you need?”

“Enough to survive someone else separating it for us,” Julie said.

She pointed to the empty delegation field.

“Could an ordinary administrator have invoked this chain?”

Elias shook his head. “No employee delegation. No engineering subcertificate. No generic automation authority.”

“Then write that,” Marcus said.

Julie did.

VANCE-REGISTERED EXECUTIVE AUTHORITY CONTROLLED OR AUTHORIZED THE BRIDGE.

ORDINARY EMPLOYEE ACTION EXCLUDED BY AVAILABLE CHAIN.

PERSONAL OPERATOR UNPROVED.

Marcus read the three lines and did not argue again. The accusation was strong enough without making it false.

A routing receipt remained attached beneath the bridge event. In the partial capture it had resolved only as vendor synchronization metadata. The restored certificate state opened the tenant subject.

NORTHBRIDGE STRATEGIC INITIATIVES.

WASHINGTON SECURE SUITE 4.

TRUST PURPOSE: STRATEGIC-RISK SYNCHRONIZATION.

APEX CONTRACT STATUS: ACTIVE.

Elias enlarged the receipt. “Hardware-authenticated participation.”

“Message?” Julie asked.

“Not here. Routing, certificate fingerprints, trust purpose, and time.”

Marcus lowered himself into the second chair.

“Northbridge sits inside Sterling’s network.”

“Publicly,” Julie said.

“His former national-security staff. His committee language. His donors.”

“Public ties are not command.”

“The endpoint is operational.”

“Yes.”

The two facts belonged in the same chain. They did not become the same fact.

Julie turned the paper over.

Under APEX she wrote what the evidence could carry: Vance’s registered executive authority, the prohibited synthetic object, the bridge, the sanitization, and the pursuit of the record.

Under NORTHBRIDGE she wrote less: an authenticated endpoint in a legitimate contractor relationship.

Under STERLING she wrote one word.

INFERENCE.

Marcus watched her underline it.

“Strong inference,” he said.

“Still inference.”

Elias scrolled farther into the receipt.

A scheduled synchronization entry appeared beneath the current trust fingerprint.

07:08 EDT.

TASK PROFILE: K17-PHASE-B.

The room changed around the time.

The case, board, and cartridge could prove how the poisoned record had entered production and how the correction had been made. They could not show who would answer the next hardware challenge or what the live exchange would contain.

Julie looked at the battery-backed clock.

The evidence had become stronger.

The missing fact had become urgent.

## Comparison

### Forward momentum

Improved. The sample turns the evidence inventory into a sequence of contested conclusions that culminates in the 07:08 operational opportunity.

### Clarity

Improved. The three-object structure remains explicit, but only the limitations needed for the next decision are repeated.

### Emotional pressure

Improved. Elias’s later signatures remain both protection and exposure. Marcus’s need to accuse Vance conflicts directly with Julie’s need for a statement that will survive review.

### Technical credibility

Preserved. Hash limits, monotonic signing history, live biometric, certificate hierarchy, delegation absence, registered custody, and routing-receipt limitations remain accurate.

### Character voice

Preserved. Julie insists on the strongest defensible claim. Marcus pushes toward institutional accountability. Elias speaks precisely because his own identity is inside the record.

### Originality

Preserved. The scene’s energy comes from the manuscript’s own evidence-as-danger engine, not borrowed stylistic signatures.

### Continuity safety

Safe. No evidence is strengthened. Vance’s personal action, Sterling’s command, message content, and the meaning of Phase B remain unproved.

# Calibration 3 — Physical Suspense

## Source

**Accepted chapter:** Chapter 6 — *The Descent*  
**POV:** Julie  
**Scene:** The trio clears hydraulic shutter L3-7, chooses the cooling-gallery route, and crosses the retracting bridge while containment closes.

## Minimum source quote

> “You trust him now?”

> “No. I trust the building.”

## Dramatic objective

The trio must get beneath L3-7 and reach the production enclave through the only route central command cannot easily seal. The action must establish the first accepted injuries to Julie’s wrist and Marcus’s boot, then create Marcus’s rib injury and Elias’s hip injury at the retracting bridge.

## What must remain

- Exact opening context at approximately 04:52 EDT.
- L3-7 is already descending with red emergency strobes and warning horn active.
- Elias is immediately in front of Julie.
- Marcus is last.
- Marcus has thrown the sealed aluminum case beneath the shutter.
- Marcus still carries and separately throws the canvas tool bag.
- Julie pulls Marcus through and first injures her right wrist during that act.
- The shutter catches and damages Marcus’s boot.
- The lower tier is isolated; the trio is not outside Apex.
- The case remains sealed and intact.
- Closed-loop production continues toward the 05:00 dependent commit.
- The marked production route is more controllable by Chen.
- Chilled-water flow and heat load reveal the cooling-gallery route.
- The retracting bridge is wet and mechanically withdrawing.
- Elias falls and first injures his left hip there.
- Marcus is struck across the ribs by the rail while helping him.
- Containment remains competent and begins using the maintenance extension.
- No firearm or heroic invulnerability appears.
- No new route, injury, tool, or system capability is added.

## What slows the accepted passage

- Several early explanations of quarantine and system visibility can be shortened after the essential closed-loop facts are clear.
- Route comparison can reach the physical chilled-water evidence sooner.
- Some system description can move into the characters’ immediate choices.
- The bridge mechanics are strong; the revision should protect rather than compress the moment of injury and opponent adaptation.

## Revised calibration sample

The shutter dropped hard enough to shake dust from the ceiling.

“Move.”

Julie drove Elias toward the narrowing gap.

The sealed aluminum case was already on the far side, where Marcus had thrown it. Its green status display flashed beneath the red emergency strobes.

Elias hit the floor too late. His palms skidded through condensation.

Julie caught his shirt between the shoulders and shoved.

“Flat.”

He folded beneath the steel.

Julie went after him with one forearm over her head. The lower rubber seal passed close enough to move her hair.

She cleared and turned.

Marcus remained outside.

The recovery door behind him was opening into white light. Dark equipment moved through the gap.

The shutter had dropped below his waist.

“Leave the bag.”

Marcus threw the canvas tool bag first. It struck beside Julie and opened, scattering the flashlight, tape, and pry bar.

He dove.

The heel of one boot caught beneath the descending seal.

Julie seized his right wrist with both hands and pulled.

His shoulder came through. The boot did not.

The shutter dragged him backward.

Julie planted both heels against the wet floor and pulled harder. Something shifted at the base of her right thumb. Pain ran through the wrist and opened her grip.

She changed position, locked his forearm against hers, and used her body instead of her hand.

Marcus kicked.

The heel tore free.

They fell together as the shutter struck concrete.

Locking dogs drove into the floor channel one after another. The final impact traveled through Julie’s back.

The warning horn stopped.

Marcus rolled off her legs and tested his foot. The boot moved, but its heel had split open far enough to show the pale lining.

“You good?” he asked.

Julie flexed her right hand.

Pain answered before strength did.

“Elias?”

He sat against the wall beside the case, glasses fogged, staring at the sealed shutter.

“It cannot open from this side,” he said. “Hydraulic plant is behind us.”

A red panel beside the steel displayed:

PHYSICAL CONTAINMENT ACTIVE.

LOWER TIER ISOLATED.

04:52:19.

Julie stood with the case in her left hand.

The primary lights were gone. Red strobes divided the concrete corridor into moving sections. Chilled-water pipes crowded the ceiling. Condensation struck the floor in slow drops.

They had not escaped Building Three.

They had gone beneath it.

Marcus gathered the tool bag. “External link?”

“Quarantine cut corporate, classified transport, and ordinary voice,” Elias said. Fear made the words quick, not uncertain. “Production stays closed-loop from the last trusted state.”

“The poisoned state,” Julie said.

“Yes.”

Marcus checked his watch. “Five o’clock?”

“The dependent counter-battery support product commits.”

The case could prove what had happened later. It could not force the local system to doubt itself before the commit.

“Recovery console,” Julie said.

“Inside the enclave.”

“Take us there.”

Elias looked toward the marked corridor ahead.

PRODUCTION ACCESS.

Then he pointed left, where a narrower service passage descended beneath paired pipes.

“Cooling gallery.”

Marcus looked at the evacuation diagram. The passage ended in a blank block west of the enclave.

“Map says dead end.”

“Unclassified life-safety drawing,” Elias said. “The enclave expansion is not on it.”

“You expect us to trust that?”

Julie put her left hand on the largest pipe. Cold penetrated the wet insulation. The flow arrow pointed into the side passage. Above it, a newer return line ran back toward them.

CHW-ARGUS-R.

INDEPENDENT PROCESS LOOP.

Air moved through the passage toward machinery under load. Fresh wheel marks cut through the condensation near the drains.

The enclave could disappear from the drawing.

Its heat could not.

Marcus saw the tag.

“You trust him now?”

“No.” Julie shifted the case against her left leg. “I trust the building.”

They entered the cooling passage.

It dropped beneath the service level and opened onto a steel gantry two stories above the chiller plant.

Six compressor housings turned in the red light below. Mist hung over the machinery.

The bridge across the gap was retracting.

Its near edge had already withdrawn from the platform, leaving less than two feet of overlap. A drive chain pulled the span toward a recess on the far side.

“How long?” Marcus asked.

“Less than a minute,” Elias said.

Julie stepped onto the wet grating.

Her boot shifted. She caught the rail with her left hand and kept the injured wrist close to her body.

The bridge moved another inch.

She crossed and turned.

“Case.”

Marcus sent it sliding along the grating. Julie stopped it with her boot. The display remained green.

Elias put one foot onto the bridge.

White light touched the passage behind Marcus.

Then a second beam.

Low. Controlled. Advancing without hurry.

“Four, maybe six,” Marcus said. “They know the bridge is moving.”

Elias started across.

He held the rail too tightly and watched his feet instead of the far platform.

The chain clanked.

His shoe found clear condensate.

Both feet went out.

He struck the grating on his left hip. His leg passed through the open rail and hung above the machinery.

Julie started back.

Marcus was closer.

He stepped onto the last section of overlap and caught the back of Elias’s belt. The bridge withdrew beneath him.

For one second Marcus had one foot on concrete and one on a span moving away.

“Hands,” he said.

Elias reached.

Marcus shoved him forward.

Julie caught Elias’s forearm and pulled. His hip struck the rail again as the leg came free.

The bridge left the near platform.

Marcus jumped.

His chest hit the far rail. The impact folded him over it and stopped his breath.

Julie caught his coveralls with her left hand. Elias grabbed his arm. Together they dragged him onto the platform.

The bridge disappeared into the opposite wall with a steel crash.

Marcus remained on one knee, one hand pressed beneath his left arm. He tried a deeper breath and abandoned it halfway.

Across the gap, the first containment operator reached the empty platform.

No one attempted the jump.

A mechanical latch opened below them.

Elias looked toward the underside of the span.

“Maintenance extension.”

A ladder assembly began unfolding from beneath the opposite platform.

Marcus stood carefully. “How long?”

“Ninety seconds.”

Apex had not given them an incompetent opponent or a permanent obstacle.

It had given them ninety seconds.

Julie picked up the case with her left hand.

“Spend them.”

## Comparison

### Forward momentum

Improved. The sample establishes the shutter, injury, closed-loop deadline, route decision, bridge hazard, and opponent adaptation without pausing for a complete quarantine lecture.

### Clarity

Preserved. The reader always knows where the case, tool bag, trio, shutter, bridge, and containment team are.

### Emotional pressure

Improved. Julie’s wrist failure and Marcus’s rib impact immediately change what each can do. Elias’s fear is visible through mechanics rather than explained separately.

### Technical credibility

Preserved. The route is inferred through chilled-water supply/return and heat load. The bridge retracts for isolation. Containment uses a maintenance extension rather than behaving helplessly.

### Character voice

Preserved. Julie trusts physical evidence, Marcus remains operational under pressure, and Elias provides specific system knowledge without becoming omnipotent.

### Originality

Preserved. The scene’s identity comes from the novel’s own combination of infrastructure, evidence custody, injury, and disciplined opponents.

### Continuity safety

Safe. The L3-7 handoff, first injury locations, evidence state, tool state, route, bridge consequences, clock architecture, and opponent behavior remain unchanged.

# Calibration conclusion

House Style v2.0 can increase propulsion without reducing the novel’s intelligence.

The successful pattern across all three tests is:

1. Reach the active problem sooner.
2. Let a physical effect or human challenge create the need for explanation.
3. State the one evidence limitation that changes action.
4. Keep emotion inside choice and mechanics.
5. End the passage on a transformed objective, narrowing clock, or new cost.

These samples are approved as style references only. Any later manuscript revision must be performed chapter by chapter against the complete accepted chapter and current canon controls.
