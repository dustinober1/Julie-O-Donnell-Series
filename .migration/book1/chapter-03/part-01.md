# Chapter 3 - The Exit Protocol

The deadbolt began to retract.

Julie watched the transfer bar.

Eighty-five percent.

The motor inside the steel door turned slowly, drawing the locking cylinder back through the frame. Beyond it, someone issued a quiet command. Shoes repositioned in the corridor.

Marcus stood to the side of the door, his body angled away from the opening.

“Do we have enough?” he asked.

“No.”

“Can we leave with what we have?”

“That’s a different question.”

The remote sanitization process climbed to ninety-three percent. Files vanished from the temporary review environment as quickly as Julie’s hidden process could copy them.

She opened the evidence-capture manifest.

The system had transferred the critical packet samples first, followed by the validation index, the unexplained ninth checksum, and the software-dependency records. The full relay-health history remained incomplete. So did the original access log showing who had modified Payload 88 after it was archived.

Useful evidence.

Not decisive evidence.

The kind institutions buried beneath requests for more context.

The deadbolt completed its withdrawal.

A second lock released above it.

Julie canceled the remaining bulk transfer and entered a final command.

SEAL PARTIAL CAPTURE.

A warning filled the screen.

INCOMPLETE ARCHIVE
FINALIZATION MAY EXCLUDE ACTIVE FILE BLOCKS
PROCEED?

“Yes,” Julie whispered.

She confirmed.

The terminal began writing a cryptographic manifest to the evidence drive. Until that process finished, removing the device could corrupt the directory and make every copied file vulnerable to challenge.

The door moved inward half an inch.

A security officer’s voice came through the gap.

“Colonel Reed, step away from the door.”

Marcus did not move.

The manifest advanced.

One of four blocks.

“Colonel, acknowledge.”

Marcus looked at Julie.

She held up one finger.

The door pressed against his shoulder.

He allowed it to open another inch, then planted his foot against the base.

“I acknowledge that you are attempting to enter a classified review room while government evidence is being preserved,” he said. “Identify yourself and state your authority.”

“Daniel Mercer, Apex Protective Services. We have an administrative order to secure the room.”

“Signed by whom?”

“Director Vance.”

“Director Vance is a contractor.”

“Colonel, move away from the door.”

Two of four blocks.

Julie could hear more people behind Mercer. At least three, judging from the movement and breathing in the corridor.

No weapons being drawn.

No safeties clicking.

That was almost worse.

A gun would have clarified the situation. Procedure allowed everyone to pretend nothing unlawful was happening.

Sarah Chen spoke from outside.

“Marcus, stop making this harder than it needs to be.”

Marcus’s expression changed at the use of his first name.

“You suspended my authority after I submitted a defect notice.”

“Your authority was suspended because you exceeded the approved review scope.”

“You locked two people inside a sealed room.”

“The room was placed under a temporary security hold.”

“You shut off ventilation.”

“The environmental interruption lasted less than two minutes.”

“Long enough for you to destroy the review partition.”

Sarah did not answer.

Three of four blocks.

The door pushed harder against Marcus.

He looked back at Julie.

She shook her head.

Not yet.

“Colonel,” Mercer said, “if you continue obstructing the door, we will document your conduct as resistance to a lawful security directive.”

“Then document this too. The evidence device in this room is government property under my custody. Any seizure requires a federal chain-of-custody receipt and identification of the receiving authority.”

Sarah’s voice remained calm.

“No one is seizing anything. We are escorting you to a compliance interview while the classification status of the copied material is reviewed.”

“By Apex.”

“By the facility security authority.”

“Name the government official overseeing the review.”

Silence.

The final manifest block began writing.

The screen flickered.

REMOTE SANITIZATION: COMPLETE.

Every directory in the visible partition disappeared.

Only the finalization window remained.

The door opened another inch.

Julie reached for the cable.

“Wait,” Marcus said.

“Working on it.”

The terminal displayed:

VERIFYING CAPTURE SIGNATURE.

A gloved hand appeared through the gap and gripped the edge of the door.

Marcus drove his shoulder against it.

The hand vanished.

Mercer swore.

“Last warning, Colonel.”

Julie watched the gray verification wheel rotate.

Once.

Twice.

The terminal chimed.

PARTIAL CAPTURE SEALED
INTEGRITY MANIFEST COMPLETE

She pulled the cable.

The screen went black.

“Done.”

Marcus stepped away from the door.

It opened immediately.

Two Apex security officers entered first. They wore dark uniforms and lightweight body armor marked PROTECTIVE SERVICES. Their sidearms remained holstered. One carried a compact electroshock weapon low beside his thigh.

Sarah stood behind them with her tablet held against her chest.

Her hair was still perfectly arranged. Only the color high in her cheeks suggested that anything unusual had occurred.

A third officer remained in the corridor.

Mercer was in his forties, broad through the shoulders, with the contained irritation of a man accustomed to being obeyed before he had to raise his voice.

“Hands where I can see them,” he said.

Marcus slowly lifted his hands.

Julie left hers on the table.

Mercer’s eyes moved to the aluminum evidence case.

“Step away from the device.”

“That device is in my custody,” Marcus said.

“Your access authority has been suspended.”

“My commission has not.”

“This facility is operating under an active data-loss response.”

“Then call the Defense Criminal Investigative Service and have them take custody.”

Sarah entered the room.

“That won’t be necessary.”

Marcus looked at her.

“Why not?”

“Because no crime has been established.”

“You locked us in a room and destroyed evidence.”

“We quarantined a compromised analytic environment after an unauthorized export attempt.”

“The export used an approved government device.”

“The approval was issued in error.”

“Before or after Julie found Payload Eighty-Eight?”

For the first time, Sarah’s control slipped.

Her eyes shifted toward Julie.

Only for a fraction of a second.

It was enough.

“You knew the package number,” Julie said.

Sarah recovered.

“I have access to the query record.”

“You denied access to the archive before I searched the index.”

“The validation archive is proprietary.”

“That wasn’t my question.”

Mercer stepped between them.

“This discussion is over. You will both accompany us to the compliance suite.”

“Are we under arrest?” Julie asked.

“No.”

“Are we free to leave?”

“You are required to remain on-site while the security incident is assessed.”

“That sounds like detention.”

“It is an administrative hold.”

“Can we refuse?”

Mercer’s jaw tightened.

“You can cooperate, or we can refer the matter to federal law enforcement as a suspected classified-data theft.”

Marcus lowered his hands.

Mercer’s grip moved toward the electroshock weapon.

Marcus stopped.

Sarah pointed toward the evidence case.

“Leave that here.”

“No,” Marcus said.

“It contains proprietary material.”

“It contains potential evidence of intelligence manipulation.”

“Your authority to make that determination has been suspended.”

“By the subject of the determination.”

Sarah’s gaze hardened.

“Marcus, you are standing inside a secure contractor facility after assisting an unauthorized consultant in penetrating protected development records. You can continue escalating this, or you can come upstairs, make a statement, and allow the appropriate offices to resolve it.”

Julie looked at the camera in the corner.

The indicator light was dark.

It had been green when they entered.

“Why did you turn off the room recording?” she asked.

Sarah looked up.

“The sector quarantine interrupted local systems.”

“The terminal stayed on backup power.”

“The recording server did not.”

“Convenient.”

Mercer reached for the aluminum case.

Marcus took hold of its handle first.

The two men stared at each other across it.

“Colonel,” Mercer said quietly, “do not make me remove that from you.”

Marcus’s voice lowered.

“Put your name on a custody receipt.”

“That is not the procedure.”

“Then you are not touching it.”

Mercer looked toward Sarah.

She considered the situation.

If her officers seized the drive without documentation, Marcus could later describe the act as destruction of government evidence. If she allowed him to retain it, Julie might carry proof out of the facility.

Sarah chose a third option.

“Escort them to Conference Room Four,” she said. “Colonel Reed may retain the case until counsel arrives.”

“Whose counsel?” Marcus asked.

“Apex general counsel has already been notified.”

“I want Army counsel.”

“You may request whoever you like from the conference room.”

“Through your phones.”

“Through the facility’s secure communications system.”

Marcus gave Julie a brief look.

A room controlled by Apex.

A telephone controlled by Apex.

Counsel selected after Apex had time to define what had occurred.

The official version was already being written.

Julie rose from the chair.

“Lead the way.”

The corridor outside Room 402B was lit only by emergency fixtures spaced along the ceiling. The ventilation had restarted, pushing cold air through the acoustic panels with a low hiss.

Mercer walked ahead.

Sarah followed behind him, close enough to monitor Julie and Marcus without appearing to be part of the escort. The other two security officers remained on either side of them.

Marcus carried the aluminum case.

Julie counted doors.

Four offices.

A secure records room.

A service corridor.

The elevator lobby at the far end.

No employees.

The entire wing had been cleared before security opened Room 402B.

“You evacuated the floor,” she said.

Sarah glanced at her.

“We reduced unnecessary personnel movement during the incident.”

“You didn’t want witnesses.”

“We didn’t want additional exposure to classified material.”

“Inside their own offices?”

“You have an answer for everything, don’t you?”

“No. That’s your department.”

Mercer stopped at the elevator call panel.

He pressed his badge against the reader.

Nothing happened.

He tried again.

The panel remained dark.

One of the other officers checked his radio.

“Control, elevators are still isolated in Sector Four.”

Static answered.

Then a voice:

“Use Stairwell C. Hold subjects at internal muster until vertical transport restores.”

Subjects.

Marcus heard it too.

“We’re not going to a conference room,” he said.

Sarah did not look at him.

“The internal muster area has secure communications.”

“Where is it?”

“Second floor.”

“Conference Room Four is on six.”

Sarah’s mouth tightened.

“The location changed.”

“Before or after counsel was supposedly notified?”

Mercer turned.

“Keep moving.”

They entered the stairwell.

Bare concrete replaced the carpeted corridor. Red emergency lights glowed above each landing. The sound of their footsteps traveled down through the building.

Julie looked at the evacuation map mounted beside the door.

They were on the fourth floor.

The visitor skybridge connected to the parking structure from the third.

The internal muster area was marked on the second, behind another controlled security boundary.

They descended.

At the next landing, Julie paused beside the manual fire-alarm station.

Mercer noticed.

“Keep moving.”

She looked at the panel.

A green status light indicated that the independent life-safety system remained active.

A second light displayed:

SECTOR 4 AIR-HANDLING FAULT
ACKNOWLEDGMENT PENDING

“You shut off ventilation in an occupied secure room,” Julie said.

Sarah continued downward.

“It has been restored.”

“The system still shows a fault.”

“Facilities will clear it.”

“You locked the room while the air system was offline.”

“For less than two minutes.”

Marcus understood before Sarah did.

“Julie.”

She pulled the alarm.

A siren erupted through the stairwell.

The noise was immediate and physical, a rising electronic howl punctuated by white strobes. A recorded voice followed:

FIRE EMERGENCY REPORTED. EVACUATE USING THE NEAREST STAIRWELL. DO NOT USE ELEVATORS.

Mercer seized Julie’s wrist.

She twisted, but his grip held.

“What the hell are you doing?”

“Reporting a life-safety failure.”

“This is a false alarm.”

“The panel says otherwise.”

Sarah reached for the alarm reset key on her lanyard.

Marcus stepped between her and the panel.

“Don’t.”

“Move.”

“You kill the alarm before county dispatch clears it, and I add interference with an emergency system to the report.”

Sarah stared at him.

“You have no report. Your credentials are suspended.”

“The fire department won’t care about my credentials. They’ll care why your secure floor lost ventilation while occupied.”

Doors opened on the landings above and below them.

Employees entered the stairwell in uneven groups, some carrying emergency bags, others looking annoyed or confused. The empty vertical shaft filled with voices and movement.

Mercer released Julie’s wrist.

“Stay together,” he ordered his officers. “We’re taking them to the ground-floor assembly point.”

Sarah leaned close to Marcus.

“You think an alarm makes you safe?”

“No,” he said. “I think witnesses make you careful.”

The crowd pressed downward.

Julie and Marcus moved with it.

The security officers tried to maintain a box around them, but the stairwell was too narrow. Employees inserted themselves between bodies. A woman with an emergency oxygen kit stopped on the landing. Two engineers argued about whether they had shut down a test rack.

The orderly containment dissolved into evacuation.

At the third-floor landing, Julie saw the sign:

PARKING SKYBRIDGE
EMERGENCY EGRESS

She reached for the crash bar.

Mercer caught her shoulder.

“Ground floor.”

“My vehicle is on three.”

“Ground floor assembly.”

The electroshock weapon was now in his other hand.

Held low.

Ready.

Julie looked at Marcus.

Mercer followed her gaze.

“Don’t,” he warned.

Sarah came down the stairs behind them.

“The parking bridge is not an authorized assembly route.”

The recorded voice repeated its evacuation instruction.

Julie pressed the crash bar.

The door moved half an inch before Mercer pulled her back.

Marcus set down the aluminum case.

Mercer’s attention shifted toward it.

That was the mistake.

Marcus drove his forearm between Mercer’s wrist and the weapon, turning the muzzle toward the concrete wall. The electroshock cartridge discharged with a flat crack. Two barbed probes struck the emergency map and snapped against the plastic cover.

The stairwell erupted in shouting.

Marcus twisted Mercer’s arm until the weapon fell. He did not strike him. He shoved the officer backward into the handrail and grabbed the evidence case.

“Go.”

Julie hit the crash bar.

The door released.

She ran into the third-floor corridor.

Marcus followed.

Behind them, Sarah shouted for security to contain the parking bridge.

Not stop the thieves.

Not detain the suspects.

Contain the bridge.

Language selected for whoever might later hear the recording.

Julie sprinted toward the glass doors at the end of the corridor. White emergency strobes reflected through the walls, multiplying her image across every panel.

The biometric turnstiles leading to the skybridge had retracted into emergency-egress position. Employees moved through them toward the parking deck.

Julie joined the flow.

Marcus stayed behind her, one hand clamped around the evidence case.

Sarah and the officers emerged from the stairwell.

“Colonel Reed!” Sarah called. “Stop now!”

Marcus did not turn.

The glass doors opened onto the skybridge.

Warm Virginia air rushed in through the evacuation vents. Below the bridge, the campus entrance had already begun to fill with flashing lights. A county fire engine turned through the outer security lane as retractable bollards sank into the pavement.

The alarm had reached someone Apex did not employ.

Julie crossed the bridge.

The parking structure was louder than the building. Car alarms chirped. Engines started. Employees gathered near concrete pillars, trying to identify their assigned assembly zones.

Julie cut away from the crowd toward the third-floor visitor section.

Her Ford sat where she had left it.

Marcus looked over his shoulder.

Sarah had stopped at the end of the bridge. She held her tablet to her mouth, speaking rapidly. Two security officers pushed through the evacuees.

“Keys?” Marcus asked.

Julie pulled the mechanical key from her pocket.

“They kept the electronic fob.”

“Can you start it?”

“It’s a truck, not a space program.”

She unlocked the driver’s door manually.

Marcus climbed into the passenger side and pulled the evidence case onto his lap.

Julie inserted the key.

The engine turned once.

Failed.

The security officers were twenty yards away.

She tried again.

The V8 caught with a rough growl.

Marcus looked at the approaching officers.

“Any chance this thing has a quiet setting?”

“No.”

Julie shifted into reverse.

The truck shot backward out of the space.

A security officer slapped the rear quarter panel and jumped aside. The second moved in front of the hood, one hand raised.

Julie braked.

He stood directly in their path, breathing hard, his other hand resting on his holstered pistol.

“Turn off the engine!” he shouted through the windshield.

Marcus lowered his window.

“Move away from the vehicle.”

“Colonel, you are not authorized to leave the property.”

“Then call federal law enforcement.”

“They are being notified.”

“Good. They can meet us somewhere your company doesn’t own.”

The officer drew his electroshock weapon.
