The anti-vehicle bollards were not.

“Julie.”

Julie turned left.

Marcus twisted in his seat.

“Still there.”

The truck’s engine vibrated through the steering wheel. Office buildings and landscaped medians passed on either side. Lunchtime traffic crowded the intersection ahead.

Forward Post Arjun

Kashmir

22:18 Indian Standard Time

Major Ananya Sharma read the American assessment twice.

The first time, she read it as an officer.

The second time, she read it as someone who had spent eleven winters watching machines misunderstand mountains.

Outside the reinforced observation post, wind drove ice against the narrow armored windows. The valley below had disappeared behind cloud and darkness. On clear nights, Sharma could identify villages by the pattern of their lights. Tonight, even the closest ridge was invisible.

Lieutenant Sameer Qureshi stood beside the communications console.

“Argus confidence is ninety-nine point eight percent,” he said.

“I can see the number.”

“Yes, ma’am.”

“Where is the corroboration?”

Qureshi opened the supporting-data page.

“Satellite collection is degraded by weather. The forward acoustic arrays report intermittent heavy-engine signatures in Sector Zebra-Nine. Two communications relays detected encrypted command traffic moving south.”

“Ground observation?”

“Negative.”

“Drone?”

“Grounded for icing.”

“Signals-intelligence confirmation from our own stations?”

“Pattern analysis is inconclusive.”

Sharma looked at the tactical map.

Red symbols marked a predicted Pakistani artillery movement beyond the Line of Control. The formation was too orderly. Batteries, command vehicles, and ammunition transports appeared to be moving into firing positions under cover of the storm.

If accurate, the deployment threatened three Indian forward posts and the supply route behind them.

If inaccurate, firing first could ignite the entire sector.

“When does the United States certify the assessment?” she asked.

“Zero-two-hundred local.”

Four hours away.

“And the recommended action window?”

“Preemptive counter-battery strike before zero-five-hundred.”

Qureshi hesitated.

“Northern Command has requested that we prepare firing solutions now.”

“Requested or ordered?”

“Requested, ma’am.”

Sharma continued reading.

The Argus assessment described the predicted attack as effectively certain.

Not likely.

Not probable.

Certain.

She had learned to distrust that word long before algorithms began using it.

“Bring the batteries to readiness,” she said.

Qureshi reached for the radio.

“No ammunition at the guns yet. No targeting data loaded into the fire-control system. We prepare, and we wait for corroboration.”

Qureshi paused.

“If the armor is real, waiting could cost us the ridge.”

“If it is real, four hours will give us more than one source.”

“And if the American certification arrives unchanged?”

Sharma looked through the armored window at the storm.

“At zero-two-hundred, we evaluate what they send.”

“Northern Command may expect immediate execution.”

“Northern Command is not standing in this bunker.”

Qureshi allowed himself the faintest smile.

Then the console chimed.

A new line appeared beneath the Argus assessment:

SOURCE REVIEW IN PROGRESS
FINAL CONFIDENCE CERTIFICATION PENDING

The line remained for less than a second.

Then it vanished.

The display returned to:

CONFIDENCE: 99.8%
SOURCE INTEGRITY: VERIFIED

Sharma leaned toward the screen.

“Did you see that?”

“Yes, ma’am.”

“What changed?”

Qureshi opened the system history.

“No revision is listed.”

“Then someone revised it without leaving a revision.”

She looked again at the red symbols moving across the mountain map.

Thousands of miles away, a system had declared the enemy’s intentions with mathematical certainty.

Here, in the actual mountains, the storm revealed nothing.

“Save a local image of that screen,” Sharma said.

“The packet is access-controlled.”

“Use a camera.”

Qureshi reached for his phone.

Sharma watched the countdown beside the assessment.

03:41:22 UNTIL FINAL CERTIFICATION.

“Major?” Qureshi asked.

She kept her eyes on the clock.

“At zero-five-hundred, if the threat is confirmed, we fire.”

“And if it isn’t?”

“Then we find out why a machine was so certain about something no human being could see.”
