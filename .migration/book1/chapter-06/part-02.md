“Usually.” Elias pointed to the final panel. “The timing controller is local. If the loop pressure changes, it shortens the interval.”

From behind them came the first impact of the maintenance ladder against the far platform.

Marcus looked through the grated door. White lights moved below the gap. “They’re almost over.”

Julie waited for the second vent to fire.

The blast filled the gallery. As it died, she ran to the first recess, set down the telemetry case, and pulled Elias in beside her. Marcus followed with the tool bag.

The third warning lamp came on.

“Now,” Elias said.

“No.” Julie pointed to the pressure gauge above the vent. The needle was still rising. “Not yet.”

The purge fired two seconds early.

Vapor struck the empty bay where they would have been. Elias stared at the gauge.

Julie waited for the needle to fall, then moved them forward.

They crossed one bay at a time, guided by pressure rather than the warning lights. At the second recess, a radio clicked behind them. At the third, boots struck the grating of the recovered bridge.

The containment team had crossed.

The final vent stood between them and a round steel hatch marked PRIMARY LOOP ACCESS. No lamp illuminated above it. Frost coated the frame.

Elias reached for the wheel.

“Wait,” Julie said.

A thin whistle rose inside the pipework.

Marcus heard it too. He pulled Elias back.

The unmarked vent erupted.

Cold vapor swallowed the hatch. The blast caught the edge of the tool bag and tore it from Marcus’s hand. It struck the wall, spilling the flashlight and pry bar into the fog. The flashlight spun across the floor and vanished through a drain channel.

When the air cleared, the pry bar lay beneath the vent, rimed white.

Marcus started toward it.

“Leave it,” Julie said.

“It’s all we have.”

“Not if your hand freezes to it.”

A beam of white light cut into the first bay behind them.

Marcus abandoned the tool and spun the hatch wheel. It resisted, then broke loose with a shriek. Elias added both hands. The wheel turned faster.

Julie watched the rear.

A helmet appeared through the vapor, then a shoulder and the dark outline of a compact rifle. The operator did not advance blindly. He waited for the purge, marked its interval, and signaled someone behind him.

“They’re adapting,” Julie said.

Marcus pulled the hatch open. “So are we.”

Elias went through first, taking the telemetry case from Julie. She followed. Marcus backed into the opening and dragged the hatch shut as a weapon light reached the final recess.

He spun the wheel from inside.

A locking bar dropped into place.

For three seconds, the hatch held.

Then metal struck the other side in a measured pattern.

Once. Pause. Twice.

Elias’s face lost what little color remained. “Override sequence. They have a mechanical key.”

“How long?” Marcus asked.

“Maybe a minute.”

Marcus listened to the impacts. “Forty seconds.”

Julie took the case from Elias. “Where’s the gate?”

He pointed down.

A fixed ladder descended through a vertical shaft beside the enclave’s return-water pipe. Red emergency light faded after the first twenty feet. Below it, a line of white floor illumination traced the base of the shaft.

“The production approach is under us,” Elias said. “Last level.”

Julie went first.

The ladder rungs were wet and brutally cold. She hooked the telemetry case through one arm and descended with her body close to the rail. The pipe beside her carried the steady vibration she had followed from the junction. The enclave was drawing heat through it, alive and working while the surrounding building locked itself down.

Halfway down, the hatch wheel turned above them.

Marcus came last. He looked up through the rungs. “They’re in.”

No one told Elias to move faster. The boots descending above did that.

Julie reached the bottom at 04:54.

The lowest corridor was quieter than the levels above. Concrete gave way to smooth armored wall panels without seams or exposed fasteners. The red emergency strobes ended at the threshold. Beyond it, narrow white lights glowed along the floor, powered from a separate circuit.

The Argus production enclave stood at the far end.

Its gate bore no Apex chevron, no government seal, no warning placard crowded with classification language. Two slabs of dark steel met at the center inside a frame sunk deep into the surrounding structure. Four hinge housings disappeared behind armored covers. Separate power indicators showed green for the lock, amber for quarantine, and white for local control.

A single access panel occupied the wall to the right.

No network jack.

No ordinary badge reader.

No visible connection to the rest of Building Three.

Elias stopped ten feet from it.

Julie continued until the panel lit in response to her movement.

LOCAL PRODUCTION ENCLAVE
 QUARANTINE ACCESS ONLY
 ADMINISTRATOR AUTHENTICATION REQUIRED

The time appeared beneath the message.

04:54:47

Thirteen seconds until the minute. Barely more than five until the release window.

Marcus set himself at the corridor bend behind them. He had no pry bar now, no firearm, and no cover beyond the armored frame. The first white weapon light swept across the bottom of the ladder shaft.

“Gate,” he said.

Elias did not approach it.

Julie looked from the panel to him. “You said local control survives quarantine.”

“It does.”

“You said you can open it.”

“I can.”

“Then what’s the problem?”

The panel’s white light reflected in his glasses. Somewhere above, a boot hit the ladder. Another followed at a disciplined interval.

Elias looked at the dark steel gate.

“The enclave doesn’t accept a normal employee credential. It uses my administrator key. The release is bound to me.”

Julie heard what he had not said.

Opening the gate would not be another ambiguous act hidden inside a damaged log. It would create a permanent record that Elias Thorne had entered the production enclave during a security quarantine while Vance’s containment team hunted him.

Whatever remained of his position inside Apex would end at this panel.

Whatever story Vance intended to tell about Payload Eighty-Eight would have Elias’s name attached to the act that challenged it.

A mechanical override engaged at the corridor bend.

Marcus turned his head slightly. “Decision time.”

Elias looked at Julie.

“I can open it,” he said. “But the moment I do, Vance will know it was me.”
